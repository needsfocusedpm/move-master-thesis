Mediation involves the intervention of a third party to help resolve conflicts and foster collaboration by addressing interpersonal dynamics, understanding differing perspectives, and guiding individuals or teams toward constructive solutions. It serves as an act of agency or action as an intermediary, functioning as a means of action or a medium of transmission to achieve resolution and understanding that conflicting parties may struggle to achieve on their own. (Oxford English Dictionary, 2025)

It is important to understand that with a project manager as a mediator, this “third party” does indeed have some kind of authority over the people involved, so in this case we are to some extent talking about a “managerial approach and procedure” of mediation taken (Moore, 2014, p. 9), with the project manager possibly exerting some power or the risk of becoming involved in the conflict.

3.5.1	The inner team

Introspection plays a crucial role in understanding and managing internal conflicts, as highlighted by Friedemann Schulz von Thun’s “Inner Team” model, which emphasizes self-reflection as a dialogue between the diverse voices within one’s mind. Its core elements are:

•	Plurality: The inner team consists of multiple voices or parts that interact, sometimes in harmony and sometimes in conflict, mirroring the dynamics of a real team.
•	There’s a Leader: The team leader, or “team captain,” is responsible for uniting the inner team, creating synergies, and transforming chaotic inner dialogues into productive collaboration. This is essential when there are inner conflicts.
•	Conflicts: Inner conflicts are inevitable and essential, but they must be acknowledged and resolved.
•	Team Development: Not all inner team members are equally visible, and sidelining or suppressing certain parts can harm inner harmony, making their integration vital for the “team development” of the inner team.
•	Adaptation: Depending on the context and situation, different members of the inner team step forward, requiring flexible adjustments to align with life’s varying demands.
•	Configuration: Ensuring the inner team’s alignment with situational needs involves understanding the context and achieving a balance that fosters congruence and effective self-management.
(Thun, 2023, pp. 21-22)

The way the members of the inner team communicate with each other can be analyzed using his “four sides of a message” model, explained in chapter 3.6.2. (Thun, 2023, p. 39)

3.5.2	Understanding

Understanding the dynamics of a conflict is supported by applying concepts such as the Conflict Model developed by Kenneth W. Thomas and Ralph B. Kilmann (“Thomas-Kilmann Conflict Mode Instrument – TKI”). It allows to analyze conflicts from two different perspectives:

•	The assertiveness of a party.
•	The cooperativeness of a party.

Depending on the degree there is a different typical conflict management style observed. (Kilmann, 2023, p. 5)

Low Assertiveness and low cooperativeness means Avoiding
Low Assertiveness and high cooperativeness means Accomodating
High Assertiveness and low cooperativeness means Competing
High Assertiveness and high cooperativeness means collaborating
A balance of all means compromising

Under high stress, assertiveness develops into attacking behavior, while cooperativeness into retreating behavior (Kilmann, 2023, p. 19)  – observations, that can be viewed as related to responding to trauma. 

The four Fs of trauma response are: Flight (mobilization), freeze (immobilization), fight (offensive) and fawn (defensive).

3.5.3	Conflict escalation

Conflicts, if remaining unmediated and consequentially unresolved follow a certain scheme identified by the researcher Friedrich Glasl.

HARDENING (1):
Positions harden and occasionally clash
Temporary missteps and tension arise
Awareness of existing tension creates strain
Belief: Tensions can still be resolved through dialogue
No rigid parties or factions have formed yet
 
DEBATE (2):
Polarization in thinking, feeling, and wanting; black-and-white thinking
Tactics: Quasi-rational, verbal aggression
Speaking to the audience, scoring points through third parties
Temporary subgroups form around positions
Discrepancy between “overtone and undertone”
"Superior versus inferior" dynamic(see Transactional Analysis)

ACTIONS (3):
“Talking no longer helps” – therefore: actions!
Strategy of creating faits accomplis
Discrepancy between verbal and non-verbal behavior, with non-verbal dominating
Danger: Misinterpretations 
“Pessimistic anticipation” lead to mistrust and acceleration
Formation of group boundaries, cohesion, and role crystallization
Empathy is lost

IMAGES & COALITIONS (4):
Stereotypes, clichés, image campaigns, and rumors focus on knowledge and abilities
Parties maneuver each other into negative roles and engage in conflict
Recruitment of supporters and formation of symbiotic coalitions
Perceptual fixation creates self-fulfilling prophecies.
Punitive behavior is framed to allow plausible deniability
Double binds arise through paradoxical instructions

LOSS OF FACE (5):
Public and direct: attacks on one’s face
Staged “unmasking actions” as a ritual
Unmasking (“Demasqué”): "disappointment" and retrospective revelations (“Aha moments”)
Angel-devil imagery and the concept of a doppelgänger
Exclusion, banishment
Isolation, echo chambers,  social autism
Disgust
Ideologies, values, and principles
Rehabilitation!

THREATS (6):
Threats and counter-threats
Credibility relies on proportionality and the execution of self-binding actions (e.g., setting tripwires)
“Second move”
Stress
Escalation accelerates through ultimatums and a “scissor effect” of narrowing options

DESTRUCTIVE BLOWS (7):
Thinking shifts to objectification, stripping away human qualities
Limited destructive blows are seen as a “fitting response”
Values are inverted: causing relatively smaller harm to oneself is perceived as a gain

FRAGMENTATION (8):
Paralysis and disintegration of the opposing system
Cutting off key figures from their support base
Destruction of vital system components, rendering the system uncontrollable and leading to its complete collapse

TOGETHER INTO THE ABYSS (9):
No way back
Total confrontation
Destruction pursued even at the cost of self-destruction, with a willingness for mutual annihilation driven by the desire to take the enemy down as well

In his scheme Glasl names a “Superior vs. inferior” position taken, referring to the model of “Transactional Analysis” by Eric Berne (International Transactional Analysis Association, 2025). 
In this model, Berne explains that in a communication situation (incl. conflict) every party is in one of three “ego” states:

•	Parent
•	Adult
•	Ego

When the communication flows between different states or levels – leading in essence to a “difference of eye level” – this may lead to or further fuel a conflict. He also mentions “double-bind,” which is a concept describing situations where an individual is confronted with two or more conflicting messages, creating a paradoxical dilemma in which responding to one message inherently negates the other. This predicament is compounded by the fact that the individual cannot escape or resolve the conflict without facing negative consequences. (Gibson, 2024)

3.5.4	The importance of a third person

Conflicts can be constructive and lead to growth if participants work cooperatively, set aside distrust, and find solutions that address their needs and interests. However, many struggle to achieve this without help and often require a third party to assist in reaching mutually acceptable resolutions. (Moore, 2014, p. ix) A mediation approach is taken when for instance

•	Parties’ emotions or expression of negative feelings about the situation or toward each other are intense and are preventing a focused or calm discussion or agreement.
•	There is a lack of trust and respect between or among disputants that is hindering productive talks.
•	Communication between parties is poor in quantity or quality, and they cannot improve it on their own.
•	Misperceptions or stereotypes are hindering productive exchanges.
•	There are multiple issues in dispute, and disputants disagree about whether or how each should be addressed or resolved.
•	Disputants are stuck in bargaining over positions, each of their preferred solutions, and are unable to identify each other’s interests and develop mutually acceptable interest-based solutions.
•	Parties do not have an effective negotiating process, are using the wrong one, or are not using a potentially viable procedure to its best advantage.
•	Parties lack trust in each other and are concerned that the settlement will not be implemented as agreed.
(Moore, 2014, pp. 16-17).

3.5.5	An alternate to war or surrender

In some cases, conflicts may benefit from strategic actions taken by one party to de-escalate tensions and build trust gradually. This approach, rooted in good faith, emphasizes the importance of transparency and genuine intentions in fostering mutual understanding. Analyzing the tensions between the USA and Russia, the American Charles E. Osgood has developed a model as a third alternative to either “winning” (war) or “losing” (surrender). His GRIT model (Graduated and Reciprocated Initiatives in Tension Reduction), which acts to “de-escalate” a conflict, is based on these elements:

•	Unilateral Initiatives: One side takes the initiative to de-escalate tensions through small, measurable, and controlled steps, even without requiring prior agreement from the opposing party, signaling goodwill while maintaining strength and security.
•	Reciprocity: The model relies on the likelihood that the opposing party will reciprocate with similar de-escalatory actions, creating a “spiral of trust.”, encouraged through consistent, transparent actions.
•	Graduated Steps: Actions are incremental, making them less risky and more feasible for both sides to implement without fear of exploitation.
•	Tension Reduction: GRIT focuses on reducing psychological and environmental tensions, which are seen as major contributors to irrational decision-making and unstable situations.
(Osgood, 1965, pp. 85-98)

3.5.6	The ABC model of situation, thoughts, and feelings

The ABC model, developed by psychologist Albert Ellis, explains emotional distress by showing how emotions (C) are not directly caused by an event (A) but are instead mediated by our thoughts and interpretations (B).
•	A - Activating event: A situation or event that happens (e.g., someone ignores the other).
•	B - Beliefs/Interpretations: The thoughts or interpretations we have about the event (e.g., “They don’t like me” or “They’re preoccupied”).
•	C - Consequences: The emotions and behaviors that result (e.g., sadness, anger, or self-doubt).
(Segal, et al., 2013, pp. 161-164)

By identifying, analyzing, and challenging the B (beliefs/interpretations)—often occurring subconsciously as immediate explanations we create to make sense of events—one can influence emotional and behavioral consequences (C) and respond more effectively. These beliefs frequently include cognitive distortions, which are irrational thought patterns like overgeneralization or catastrophizing that intensify emotional distress. Challenging beliefs involves actively disputing their accuracy and rationality by considering evidence both supporting and contradicting these thoughts. (Dan & Sekoul, 2021)

This process helps “peel back” the layers of thoughts, positions, interests, and values to uncover the core needs of another person.

Empirical studies highlight the effectiveness of the ABC model in real-life contexts. For instance, a study with Norwegian adolescents showed that teaching students the ABC model significantly reduced anxiety, depression, and dysfunctional thoughts, while increasing hope and self-esteem (Sælid & Nordahl, 2017). 

3.5.7	A model for empathy

Building on strategies like mediation and tension-reduction models, which focus on resolving conflicts and fostering trust, Marshall B. Rosenberg takes the concept a step further by emphasizing the role of human needs and emotions in collaboration. His approach, Nonviolent Communication (NVC), provides a framework for understanding and addressing conflicts through empathy, helping individuals express their perspectives honestly while fostering mutual understanding and connection. In his model he differentiates between

•	The concrete actions we observe that affect our well-being
•	How we feel in relation to what we observe
•	The needs, values, desires, etc. that create our feelings
•	The concrete actions we request to enrich our lives
(Rosenberg, 2015, p. 7)

Daniel has further elaborated this model to better understand its components and to apply it to different situations of interpersonal collaboration. In addition to the external observation explained in the NVC process there is the element of thoughts, ideas and imagination that might play a role. Individuals often think in judgements: Positions in the sense of being right or wrong, which Rosenberg calls “Evaluation” (Rosenberg, 2015, p. 26). From methods such as the “Power & Interest Matrix” in Project Stakeholder Management we understand interests as desires (see also: WIIFM, “What Is In For Me”), with can be understood to be further related to our values and at the lowest level our individual needs.

(from outside to inside)
Observations, Stimuli: Hearing, Perception, Experience, Trigger
Positions: judgment, right and wrong, ok and not ok
Imagination: thoughts
Interests: what one wants to achieve
Feelings: Emotion
Values: Common or otherwise manifested ideas of what one values
Needs: as the most inside and abstract level

The extent to which these needs are being met defines our emotional reaction, may it be positive (good feelings) or negative (bad feelings) (Rosenberg, 2015, p. 44). In other words, the needs are stimulated leading to a certain feeling. This function is by some called a “trigger”, or “button(s) being pushed”.

3.5.8	Understanding cultural differences

While Nonviolent Communication (NVC) focuses on understanding and addressing individual needs and emotions to resolve conflicts and build empathy, cultural differences add an additional layer of complexity to interpersonal dynamics. Geert Hofstede’s cultural dimensions and Erin Meyer’s insights in the “Culture Map” highlight how deeply ingrained cultural norms influence communication, values, and expectations. Recognizing these differences is essential for applying NVC effectively in diverse settings, where empathy must extend across cultural boundaries to foster mutual understanding and collaboration.

Geert Hofstede defines culture as "the collective programming of the mind that distinguishes the members of one category of people from another". (Hofstede & Hofstede, 2010, p. 6) He refers to the following elements, which can be different in different cultures:

•	Power Distance: The extent to which less powerful members of organizations and institutions accept and expect that power is unequally distributed. (Hofstede & Hofstede, 2010, p. 55) 
•	Individualism and Collectivism: Individualism refers to societies in which the bonds between individuals are loose, while collectivism refers to societies in which people are integrated into strong, cohesive groups from birth. (Hofstede & Hofstede, 2010, p. 89) 
•	Masculinity and Femininity: Masculinity stands for a society that values competitiveness, assertiveness and material success, while femininity stands for a preference for relationships, modesty, caring for the weak and quality of life. (Hofstede & Hofstede, 2010, p. 138) 
•	Uncertainty Avoidance: The extent to which members of a culture are uncomfortable with uncertainty and ambiguity, leading them to support beliefs and behaviors that promise certainty and conformity. (Hofstede & Hofstede, 2010, p. 188) 
•	Long- and Short- Term Orientation: Long-term orientation focuses on the future, values thrift and perseverance, while short-term orientation focuses on the present or past and values respect for tradition and fulfillment of social obligations. (Hofstede & Hofstede, 2010, p. 239) 

One topic discussed in of this thesis is Humor, which is highly culture specific. For example, in Dutch culture, “friendly insults” like “You scoundrel” express warm sympathy, while in Indonesia, where maintaining face is crucial, such remarks are taken literally. (Hofstede & Hofstede, 2010, pp. 390-391)
Inspired by Hofstede’s and other existing culture models and based on her own experience and research, Erin Meyer in her “Culture Map” model lists the following elements to being different per geographic culture, and by that, possibly per individual working in a culturally diverse project team:

Low-context
Good communication is precise, simple, and clear. Messages are expressed and understood at face falue. Repetition is appreciated if it helps clarify the communication.	

High-context
Good communication is sophisticated, nuanced, and layered. Messages are both spoken and read between the lines. Messages are often implied but not plainly expressed. (Meyer, 2015, p. 39)

Direct negative feedback
Negative feedback to a colleague is provided frankly, bluntly, honestly. Negative messages stand alone, not softened by positive ones. Absolute descriptors are often used (totally inappropriate, completely unprofessional) when criticizing. Criticism may be given to an individual in front of a group.

Indirect negative feedback
Negative feedback to a colleague is provided softly, subtly, diplomatically. Positive messages are used to wrap negative ones. Qualifying descriptors are often used (sort of inappropriate, slightly unprofessional) when criticizing. Criticism is given only in private. (Meyer, 2015, p. 69)


Applications-first
Individuals are trained to begin with a fact, statement, or opinion and later add concepts to back up or explain the conclusion as necessary. The preference is to begin a message or report with an executive summary or bullet points. Discussions are approached in a practical, concrete manner. Theoretical or philosophical discussions are avoided in a business environment.	

Principles-first
Individuals have been trained to first develop the theory or complex concept before presenting a fact, statement, or opinion. The preference is to begin a message or report by building up a theoretical argument before moving on to a conclusion. The conceptual principles underlying each situation are valued. (Meyer, 2015, p. 96)

Egalitarian
The ideal distance between a boss and a subordinate is low. The best boss is a facilitator among equals. Organizational structures are flat. Communication often skips hierarchical lines.	

Hierarchical
The ideal distance between a boss and a subordinate is high. The best boss is a strong director who leads from the front. Status is important. Organizational structures are multilayered and fixed. Communication follows set hierarchical lines. (Meyer, 2015, p. 125)

Consensual
Decisions are made in groups through unanimous agreement.

Top-down
Decisions are made by individuals (usually the boss). (Meyer, 2015, p. 150)

Task-based
Trust is built through business-related activities. Work relationships are built and dropped easily, based on the practicality of the situation. You do good work consistently, you are reliable, I enjoy working with you, I trust you.

Relationship-based
Trust is built through sharing meals, evening drinks, and visits at the coffee machine. Work relationships build up slowly over the long term. I've seen who you are at a deep level, I've shared personal time with you, I know others well who trust you, I trust you. (Meyer, 2015, p. 171)

Confrontational
Disagreement and debate are positive for the team or organization. Open confrontation is appropriate and will not negatively impact the relationship.

Avoids confrontation
Disagreement and debate are negative for the team or organization. Open confrontation is inappropriate and will break group harmony or negatively impact the relationship. (Meyer, 2015, p. 201)

Linear-time
Project steps are approached in a sequential fashion, completing one task before beginning the next. One thing at a time. No interruptions. The focus is on the deadline and sticking to the schedule. Emphasis is on promptness and good organization over flexibility.

Flexible-time
Project steps are approached in a fluid manner, changing tasks as opportunities arise. Many things are dealt with at once and interruptions accepted. The focus is on adaptability, and flexibility is valued over organization. (Meyer, 2015, p. 227)

While Meyer’s research as such may not typically be regarded as a fully “scientifically validated”, her research draws heavily from existing cultural frameworks, such as those by Edward Twitchell Hall, Geert Hendrik Hofstede, Alfonsus (Fons) Trompenaars, Charles Hampden-Turner, Richard Eugene Nisbett which are more extensively validated through empirical data.

Effective intercultural communication is vital to minimizing misunderstandings. Cultural dimensions significantly shape conflict management approaches, necessitating adaptive leadership or ‘switch leadership’ to respond to evolving cultural contexts within a project. Appreciating culture as a multilayered construct—including national, organizational, and individual dimensions—enables deeper insight beyond superficial stereotypes. Project managers must proactively engage with diverse cultures, critically reflect on their assumptions, and practically embrace diverse cultural practices to enhance mutual understanding, empathy, and effective collaboration. (Isern, 2016)

Trust, as outlined under 3.5.10, emerges as a central facilitator in intercultural project settings, significantly enhancing transparency, effective communication, and team cohesion. Building trust actively across cultural boundaries transforms diversity from a potential source of misunderstandings into a key asset for project success and collaborative excellence. (Lückmann & Färber, 2016) 

3.5.9	Differences between generations

Building on the importance of understanding cultural differences, generational differences add another layer of complexity to interpersonal and organizational dynamics. As multiple generations work side by side, each brings distinct needs, values, and expectations shaped by their unique experiences and societal influences. Recognizing and addressing these generational differences is crucial for fostering effective collaboration, engagement, and motivation in diverse teams and workplaces.

Four generations are currently working together on the German labor market (baby boomers, generations X/Y/Z), all of whom have different needs, wishes and expectations of the labor market and employers. (Hesse & Mattmüller, 2019, p. 55)

The changing elements include:

•	Trend towards individualization: Generation Z places particular value on personal autonomy and self-realization, which increases the focus on individual needs and interests.
•	The new search for meaning: Modern employees are increasingly looking for jobs that not only offer economic security, but also fulfill a deeper, social or personal meaning.
•	The increasing rejection of responsibility: Many young career starters are showing a growing reluctance to take on long-term and far-reaching professional responsibility.
•	The indecisiveness of the new generation: The well-educated young generation has access to numerous career opportunities, which often leads to indecision and frequent job changes.
•	Hierarchy is losing respect: Flat hierarchies and communication at eye level are increasingly preferred by the younger generations over traditional, rigid organizational structures.
•	Focus on comfort: The desire for a comfortable working environment with a good work-life balance and flexible working conditions is becoming increasingly important for many employees.
•	The protean career as the career path of the future: The protean career is characterized by adaptability and self-determination, whereby individuals adapt their career paths flexibly to personal goals and changes. It therefore has no "common thread" in the narrower sense, it is not linear.
(Hesse & Mattmüller, 2019, p. 86)

3.5.10	Error, Psychological Safety and Trust

Projects are inherently uncertain, and the human dimension, including interpersonal dynamics, introduces additional layers of what might be termed “social uncertainty.” Consequently, unexpected events inevitably occur—some leading to outcomes perceived as negative, undesirable, or entirely unforeseen. These occurrences can be understood as errors, and thus it can be stated that errors are a natural component of project work.

However, the mere occurrence of errors is not the decisive factor influencing project outcomes; rather, it is the environment in which errors are addressed that makes the difference. Research has demonstrated that teams often perceived as “better” might initially appear to report more mistakes. Upon deeper investigation, it becomes clear that such teams do not necessarily commit more errors but instead foster an atmosphere that encourages openness in reporting and discussing them. This supportive environment is characterized by psychological safety, where trust and candor enable teams to acknowledge mistakes transparently and learn constructively from them. Thus, the true marker of high-performing teams is not the absence of errors, but their willingness and ability to openly confront and collaboratively learn from these inevitable incidents.  (Edmondson, 2024, pp. 12-15)

Amy Edmondson defines Psychological Safety as an environment where team members feel comfortable taking interpersonal risks, admitting mistakes, and openly discussing errors. Extensive research across multiple fields now confirms that psychologically safe teams perform better, innovate more, experience less burnout, and, in healthcare settings, even achieve lower patient mortality rates. Psychological safety enables groups to fail well by promoting openness, candor, and the willingness to ask for help, ultimately enhancing learning, growth, and overall team effectiveness. (Edmondson, 2024, pp. 15-16)

Moreover, emotional intelligence significantly strengthens trust within project teams. Leaders who effectively manage their own emotions and perceive team members’ emotional states foster environments characterized by openness, clear communication, and effective collaboration, thus providing ideal conditions for psychological safety and candid error reporting. (Castro, et al., 2022)

Psychological safety itself depends fundamentally upon trust. Trust, as a foundational element of successful relationships, collaboration, and leadership, fosters open communication, reduces interpersonal conflict, and cultivates an environment in which individuals feel secure enough to contribute honestly and innovate without fear of negative consequences. The Integrative Model of Organizational Trust by Mayer, Davis, and Schoorman (Mayer, et al., 1995, pp. 709-734) provides a valuable framework for understanding trust in organizational contexts. According to this model, trustworthiness rests upon three key factors:

•	Ability: Demonstrating competence in a specific domain.
•	Benevolence: Showing genuine goodwill toward others.
•	Integrity: Consistently adhering to principles accepted and valued by others.

Recent empirical findings validate the significance of Ability, Benevolence, and Integrity as foundational dimensions for building and maintaining trust within project teams, strongly supporting Mayer et al.’s Integrative Model of Organizational Trust. Additionally, trust in the project manager was empirically confirmed to significantly correlate with overall project success, emphasizing trust as a crucial factor for achieving positive project outcomes. (Castro, et al., 2022)

In practical terms, trust involves a willingness to be vulnerable, confidently expecting that others will act in ways that meet our needs and expectations. Such trust is evidenced by risk-taking behaviors—actions like delegating critical tasks, openly sharing sensitive information, or depending upon team members to follow through reliably, despite uncertainty or potential negative outcomes. Thus, in project contexts, trust functions as the crucial relational underpinning that enables psychological safety and transforms error management into an opportunity for continuous learning and improvement. (Edmondson, 2019, pp. 16-17)

Research explicitly demonstrates that a project manager’s emotional intelligence (EI)—their capacity to understand and manage emotions—is a powerful predictor of trust within teams, reinforcing the critical role emotionally intelligent leadership plays in effective team collaboration and successful project outcomes. (Castro, et al., 2022)

Although trust and psychological safety share much common ground, they are not interchangeable. Psychological safety is experienced at a group level: team members typically hold shared perceptions regarding how safe it feels to speak openly, admit errors, or express concerns. Trust, conversely, exists within interpersonal relationships between individuals or between an individual and an organization, making it more specific and personal; for instance, one might trust one colleague explicitly yet have reservations about another. 

Additionally, psychological safety describes a temporally immediate experience related to anticipated interpersonal reactions—it concerns whether others will offer understanding and support in response to one’s immediate actions or admissions. Trust, by contrast, relates to expectations about whether others will behave reliably or fulfill promises in future interactions. In other words, trust involves extending the benefit of the doubt to others, whereas psychological safety involves expecting others to extend that same benefit of the doubt to oneself. (Edmondson, 2019, pp. 16-17)

Job satisfaction alone was found not to significantly predict project success. Instead, trust was identified as a far more influential interpersonal factor within project environments, underscoring the importance of actively fostering trust within project teams to achieve successful outcomes (Castro, et al., 2022). Given the inherent temporary nature of projects, active and rapid cultivation of trust is particularly essential for enabling effective collaboration and ensuring project success.
