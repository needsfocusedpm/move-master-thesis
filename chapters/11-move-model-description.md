As the model evolved based on insights and patterns identified in the interviews, key requirements were validated and refined, resulting in several updates and improvements. Additionally, the final model was updated by recognizing insights derived from the quantitative element of the research (web survey):

•	The initial model treated “motivation” and “mediation” as separate processes but was later integrated into a single core process, with distinctions maintained to address specific differences. This integration reflects findings that teams motivated through a needs-focused approach are less likely to experience conflicts, and when conflicts occur, they resolve them more efficiently. Conversely, resolving conflicts through needs-focused mediation was found to enhance motivation, underscoring a logical interplay between the two elements.
•	The initial separation between “mindset” and “skillset” elements was reconsidered based on interview feedback indicating overlap and indistinct boundaries, leading to their integration.
•	Following recommendations from interviewees to simplify the skills framework, a core set of nine skills was established and validated through a web-based survey. An additional tenth skill emerged from an open question in the survey, resulting in a refined, ten-skill model structured for clarity and comprehensibility.
•	Further details and explicit references to established theoretical frameworks, such as Tuckman’s team development phases and project lifecycle stages, have been integrated into the model without altering its fundamental structure, enhancing theoretical alignment and practical relevance.
•	Recognizing the dynamic nature of needs management, the model has shifted from a linear, checklist-oriented approach to a circular and iterative one. This change explicitly emphasizes periodic structured interactions—such as retrospectives, phase-gate reviews, pulse-checks, and regular emotional check-ins—to sustain momentum and ensure continuous responsiveness to evolving team and project needs.
•	The mediation component has been refined into a structured, formal, mediation-inspired process that emphasizes emotional integrity (self-awareness, emotional clarity, stability) before engaging in emotionally charged interactions. Additionally, the model explicitly integrates “needs validation and acknowledgment” as an essential step prior to exploring practical solutions, reinforcing emotional effectiveness and stability.
•	Clear criteria and signals were established to identify when internal mediation limits are exceeded, indicating the necessity of involving external mediation support. Additionally, the importance of a safe, confidential environment facilitating emotional openness during mediation has been explicitly articulated.
•	To ensure practical applicability, structured guidelines for implementing the MOVE model at both project and organizational levels were included:
o	At the project level, the model supports project staffing and HR development with explicit connections to project lifecycle stages and stage-gate processes, ensuring consistency throughout project execution.
o	At the organizational level, the model provides integration points for HR and recruiting, organizational project management (OPM), project management offices (PMOs), PM standards, PMO maturity development, and organizational change initiatives.
•	Finally, the model was refined and extended in three key ways: First, trust emerged as an overarching theme throughout the research, leading to the development of a dedicated trust model. Second, the competency framework was expanded into a standalone competency model. And third, “MOVE” was established as the overarching title for the entire framework—reflecting the shared focus of all three components on moving work forward and engaging people meaningfully. A simple, intuitive visual representation was created to support understanding and practical application by managers and project teams.
 

1.1.1	Not just looking: Focusing.

In this thesis, the author deliberately uses the term “needs-focused leadership” instead of “needs-based” or “needs-oriented” leadership. The choice emphasizes that addressing human needs in leadership is not just about direction or foundation, but about a deliberate, systematic engagement with what lies beneath the surface. “Focused” highlights the importance of discerning subtle motivations, differentiating between emotional layers, and considering diverse perspectives rather than responding to general or superficial notions of need.

At the core of this approach lies the principle of focusing: a deliberate, investigative stance aimed at uncovering the essential motivations behind behavior. Rather than merely acknowledging needs at face value, needs-focused leadership involves looking beyond what is immediately visible to gain clarity about what truly matters.

This depth of focus enables leaders to engage more authentically with stakeholders, ensuring that their actions align with core human needs rather than surface-level symptoms.

A Focus on Feelings

The extent to which needs are fulfilled or—especially in conflict situations—violated or threatened, is directly reflected in an individual’s emotional state, their feelings. These feelings, expressed through body language, tone of voice, and facial expressions, function like an emotional “traffic light,” providing others visible clues about someone’s internal emotional experience. However, feelings often have multiple layers that need to be carefully uncovered and interpreted.

In contexts such as demotivation or conflict, the most immediately recognizable feeling is usually anger. This makes Gottman’s Anger Iceberg Model particularly valuable. In this model, anger represents only the visible tip of the emotional iceberg—easy to see but challenging to interpret accurately, as the deeper underlying emotions remain hidden. Understanding this model allows individuals to explore beneath the surface, identifying core emotions outlined by Ekman, which the author interprets as “water-line level emotions”. While these emotions are still part of the visible portion of the iceberg, requiring slightly more focus to recognize compared to the immediately evident “tip” of anger, they remain relatively universal and similarly understood across different cultures. Nevertheless, they are still not as deeply hidden, articulated, or personally nuanced as the layers that lie even further beneath. 

Moving beyond Ekman’s initial insight, it becomes possible to differentiate emotions even more finely, as demonstrated by researchers such as Nummenmaa and Baer, who identified more than eight core feelings and numerous nuanced emotional states.

Thus, focusing on feelings—essential to understanding underlying needs—means precisely recognizing, specifying, and validating emotions. 

A Focus on Needs

When aiming to understand people, it makes sense to start from a broader, more general perspective and gradually move closer to uncover what’s beneath the surface. Initially, it’s valuable to understand how individuals perceive life itself. Antonovsky and Eriksson refer to this as the Sense of Coherence (SOC). Simply put, SOC captures how someone makes sense of life’s challenges, seeing them as understandable, manageable, and worthwhile.

Taking a step deeper, it becomes helpful to identify specific "drivers" or internal messages that guide behavior, as described by Kahler. These drivers, such as the urge to be perfect or to please others, shape our day-to-day actions and reactions, often subconsciously influencing our emotional responses.

Going further, we can explore deeper personal motives. According to McClelland, fundamental motives like Achievement, Power, and Affiliation guide our choices and interactions, while Ryan highlights motives such as Autonomy, Competence, and Relatedness. Recognizing these core motives helps clarify why individuals pursue certain goals and relationships.

Next, understanding needs in greater depth brings us to Maslow’s hierarchy, ranging from basic physiological and safety needs to higher-level needs like belonging, esteem, and ultimately self-actualization. Rogers emphasizes internal psychological conditions necessary for personal growth, such as Self-direction, Openness to Experience, Acceptance of Others, and Trusting oneself. Being attuned to these aspects means recognizing someone’s capacity for authenticity, openness, and genuine connection.

The closer and further one looks, the more nuanced and differentiated the picture becomes. Additional elements, such as the various strengths outlined in the VIA model, can be identified, offering insights into individual characteristics that enable people to thrive. Moreover, the concept of needs grouped into ME, US, and SYSTEM needs, provided by the Spectrum of Needs model, further deepens our understanding by categorizing needs according to different relational contexts—personal, interpersonal, and organizational or systemic.

While focusing on emotions alone can already be challenging, especially during emotionally intense situations, addressing the underlying needs and deeper psychological elements is even more complex. However, by carefully stepping from a broad understanding toward these deeper internal layers, we gain profound insights into human behavior.

A Focus on Strategy and Action

To identify and select the most effective strategy—whether it involves internal thoughts, external actions, or adjustments to our external environment—it is essential to take a careful and similarly focused approach. This ensures that our choices serve not only our own interests but also those of the people involved.

In conflicts or challenging situations, we might experience emotional stress, prompting impulsive reactions, as highlighted by Walker. To handle such situations more effectively, Segal’s ABC model can assist by distinguishing between actual events and our emotional responses to them. Additionally, the decision-making process concerning how much energy to invest in an action—characterized by valence (how strongly we want something) and expectancy (how likely we believe our effort will succeed)—can be extremely rapid, sometimes causing premature action without adequate consideration of important contextual elements, such as culture. Here, Hofstede’s cultural dimensions model provides valuable insights, helping us understand and respect cultural differences and enabling appropriate actions.

Listening closely to our “inner team,” as outlined by Schulz von Thun, is crucial. Recognizing the current escalation level of conflicts, described by Glasl, is also vital to prevent further escalation. Differences in perceived status or “eye-level” communication, as detailed in Berne’s transactional analysis, may exacerbate conflicts, making resolution more challenging. At times, a neutral third party, as recommended by Moore, becomes essential to mediate effectively. Such moderation can help focus not only on solving the problem but also on managing emotions, a principle emphasized by Kabat-Zinn’s mindfulness approach, promoting a “being” rather than a purely “doing-driven” mindset.

Effective conflict resolution further benefits from balancing assertiveness and cooperativeness, as described in the Thomas-Kilmann Instrument. Supporting actions aimed at reaching agreements include active de-escalation and trust-building strategies, outlined in Osgood’s GRIT model. Integrating these strategies with Rosenberg’s Nonviolent Communication method, as well as trust and psychological safety models described by Mayer, Edmondson, and the author’s own trust model, provides a robust framework for successfully navigating and resolving interpersonal conflicts.

All this requires considerable time and effort, as well as a specific "type of person" equipped with the appropriate competencies. This is precisely where the MOVE model becomes relevant: it guides individuals towards a needs-oriented leadership perspective and approach. Grounded in Seligman’s Positive Psychology, the MOVE model emphasizes Motivation and Engagement enhanced through experiences of Flow, positive Relationships, and positive Emotions, ultimately leading to trust-based cooperation, agreement, and overall well-being.

 
1.2	Structure and elements

The MOVE Model is a comprehensive and disciplined framework designed to foster personal growth, resolve conflicts effectively, and support the selection or development of project leaders based on essential competencies for successful leadership. It comprises three interconnected components:

The associated (MOVE) Process Model outlines a structured, needs-focused approach to maintaining motivation within project teams and mediating conflicts as they arise. This integrated model demonstrates the interplay between motivation and mediation, providing clarity and practical guidance for implementation. Regarding motivation, the model emphasizes helping individuals understand and meet their needs, pursue meaningful goals, and sustain satisfaction and purpose. In terms of mediation, it facilitates recognizing differences, resolving conflicts constructively, and navigating complex interpersonal or cultural dynamics. Its clear structure supports seamless integration with existing organizational processes and frameworks.

The associated (MOVE) Competency Model outlines the key competencies essential for effective, needs-focused project leadership. Each competency integrates both developable skills—which can be enhanced through training, coaching, and personal development—and more hard-wired personality traits, which tend to be stable over time and should primarily inform the selection and placement of project leaders. This dual focus ensures that leadership development efforts are both targeted and realistic, while also supporting thoughtful staffing decisions.

The associated (MOVE) Trust Model outlines the key elements that form the foundation for establishing and sustaining a needs-focused, cooperative team environment—acting like the “glue” that holds everything together. It identifies three equally important components, with trust being at risk the moment even one of them becomes unstable.

Importantly, the MOVE Model is not intended as an exclusive leadership approach; rather, it complements the broader set of competencies required of contemporary, adaptive leaders. Its effectiveness depends on integrating the model with the leader’s individual personality traits, the specific project context, and the organization’s culture.

A foundational assumption of the MOVE Model is that project leaders already possess essential core competencies in project management. To reference these baseline competencies, the widely recognized “Talent Triangle” developed by the Project Management Institute (PMI)—the world’s largest professional association for project management—serves as a practical orientation. Although this model was developed primarily from practical experience rather than scientific research, its widespread acceptance and application within the professional community provide a meaningful framework to define the fundamental competencies assumed by the MOVE Model.

1.3	Implementation Guidelines

This chapter outlines how the MOVE model can be practically applied—both within individual projects and across the broader organizational environment. It offers hands-on strategies for embedding needs-focused leadership into day-to-day practice, supporting trust, emotional alignment, and sustainable team performance.

1.3.1	In a project

Emotional Frontloading

Effective team collaboration and project success begin with Emotional Frontloading—a deliberate and time-intensive initial process. At its core is the creation of an environment defined by psychological safety, characterized by openness, emotional alignment, and a constructive attitude toward failure. This means fostering a climate that carefully balances tolerance for mistakes with reliability and accountability. Within this context, humor plays a notable role by reducing tension and facilitating more natural, inclusive interactions. Especially in intercultural teams, Emotional Frontloading becomes even more essential, as emotional alignment requires greater sensitivity and care.

At the outset of the project, a dedicated session—often referred to as Workshop Zero or Emotional Kick-off—can be used to operationalize Emotional Frontloading. During this session, agreeing on shared ground rules and clearly defining common team values are essential steps toward emotional alignment. Clarifying individual roles, working styles, levels of autonomy, and task orientations—and linking them explicitly to broader strategic goals—ensures consistent direction and coherence within the team. Instruments such as a Team Charter or Teaming Agreement can be used to formalize these understandings and strengthen mutual accountability.

A valuable complementary step is the open exchange or co-creation of personality profiles during informal team-building activities. This promotes deeper mutual understanding and enhances the team’s ability to anticipate and proactively address potential conflict dynamics. Similarly, incorporating emotional “lessons learned”—such as personal motivation triggers or recurring sources of friction from past experiences—provides the team with concrete, preventive strategies for navigating future challenges.

To deepen individual engagement, project managers can also develop a personalized “project story” for each team member. This narrative links the project’s purpose, goals, and expected contributions to the individual’s unique needs, values, and strengths. Whether expressed verbally or in writing, these individualized perspectives help each team member see how the project connects to what matters most to them—fostering emotional resonance, ownership, and long-term motivation.

In this context, integrating the MOVE Trust Model into the Team Charter or as part of the team’s set of ground rules offers particular value. It provides a structured framework for understanding, building, and maintaining interpersonal trust—an essential foundation for psychological safety and sustainable collaboration.

Ultimately, prioritizing Emotional Frontloading equips teams with a strong emotional foundation, boosting their resilience, adaptability, and effectiveness across the entire project lifecycle.

Project Phases, Stages, and Emotional Checkpoints

Projects are typically structured into phases, each beginning with dedicated setup activities—most notably during initial project initiation—and concluding with retrospective reviews. These transition points, marking the end of one phase and the beginning of another, are ideal opportunities for targeted, needs-focused actions.
At the start of each phase, teams should conduct an emotional check-in, establishing alignment not only on factual content and goals but also on emotional readiness and team cohesion. Likewise, phase conclusions should feature reflective reviews, asking questions such as “How have we done?” and “What’s next?”—considering emotional aspects alongside technical or performance-related criteria. Integrating emotional frontloading into these checkpoints enhances team stability, motivation, and long-term effectiveness.

Organizations employing a structured stage-gate model often formalize these emotional check-ins and check-outs. In such environments, projects must fulfill specific criteria or deliverables to advance from one phase to another. Consequently, emotional checkpoints might become mandated components, explicitly outlined by organizational guidelines, ensuring that emotional and interpersonal aspects receive consistent attention throughout the project’s lifecycle.

The MOVE model can be applied at the following points:

1.	Early in the project, linked to initial planning activities, a comprehensive "emotional frontloading" activity is conducted. This includes defining the project approach, establishing ground rules, and formalizing initial emotional alignment and consensus in a Team Charter. Given the project's focus on organizational and cultural change, a change manager participates from the outset.
2.	At the conclusion of the detailed planning and preparation phase—prior to initiating the project's most critical tasks—an "emotional review" is carried out. This step identifies lessons learned and defines specific actions for the forthcoming phases.
3.	The subteam responsible for technical development undertakes its own emotional setup and check-in. As this team employs an adaptive (agile) approach, distributing several management responsibilities among team members or through dual leadership structures, regular informal "emotional retrospectives" are conducted throughout the development phase.
4.	Toward the end of the development phase, just before an integrated stage where close cooperation between the development subteam and the core project team becomes essential, another emotional review takes place. This includes preparatory and alignment activities to ensure smooth initiation of the next phase.
5.	Upon completion of the main project work, a substantial emotional review is conducted to prepare for the handover to operations. At this stage, emotional alignment explicitly involves members from future operational roles, supported by the organizational change manager.
6.	Once the project concludes, a comprehensive emotional review is executed, engaging stakeholders who participated at various stages throughout the project. This final step involves capturing and documenting best practices and lessons learned for future initiatives.

Project Staffing and MOVE Competencies

Projects aim to involve the best-suited individuals, a principle that applies equally to project managers and team members. When a company or project sponsor seeks a leader capable of employing a needs-focused approach, the MOVE competencies serve as an essential reference framework. Evaluating a potential project leader involves several critical questions: Which competencies does this person already possess, and how developed or robust are they?

Competencies understood primarily as traits, which are challenging and time-intensive to develop, play a particularly critical role. A leader lacking these foundational traits cannot realistically be expected to effectively guide a team with a needs-focused approach. In such cases, it may be advisable to select another, more suitable leader. If this isn’t feasible, stakeholders should consciously adjust their expectations regarding the quality and effectiveness of the project’s leadership.

Dual Leadership Models and Their Limitations

Projects may occasionally face situations where the designated project leader—due to their personality traits or specific skill set—cannot effectively fulfill the requirements of a needs-focused leadership approach. In these cases, some organizations consider adopting a dual leadership model. Under this approach, leadership responsibilities are divided: One person is responsible for performing the task leadership functions and another person for the maintenance leadership behaviors. (Pavitt & Curtis, 1994) 

In practice this may may mean that one project leader primarily manages the technical or product-related aspects, while a second person assumes the role of an “emotional leader,” attending specifically to the emotional and interpersonal dimensions of the project.

A dual leadership model offers a range of advantages that go far beyond a simple division of labor. By sharing responsibilities, co-leaders reduce individual workload and the risk of burnout, enhancing both the sustainability and long-term effectiveness of leadership. Joint decision-making creates a natural system of checks and balances, resulting in more thoroughly considered and balanced outcomes. At the same time, when one leader focuses on social and emotional aspects, team cohesion and trust are strengthened.

Another key benefit is the ability to clearly separate strategic and operational responsibilities. While one leader concentrates on long-term direction, the other manages day-to-day operations—an especially effective setup in complex or fast-changing environments. Co-leadership also serves a symbolic function: visible collaboration at the top signals shared ownership and fosters a more inclusive, participatory team culture.

Moreover, the diversity of perspectives within a leadership duo creates a fertile ground for innovation. Constructive friction between two approaches can generate more creative solutions and bolder initiatives. From a continuity standpoint, the model also provides resilience: if one leader is unavailable, the other can step in, ensuring stable leadership. Finally, internal accountability is strengthened, as mutual oversight helps reduce the risk of unilateral errors and can build greater trust among external stakeholders.

While dual leadership offers substantial benefits, it also comes with inherent challenges that can undermine its effectiveness if not managed carefully. One of the most common issues is role ambiguity: when responsibilities between co-leaders are not clearly defined, confusion, inefficiencies, and decision gaps can quickly emerge. This lack of clarity can frustrate team members and slow down operations.

Decision-making can also become slower, as co-leaders typically rely on consensus, which may delay action—especially under pressure or in fast-moving environments. In cases where alignment is lacking, power struggles or interpersonal conflicts can arise, potentially paralyzing the organization and eroding trust within the team.

Moreover, co-leadership demands ongoing coordination, which increases the communication burden and raises the risk of sending mixed messages to staff. If there’s a perceived imbalance in visibility, workload, or influence, resentment may build—destabilizing the partnership. This is especially problematic in organizations with deeply rooted hierarchical structures, where shared leadership may meet resistance or create confusion among stakeholders.

Another risk is that employees might exploit divisions between co-leaders to advance their own agendas, undermining authority and weakening team cohesion. Over time, many co-leadership arrangements prove fragile, often reverting to single-leader models due to instability, leadership turnover, or shifting strategic priorities. Finally, co-leadership places heightened emotional demands on both leaders; it requires mutual trust, maturity, and a willingness to share power—traits that not every leadership pairing possesses or is prepared to cultivate.

While there is substantial scientific evidence on both the benefits and challenges of dual-leadership models, organizations considering this approach must carefully weigh its advantages and drawbacks—and ensure that the necessary structures and protocols are in place to harness its strengths while minimizing potential risks. (Wendt, 2024) The topic is shaped by ongoing workplace dynamics and remains the subject of active research. While it is relevant, it goes beyond the scope of this thesis. However, it could certainly be explored further in the context of project management.

Needs-Focused Leadership in Self-Organized (Sub-)Teams

In projects employing an adaptive (agile) approach, product or service development is often carried out by self-organized (sub-)teams. Such teams typically function with greater autonomy, making their own decisions about how to perform tasks. Leadership in these contexts tends to be facilitative rather than directive, exemplified by roles such as the Scrum Master in Scrum methodology. This facilitative role aligns closely with the concept of Servant Leadership, where the leader supports and enables the team’s workflow rather than dictating it.

Servant leaders embody many elements associated with needs-focused leadership. However, self-organized teams differ from traditional teams with directive leadership, particularly regarding how leadership responsibilities are distributed. In self-organized setups, many leadership responsibilities—including emotional management and needs-focused actions—are actually carried out by team members themselves rather than solely by the designated leader.

For effective self-organization and emotional alignment (e.g., emotional check-ins and check-outs), it’s beneficial if team members themselves possess the key traits and skills outlined in the MOVE Competency Model. These competencies should not be limited to the facilitator (e.g., Scrum Master), but also be present within the team, enabling more autonomous management of emotional and interpersonal dynamics.

Managerial Mediation, Needs-Focused Leadership, and Boundaries

Needs-focused leadership inherently reduces the relational distance between leaders and team members. While the leader often assumes the role of a coach, supporting individual team members’ personal and professional development based on their specific needs, there is a critical boundary that must not be crossed—namely, avoiding a dynamic that resembles a psychotherapeutic (“red-couch”) setting. Respecting these limits is closely tied to the competency of establishing and maintaining emotional integrity, which requires the leader to remain aware of and adhere to clear interpersonal boundaries.

Similarly, when project managers mediate conflicts within their teams—referred to as managerial mediation—there are inherent boundaries and limitations that must be acknowledged and upheld. Situations may arise where the manager’s neutrality becomes compromised, trust issues emerge, or conflicts escalate beyond the leader’s capacity to effectively mediate.

In these scenarios, involving a neutral third party is essential. Suitable individuals might include uninvolved team members or managers, HR professionals trained in mediation, or external mediators from outside the organization. Regardless of who assumes this mediation role, maintaining neutrality, impartiality, and trustworthiness remains critical.

Importantly, even when mediation responsibilities shift away from the primary project leader, a needs-focused approach should continue to guide the conflict resolution process. Consequently, third-party mediators must embody the competencies outlined in the MOVE Competency Model, ensuring mediation remains consistent, empathetic, and effective while respecting clearly established emotional boundaries.

Competency Development and HR Support

The MOVE Competence Model integrates both skills—trainable behaviors that can be enhanced through targeted development—and traits, which are more stable personality characteristics and better suited for selection and staffing decisions. While both are essential for effective leadership, HR development should primarily focus on skills, as they offer more immediate opportunities for growth and behavioral change.

To foster needs-focused leadership in projects, organizations should prioritize the structured development of skill-related competencies. Some of these may already be covered in existing project management or soft skills programs. However, many essential skills—particularly those linked to emotional intelligence, psychological safety, or needs-focused communication—require dedicated training, coaching, or supervision.

As part of this foundation, training in Mental Health First Aid (MHFA), mental health-sensitive leadership, and basic neurodiversity awareness should become a standard component of any foundational project management curriculum. These competencies equip project managers to recognize early signs of mental distress, respond appropriately, and foster inclusive environments that account for diverse cognitive and emotional needs.

To complement this broader awareness with tools for individual-level insight and intervention, personality models such as the Process Communication Model (PCM) can serve as valuable tools in this process. By offering nuanced insights into communication patterns and stress responses, such profiles help:

•	identify current skill strengths and gaps,
•	guide personalized development plans,
•	and support the design of effective interventions tailored to the individual’s needs and role.

The thesis findings show clear differences in how competencies are prioritized across organizational levels. Executives often value self-regulation and systems thinking, while team members place higher importance on listening, emotional balance, and clarity. These diverging perspectives can create hidden misalignments unless addressed explicitly.

To bridge these gaps:

•	Open cross-level dialogues to explore why each group ranks competencies differently. This creates mutual understanding, reduces misinterpretation, and surfaces implicit expectations.
•	Combine self-leadership with relational training. For example, pair modules on self-regulation with exercises in active listening and empathy under a unified theme like “Self-leadership and High-Quality Connection.”

Another critical challenge is translating strategic intent into day-to-day clarity. Project Managers often serve as the bridge between the sponsor, other executives and experts—but without support, this translation can falter.
•	Train project managers to break down complex strategies into step-by-step clarity drills. This addresses team members’ strong need for direction and builds alignment with higher-level goals.
•	Make systems thinking visible and tangible. Since experts rarely see systemic considerations in action, encourage project managers to share tools like root-cause maps or structured decision frameworks. This not only builds appreciation but also enhances transparency and understanding across roles.

One particularly promising development format is the creation of coaching triads that span organizational layers: one executive, one manager, and one expert. Each participant:

•	coaches the next level down on their highest-rated competency, and
•	receives coaching from the level above on their most underdeveloped one.

This format promotes horizontal and vertical learning, strengthens mutual appreciation, and aligns leadership practices across the hierarchy.

MOVE Competencies and Organizational Change Management

Focusing on the human side of work is especially critical in Organizational Change Management (OCM). OCM deals with implementing changes—such as modifications to systems, processes, infrastructure, or organizational culture—and aims to help individuals embrace these changes, thereby reducing resistance.

Projects frequently involve change initiatives, with the project leader often tasked to handle change management responsibilities or a dedicated change manager (or change agent) being appointed. While change management is closely related to project activities and their outcomes, it typically extends beyond the project’s conclusion, influencing operational and functional business activities.

Resistance to change often emerges because stakeholders perceive their needs as being threatened or actively compromised. Consequently, the MOVE Process Model—designed with a needs-focused approach—can be effectively applied within change management activities. Similarly, the MOVE Competency Model is directly relevant for professionals working in change management roles, providing a structured foundation for addressing stakeholder needs and facilitating smoother transitions.

1.3.2	In the Organizational Project Management (and beyond)

Organizational Project Management and Needs-Focused Leadership

Organizational Project Management (OPM) encompasses the governance framework, processes, tools, techniques, standards, and guidelines that define how projects are initiated, planned, executed, monitored, controlled, and closed within an organization. In a multi-project environment, OPM involves continuous management of the project portfolio, ensuring efficient use of resources and confirming that the organization is “doing the right things.”

Leadership style and associated organizational culture influence OPM effectiveness. Companies choosing to adopt a needs-focused leadership approach in their projects aim to enhance both individual project outcomes and overall project portfolio performance. To successfully implement this approach, individuals responsible for organizational project management must understand not only the benefits and positive effects of needs-focused leadership but also practical strategies for embedding it into organizational practices.

Integrating Needs-Focused Leadership into Project Governance Standards

Governance standards and processes fundamentally shape how projects are executed within an organization. When introducing a needs-focused leadership approach, companies should integrate the MOVE models into existing governance frameworks in several key ways:

•	Project Management Standards and Processes: The MOVE Process Model can be embedded across various stages of the project lifecycle. Traditional planning processes—usually focused on scope, schedule, and resources—can incorporate explicit emotional frontloading activities. Similarly, regular project reviews or formal stage-gate evaluations can include emotional check-ins and check-outs, generating emotional lessons learned for future initiatives.
•	Skill and Competency Frameworks: Existing competency frameworks for staffing and developing project managers can be expanded by integrating the MOVE Competency Model, tailored to align with the organization’s specific leadership expectations and values.
•	Organizational Body of Knowledge: Companies can develop or enhance an internal repository of best practices, including effective leadership behaviors and needs-focused methodologies, supporting consistent application and continuous improvement of leadership competencies.
•	Operational Guides and Tools: Practical project management guides and operational manuals may include concrete recommendations for applying needs-focused leadership, translating competencies into everyday practice.

In addition to internal governance frameworks, established industry standards such as the Project Management Institute’s Project Management Body of Knowledge (PMBoK Guide) and its associated practice guides, models like PMI’s Talent Triangle, or competency-based frameworks such as IPMA’s Individual Competence Baseline (ICB) could also benefit from integrating the MOVE Process and Competency Models. 

Organizational-level models such as the PMO Value Ring—focused on value-oriented PMO design and service delivery—and PMI’s 2024 PMO Practice Guide—emphasizing customer-centricity, adaptability, and strategic alignment—may likewise be enriched by incorporating needs-focused elements. Especially in areas like stakeholder engagement, service prioritization, and PMO maturity development, a needs-based perspective adds human-centered depth to value generation and reinforces the PMO’s role as an enabler of emotionally intelligent leadership and sustainable collaboration. 

Extending these cross-industry standards with needs-focused elements would enhance their maturity, applicability, and effectiveness, further elevating leadership practices across various organizational and project contexts.

Continuously refining these internal and external standards and guidelines ensures ongoing evolution and maturity of project management capabilities, ultimately strengthening overall organizational performance.

Project Management Offices and Needs-Focused Leadership

Project Management Offices (PMOs) play a crucial role in shaping how projects are executed within an organization. They define governance frameworks and processes, set standards, and clarify expectations regarding the mindsets and skills required from effective project managers. In some organizations, PMOs also maintain a pool of internal project managers, similar to an HR-managed pool of professionals, who are assigned to projects as needed.

Consequently, establishing a needs-focused leadership approach within an organization depends on the PMO professionals’ understanding of this leadership style. PMOs need to clearly identify the specific skillsets, mindsets, and personality traits—outlined in the MOVE Competency Model—required to authentically implement and embody needs-focused behaviors.

Like Organizational Project Management itself, PMOs typically have a certain maturity level, with an ongoing ambition to continually improve their capabilities. Such improvements include increasing their knowledge and understanding of effective project execution, as well as supporting projects in operationalizing new or innovative leadership approaches. In this capacity, PMOs function similarly to mentors, guiding project leaders to effectively adopt and apply a needs-focused leadership style.

Therefore, PMO leaders themselves must thoroughly grasp and champion the concept of needs-focused leadership, as their influence directly affects how effectively and authentically this approach is integrated and practiced throughout the organization.

The Strategic Role of Human Resources in Needs-Focused Leadership

The Human Resources (HR) department plays a critical and strategic role in the successful implementation and ongoing application of needs-focused leadership within an organization. HR’s strategic influence spans multiple areas:

•	Supervision: HR might directly or indirectly provide supervision services to project leaders and teams. Supervision enables leaders and team members to develop and grow in a structured, sustainable manner, benefiting from this type of external development support.
•	Recruiting and Staffing: HR professionals are deeply involved—and sometimes the primary decision-makers—in recruitment alongside functional managers. Their selection decisions strongly shape the type of leaders hired and assigned as project managers, directly influencing the presence of needs-focused leadership competencies within the organization.
•	Learning and Development: HR designs and implements leadership development programs, training, and initiatives that support employees’ personal growth. They may also facilitate coaching, mentoring, or supervision. It’s important to note that according to the MOVE Competency Model, not all competencies can be developed solely through training—some traits necessitate thoughtful staffing, reassignment, or even outplacement decisions.
•	Target and Performance Management: Though challenging to directly quantify needs-focused leadership with conventional KPIs, effective leadership behaviors can be incorporated into performance management strategies. These evaluations may rely on stakeholder feedback, collected throughout or at the conclusion of projects, reinforcing and recognizing desired leadership behaviors.
•	Compensation, Rewards, and Recognition: Linked closely with performance management, HR may structure compensation, incentives, and recognition programs to reward visible and effective application of needs-focused leadership behaviors, thereby reinforcing their importance within the organizational culture.
•	Talent Management: HR actively identifies and supports high-performing employees who naturally embody needs-focused leadership principles, positioning them as internal role models. Conversely, individuals who consistently struggle to develop necessary leadership traits (as opposed to skills) may be candidates for reassignment or, as a final step, outplacement.
•	Organizational Development: HR frequently oversees or supports organizational development efforts, influencing the establishment of roles, competencies, and structures. This responsibility extends to defining project manager roles and supporting the strategic development of Project Management Offices (PMOs), emphasizing the necessary mindsets and skillsets as described in the MOVE models.
•	Corporate Culture Development: HR is instrumental in shaping the corporate culture—including leadership culture. While organizational culture evolves from existing conditions, HR helps define and guide the path toward a desired future state. Through this influence, HR can intentionally embed needs-focused leadership principles into the broader organizational culture.
•	Employer Branding: Closely related to corporate culture, HR defines and communicates the organization’s employer branding strategy and Employee Value Proposition (EVP). Explicitly integrating a needs-focused leadership approach into employer branding can enhance organizational attractiveness, particularly appealing to new generations of talent who increasingly prioritize empathetic and needs-centered leadership.
•	Organizational Change Management: HR often plays a role in the overarching organizational change management, being responsible implementing the change, driving it, develop change agents and so on. The MOVE model addresses human needs, which are similarly addressed in organizational change management, making this tool equally relevant for this environment.

Through these strategic roles, HR contributes to embedding needs-focused leadership across organizational practices, culture, and identity, positioning the company as a forward-thinking and attractive employer for future talent.

Embedding Needs-Focused Leadership into Organizational Culture

Each project tends to develop its own distinct cultural characteristics, especially when involving cross-unit, cross-country, or cross-cultural collaboration. Yet, projects never operate in isolation—they remain intrinsically connected to the broader organizational environment and its established culture. Introducing a needs-focused leadership style within projects, although beneficial on a project-specific level, can initially trigger skepticism or even resistance from stakeholders unfamiliar with human-centered approaches. For instance, functional leaders accustomed to traditional management styles might perceive such empathetic and relationship-oriented practices as signs of managerial weakness.

Therefore, it’s crucial to proactively identify and manage both threats and opportunities linked to adopting this leadership style. Possible conflicts arising from different leadership expectations must be carefully mitigated. Conversely, there is a opportunity for positively influencing overall corporate culture, contributing to the organization’s broader “corporate health.”

Senior leadership plays a decisive role in the successful implementation, application, and organizational acceptance of needs-focused leadership. Senior executives directly impact projects through participation in steering committees and indirectly through their influence on project sponsors, who often report directly to them. The deeper senior leaders understand and actively promote the benefits of needs-focused approaches, the greater the likelihood of successful adoption within individual projects.

Additionally, establishing and maintaining psychological safety and trust—critical preconditions for needs-focused leadership—is essential. The extent of psychological safety and trust embedded within the broader organizational culture directly affects the ability of project teams to achieve the necessary openness, authenticity, and mutual respect. An environment characterized by psychological safety and trust thus becomes both a foundational requirement and an enabler of a genuinely needs-focused leadership culture in projects.

ESG, the “Social” Element, and Needs-Focused Leadership

ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance) criteria are sustainability standards used to evaluate how responsibly a company operates. The “Social” (S) component specifically focuses on aspects such as occupational health and safety, diversity, employee well-being, and corporate social responsibility, aligning closely with initiatives like the UN Global Compact.

Implementing a needs-focused leadership approach within project management supports the ESG “Social” dimension. By emphasizing empathy, psychological safety, trust, and addressing individual stakeholder needs, this leadership style actively promotes organizational health, creating an inclusive and engaging work environment. As a result, it enhances employee satisfaction, strengthens workplace culture, and directly contributes to achieving social sustainability objectives.

 
1.4	MOVE Process Model

A somewhat circular process approach, as seen in models like 

•	PDCA (Plan – Do – Check – Act) (EABPM, 2014, pp. 69-76)
•	Six Sigma: DMAIC (Define, Measure, Analyze, Improve, Control) (ASQ, 2025)
•	Appreciative Inquiry (Discovery – Dream – Design – Destiny) (Cooperrider, et al., 2008, pp. 5-7)
•	ADKAR (Awareness – Desire – Knowledge – Ability – Reinforcement) (Hiatt, 2006, pp. 2-3)

offers several benefits. It promotes continuous improvement by allowing for regular feedback and adjustments. This approach enhances flexibility and adaptability, making it easier to respond to changes and new information. It also fosters a culture of continuous learning and development, encouraging ongoing collaboration and communication. By repeatedly cycling through phases, it ensures that improvements are sustainable and aligned with evolving needs and goals.

Applying the MOVE Model effectively in project contexts requires a mindful integration of structured processes, interpersonal skills, and supportive tools, all aligned closely with the project’s governance framework and organizational culture.

A critical managerial competency is the ability to balance swift intervention with sufficient time for achieving sustainable resolutions. Leaders must respect each step of the process, resisting the temptation to prematurely move toward strategic solutions without adequate reflection. Reflective pauses between steps, allowing space for deeper consideration and specification of elements—such as individual positions, interests, values, emotions, and stimuli—contribute to the effectiveness of the intervention.

Ensuring emotional integrity throughout this process is fundamental, as emotional stability may fluctuate and should be continuously monitored. Authentic engagement and genuine exploration of interpersonal dynamics are essential, since superficial attempts at resolving conflicts can exacerbate existing tensions. Consequently, the mediation component of the MOVE Model requires the creation of safe, confidential environments. Mediators must actively guide individuals toward emotional openness and foster psychological safety, supported by a foundation of trust, aligning closely with principles described in the concept of “emotional frontloading.”

Both formal structured methods—such as personality profiling tools like PCM (Process Communication Model) and Spectrum of Needs, which serve as overarching supportive mechanisms—and informal interactions within dedicated reflective spaces enhance open communication. Regularly scheduled checkpoints—such as stage gates, retrospectives, or pulse-checks—provide structured opportunities for needs assessment, emotional check-ins, and strategic alignment.

Time allocation is particularly crucial, and sufficient time must be consciously provided to manage interpersonal dynamics effectively, while also respecting the inherent constraints of the project’s timeline. While typically conducted by a single project leader, the inclusion of external support through individual or group supervision should be considered. Supervision can contribute to personal development, team cohesion, and sustained effectiveness of the mediation and motivational processes.

Additionally, the project leader’s personality traits and competencies—outlined explicitly in the MOVE Competency Model—must be carefully considered during selection or staffing decisions. Traits that are inherently stable should influence leader selection, while skills that can be developed over time should inform training and personal growth plans. 

The MOVE Model presupposes a directive leadership approach by a single leader, complemented by sufficient flexibility to support varying degrees of self-organization within the team. Higher levels of self-organization imply greater process responsibility for team members, potentially affecting the leader’s mediation and motivational role, as explored in the section contrasting needs-focused leadership and servant leadership approaches.

Finally, the MOVE Model strongly recommends the thoughtful integration of AI as an emotional co-pilot to enhance personalized motivational support, provide neutral and unbiased mediation, simulate conflict scenarios, identify blind spots and biases, support tailored learning and professional growth, and offer personalized psychological assistance. Such AI integration, however, must always be implemented in a balanced and consciously managed manner, complementing rather than replacing human judgment and empathy.
 
1: Establish emotional integrity

Before the project manager can identify and analyze a team member’s needs—either to define motivational strategies or mediate conflicts effectively—he must first establish his own emotional integrity. This involves recognizing possible emotional gaps within himself  (Torbert, 2004, p. 39), and honestly assessing his current emotional state: Is he feeling balanced and well? Is he experiencing any signs of elevated stress? Are his own needs satisfied? Achieving emotional integrity, a state in which one’s own emotional triggers (“buttons”) remain unaffected by the present situation, is an essential precondition for addressing another person’s needs without becoming part of the conflict himself.

In this self-assessment, it is equally important to consider whether any experienced emotional reactions may have been absorbed from others. Emotions are contagious, especially in high-pressure environments. A project manager might unknowingly internalize another person’s frustration, fear, or stress, mistaking it for their own emotional state. Recognizing and filtering out such emotionally transmitted signals helps maintain clarity and neutrality.

In addition to emotional self-awareness, the project manager must also ensure there are no conflicts of interest that could compromise his objectivity. Unrecognized or unmanaged conflicts of interest may lead to unconscious bias or personal involvement in disputes, undermining the project manager’s role as a neutral and trustworthy mediator. This includes the possibility that the project manager is already a party in an emerging. Failing to recognize such entanglements early may erode trust and reduce the effectiveness of any intervention attempted later.

In this context, it is also valuable for the project manager to take a forward-looking perspective: Are there foreseeable dynamics or upcoming interactions that could challenge his emotional integrity or trigger future conflict? Identifying such potential threats in advance—especially those that could intimidate or destabilize one’s emotional balance—helps to proactively strengthen resilience and maintain neutrality under pressure.

This self-awareness also supports “Emotional Frontloading,” a concept explored further in a subsequent section, emphasizing proactive emotional transparency to build trust and psychological safety within teams. Additionally, needs assessment should ideally be reciprocal. Encouraging team members to reflect on and analyze the project manager’s communication style and personal needs fosters mutual understanding, empathy, and enhances team collaboration.

The integrity battery.

The author’s concept of the integrity battery envisions emotional stability and resilience as a mental energy source fueling our capacity to navigate life effectively. This “battery” represents the energy we draw upon to maintain emotional integrity throughout the day.

When we encounter experiences or thoughts that “push our buttons,” energy from the integrity battery is consumed to help preserve emotional balance and stability. This energy sustains us and enables us to extend understanding and compassion, both toward ourselves and others. However, maintaining emotional integrity is not a one-time task; it requires permanent monitoring, as our emotional states can fluctuate continually.

If the integrity battery runs low, our ability to empathize, adopt others’ perspectives, and respond compassionately diminishes. Once completely drained, we lose the capacity to connect with or fully understand the feelings and viewpoints of those around us. To mitigate this, the author recommends “reflective pausing,” allowing sufficient time for emotional recharging and enabling one to re-establish emotional integrity.

Regularly recharging the integrity battery is essential for emotional well-being. It involves addressing the underlying needs connected to our emotional triggers. Methods of self-affirmation—such as mindfulness practices, reflection, and other positive habits—replenish this energy source, restoring emotional stability and supporting meaningful interpersonal connections.

Additionally, using structured personality or needs profiles—such as the Process Communication Model (PCM) or a Needs Profile based on the Spectrum of Needs—can further enhance self-awareness, aid emotional self-assessment, and facilitate targeted interventions to maintain a healthy integrity battery level.

2: Identify needs

This step involves the project manager identifying important needs expressed by a team member. A crucial prerequisite is the ability to clearly differentiate between needs, feelings, and stimuli, actively differentiating and focusing to uncover the specific underlying needs beneath superficial expressions or behaviors. The core question here is: Which pronounced needs should be fulfilled to elicit positive emotions or foster motivation in the team member? It is essential to concentrate explicitly on underlying needs rather than on stimuli—the external factors influencing those needs—as multiple strategies and environments usually exist to fulfill any given need.

In this context, it is also important to consider that the emotion a team member expresses may not always originate from their own unmet need. Emotions are contagious—especially in closely interacting teams—and a person may unknowingly adopt the frustration, fear, or stress of others. This can lead to emotional expressions that reflect the group’s dynamic more than the individual’s internal state. Accurately identifying needs therefore requires sensitivity to these influences and openness to the possibility that a felt emotion may not point to a personal need, but to an absorbed one.

Pronounced needs can be identified through several complementary methods. A foundational prerequisite for all of them is active listening—a deliberate, attentive presence that allows the project manager to truly hear what is being said, notice what is left unsaid, and observe the emotional undercurrents behind verbal expressions. 

Equally important is the adaptation to the team member’s preferred communication style. This involves adjusting tone, pacing, level of directness, and even vocabulary to match how the other person feels most comfortable expressing themselves. In remote or hybrid settings, creating conditions that allow for emotionally attuned communication becomes especially important. Switching on cameras, using high-quality microphones, and complementing spoken dialogue with chat functions or written follow-ups can help minimize misunderstandings and support a more accurate identification of needs.

The following tools and approaches can then be applied in practice to help surface and specify needs:

•	Direct inquiry or educated guessing, ideally supported by framing, advocacy, and illustration.
•	Systematic questioning techniques, designed to methodically uncover deeper or unexpressed needs.
•	Circular questioning, which explores relationships, perceptions, and patterns by inviting perspective-taking (e.g., “What do you think your colleague would say about this situation?”), helping to uncover systemic influences and broaden understanding.
•	Using structured personality or needs profiling tools, such as the Process Communication Model (PCM) or a Needs Profile, to provide additional clarity.
•	Contextual analysis, which helps in understanding the situational environment, historical background, and project-specific factors that shape or influence team members’ needs. This includes checking for signs of the “Garbage Can Phenomenon,” where unresolved issues from past experiences or unrelated contexts may influence current expressions of need.
•	The doubling technique—empathetically speaking for participants—when employed sensitively and with explicit consent, especially in moments of emotional hesitation or difficulty articulating needs.
•	Reflective pausing, including the practice of “sleeping on it,” providing additional time and mental space for deeper insights and clearer specification of needs.
The practices of framing, advocating, and illustrating enhance the clarity and effectiveness of the identification process:

•	Framing explicitly defines the purpose, context, and shared assumptions of discussions. Clarifying the issue, stating goals clearly, and openly sharing one’s perspective prevents misunderstandings and aligns team members toward common objectives.
•	Advocating means explicitly expressing a need, perspective, or proposed course of action. Effective advocacy avoids vagueness and encourages constructive collaboration by clearly articulating personal or team needs.
•	Illustrating supports advocacy by providing concrete examples, context, and specifics, thus reducing ambiguity and potential misinterpretation. Effective communication emerges from balancing advocacy and illustration: advocacy without illustration risks misunderstanding, while illustration without advocacy lacks clear direction.

Using an inquiry—especially one based on an educated guess—is valuable, as it invites either confirmation or disconfirmation. Encouraging team members to disconfirm assumptions when necessary promotes genuine dialogue and facilitates the discovery of common ground and deeper, authentic needs.

(Torbert, 2004, pp. 28-30)

In addition to identifying current needs, project managers should also remain attentive to potential future tensions—such as needs that are not yet expressed but may become critical later. This includes observing early indicators of discomfort, withdrawal, or emotional overcontrol, which might signal a risk of future frustration or conflict. Taking this anticipatory stance helps prevent escalation and supports timely intervention before trust or motivation erode.

It is equally important to recognize that shy or emotionally reserved team members require empathetic validation and gentle encouragement to express themselves openly. The project manager should create a safe, empathetic space that allows every team member to share their true needs comfortably.

Furthermore, the project manager should identify and address overarching team-wide needs—such as clarity, orientation, direction, autonomy, and freedom—that foster resonance, emotional alignment, and meaningfulness within the team, all without overlooking the specific individual needs of each member.

3: Analyze needs

At this stage in the process, the project manager analyzes identified needs by examining key factors, such as:

•	Is the need connected to specific values and interests?
•	Which cultural factors influence how the need is understood and analyzed (considering cultural norms, values, patterns, and behaviors)?
•	What is the priority or degree of prominence of this need?
•	Is there an urgency associated with fulfilling this particular need?
•	Which identified needs might be competing or conflicting with other existing needs?
•	Is the resistance expressed by team members predominantly emotional or technical, as this differentiation offers distinct and targeted intervention strategies?

It is also important to clearly distinguish between the pronouncedness of a need—how strong or dominant it is in a person’s psychological structure—and its current level of fulfillment. A need may be highly pronounced (e.g., a strong need for autonomy or recognition) but currently well met, while another, less pronounced need may be severely unmet and cause disproportionate tension. Accurate analysis depends on differentiating these two dimensions.

During this stage, it is essential to check for possible biases or cognitive distortions—both in the project manager’s own interpretation and in the expressed views of stakeholders. Introducing reflective pauses and peer dialogue can help surface such distortions and correct course early.

To deepen understanding and promote transparency, systems thinking should also be made visible during the analysis phase. By mapping root causes, identifying interdependencies, and visually communicating systemic reasoning—through tools like causal (root-cause) diagrams or influence maps—the project manager not only sharpens their own diagnostic accuracy but also helps team members and experts appreciate the complexity of leadership decisions. 

As this process step involves exploring potential psychological dimensions, it should also include a check for indicators of mental health concerns. Assessing the team member’s current stress level can offer early clues about emotional strain, cognitive overload, or deeper unmet needs. This analytical stage is particularly well-suited to identify situations where issues may exceed standard leadership competencies and require the involvement of trained professionals. When certain signals emerge during the needs analysis, recognizing the need for external mental health support becomes a logical and responsible extension of the process.

Especially if structured tools (such as a PCM profile or a Needs Profile) have generated relevant data, Artificial Intelligence can be employed to further analyze this information, providing deeper insights into underlying patterns and priorities.

Moreover, recognizing the limits of internal mediation is essential. Explicit criteria and signals have been established to identify when external mediation becomes necessary. These criteria include:

•	Neutrality: The project manager becomes personally involved or demonstrates bias.
•	Escalation: Conflicts escalate into hostility or frequent emotional outbursts (see Glasl’s levels of escalation).
•	Repeated failures: Previous mediation efforts have not resulted in sustainable solutions.
•	Trust issues: Team members express distrust or question the credibility of the manager.
•	Complexity: The conflict involves deeper underlying interpersonal or psychological issues.
•	Manager involvement: The manager is directly involved or experiences role conflicts.
•	Repercussions: Team members fear retaliation or have concerns regarding confidentiality.
•	Resource drain: The mediation process excessively distracts the manager, impairing team productivity.

4: Define strategies

The fourth step involves defining specific actions to actively engage and motivate the team member, carefully translating identified needs into clear and structured strategies.

These strategies can be categorized into two types:

•	Internal elements: These include thoughts, shared values (which may be explicitly captured in a team charter), and other positive affirmations that strengthen emotional engagement and intrinsic motivation.
•	External elements: These involve shaping or adjusting environmental conditions, creating favorable situations, or defining concrete activities and actionable tasks.

When developing strategies, it is important not to rush into solutions prematurely, even under pressure. Remembering the value of deliberate pausing is crucial. Purposeful pauses allow deeper reflection, preventing superficial solutions and enhancing the sustainability of chosen strategies.

Additionally, conflicts should not always be viewed negatively; rather, they can be opportunities. Properly identified and managed conflicts can foster innovation, strengthen team cohesion, and boost motivation. Such productive disagreements often surface previously unheard or undervalued ideas, unlocking new potentials and opportunities.

In the case of conflict, it is helpful to aim for a meaningful compromise or a win-win outcome—keeping in mind that emotional validation itself can be part of that “win,” as it triggers positive emotions and fosters emotional relief. If the conflict has led to estrangement or diverging paths, strategies can also focus on restoring emotional alignment, for example by referencing shared positive experiences (“the good times”) and reactivating what once created connection. Reminding each other of past alignment not only taps into emotional memory but also creates a bridge for future collaboration.

An essential aspect of strategy definition is empowering and inspiring team members toward self-help, supporting them to independently discover solutions, even in conflict situations. The project manager acts as a coach, facilitating team members’ capabilities for self-guided conflict resolution and personal growth.

In all cases, the universally effective approach, termed by the author as “Strategy Zero,” should be considered as a foundational step. Strategy Zero involves simply acknowledging and emotionally validating the unmet needs. This validation alone reduces stress and negative emotions, immediately contributing to emotional relief, even before concrete solutions are proposed.

It is important to note that a needs-focused approach does not mean that every need must be met. In practice, some needs may remain partially or temporarily unfulfilled. What matters is how these unmet needs are handled. When individuals feel emotionally validated and seen—despite a lack of immediate resolution—they are more likely to stay engaged and emotionally stable. This ability to “hold” unmet needs without rushing to fix them is a key component of emotionally mature leadership.

Finally, using structured profiles such as the Process Communication Model (PCM) or a Needs Profile can greatly enhance the precision and effectiveness of the developed strategies. These tools provide clarity on individual preferences and emotional patterns, allowing tailored, needs-based interventions.

5: Sustain the momentum

Factors influencing the extent to which team members’ needs are met evolve continuously, particularly when changes occur within the project or its external environment. Therefore, it is essential to regularly reassess how satisfied or motivated team members remain and identify any new developments or shifting circumstances.

Implementing regular “pulse-checks,” structured retrospectives, and dedicated “empathy checkpoints” ensures sustained attention to evolving team needs, fostering continuous improvement and enabling timely adjustments. These checkpoints must be genuine and authentically conducted—any institutionalized processes should always reflect sincere interest and meaningful engagement, rather than superficial routines.

Additionally, integrating these reflective processes strategically with specific points in the project lifecycle—such as routine retrospectives, stage-gate meetings, or phase-end reviews—and aligning them carefully with existing project management frameworks will further support consistent and effective monitoring of emotional integrity and needs satisfaction. Whenever adjustments are required, the cycle can be revisited from step one: establishing emotional integrity. 

1.4.2	AI as a Project Companion

In the context of a specific project, AI can act as a real-time companion for both project managers and team members, enhancing collaboration and preventing interpersonal friction before it escalates. By analyzing team dynamics, communication patterns, and individual behavior profiles, AI can generate hyper-personalized strategies, proposing targeted actions, adjustments in team routines, or communication methods tailored to each person’s identified needs. This creates a leadership environment that feels more responsive, empathetic, and effective.

When tensions arise, AI can assist in balancing competing needs by identifying overlapping or conflicting interests among team members. Through pattern recognition and contextual analysis, it can suggest integrative solutions rooted in shared values or complementary goals—thus supporting the project manager in addressing tensions without taking sides.

AI-powered facilitation tools, especially integrated into chat or meeting platforms, help keep discussions structured and focused. They can detect inflammatory language, recurring misunderstandings, or communication breakdowns, ensuring that the tone remains respectful and the conversation aligned with core needs.

Thanks to its neutral perspective, AI can evaluate input from multiple sides without emotional bias, surfacing potential areas of agreement and offering real-time prompts to de-escalate emotionally charged exchanges. This is particularly valuable in complex or sensitive stakeholder settings, where human facilitators might be perceived as partial.

Beyond immediate facilitation, AI can deliver predictive insights and simulation, flagging subtle early-warning signals that point to mounting tension or unresolved issues. By simulating different conflict trajectories and mapping potential stakeholder responses, it enables proactive intervention—well before minor issues become major barriers to project success.
 

1.5	MOVE Competency Model

The model has three main components. Each components consists of a set of competencies.

1. System-Related & Execution Elements:
Creating Clear Paths to Action (Turning needs into structured steps)
Visionary & Growth-Oriented Leadership (Strategic alignment, ethical growth, innovation, and future-oriented team development)
Systems-Oriented Problem Solving (Seeing connections and root causes)

2. Relational Competence:
Service-Oriented Leadership (Prioritizing team members' individual needs)
Facilitating Open Dialogue and Feedback (Two-way communication and transparency)
Assertiveness (Respectfully expressing personal and team needs)
Attentive and Empathetic Listening (Active, respectful understanding of concerns)

3. Personal Mastery: 
Impartial Thinking (Objectivity and fairness in decisions)
Emotional Balance (Resilience, self-awareness, calm under stress)
Identifying & Managing Personal Triggers (Self-reflection, mindfulness to stay neutral)

Identifying and managing personal triggers involves proactive self-reflection and mindfulness, enabling leaders to remain neutral and composed, even in challenging or emotionally charged situations. This skill is grounded in structured reflection, mindful awareness, and early detection of emotional cues—both internally and from others’ body language—facilitating timely intervention and self-management.

Leaders practicing this approach openly recognize, understand, and learn from their personal weaknesses and challenging experiences, viewing conflicts positively as opportunities for growth, creativity, innovation, and enhanced team cohesion. With a genuine willingness to learn, adapt, and maintain humility about their limitations, they leverage tools like personality assessments (e.g., PCM) to deepen self-awareness and inform reflective growth.

The competency element “Identifying and Managing Personal Triggers” incorporates theoretical concepts such as Self-Affirmation (Steele), mindfulness practices described by Kabat-Zinn—particularly focusing on aspects like Acceptance and Letting Go—and executive functions including Frustration Tolerance and Impulse Control.

Emotional balance encompasses maintaining resilience, self-awareness, and composure in stressful or ambiguous project contexts. Leaders practicing emotional balance demonstrate emotional stability, actively managing emotions and impulses through tailored regulation techniques, particularly in high-pressure scenarios. Central to this skillset are emotional intelligence (EQ), explicit self-awareness, and emotional adaptability—including sensitivity toward neurodiversity and a proactive understanding of mental health.

This capability includes tolerance for uncertainty, adaptability, and a positive mindset that reframes conflict as an opportunity for growth, creativity, innovation, and strengthened team cohesion. Integral to emotional balance is competence in Mental Health First Aid (MHFA), including awareness of mental health challenges, recognizing signs of emotional distress in oneself and team members, and knowing appropriate immediate responses. Leaders should be capable of providing empathetic initial support while clearly understanding when to involve professional mental health resources. Protecting personal emotional boundaries while supporting others is equally vital.

Mindful emotion management, patience, and humor sensitivity—leveraging humor constructively without compromising psychological safety—further support emotional integrity and mental health awareness within the team.

Ultimately, emotionally balanced leaders foster authentic human connections, embrace failure positively, and use intuition wisely, cultivating an environment where emotional resilience, optimism, and mental health are actively supported, sustainably enhancing individual and collective performance.

The competency element “Emotional Balance” aligns closely with Masten’s resilience elements, as well as key aspects of mindfulness practices such as Patience, Beginner’s Mind, Trust, and a Non-striving approach.

Impartial thinking emphasizes objectivity, neutrality, and fairness in leadership decision-making processes, ensuring inclusive collaboration and equitable treatment across diverse teams. Leaders employing this skill effectively balance their responsibilities for impartial mediation with the pragmatic demands of project delivery, practicing explicit neutrality techniques to transparently disclose and manage personal biases or conflicts.

Grounded in ethical awareness, integrity, humility, and honest self-reflection, this competency extends to cultural awareness, intercultural cooperation, generational diversity, and neurodiversity sensitivity. Leaders remain unbiased, non-judgmental, and respectful toward diverse mindsets, consciously avoiding stereotyping or generalizations. They demonstrate political intelligence through balanced judgments, making explicit ethical decisions and practicing ethical stewardship of organizational resources.

Ultimately, impartial thinkers foster environments characterized by managed neutrality, inclusivity, and fairness, where diverse perspectives are valued and integrated into decisions that benefit both individuals and the broader organizational context.

The competency element “Impartial Thinking” is closely associated with theoretical frameworks such as Mindfulness—particularly the practices of cognitive debiasing and non-judging—as well as with aspects of executive functioning like changing perspectives. Additionally, it aligns with Balanced Leadership, a sub-component of the overarching concept of Servant Leadership, which emphasizes balancing opposing forces or interests.

Attentive and empathetic listening centers on authentically engaging with others to genuinely understand their concerns, feelings, and perspectives. This involves active and reflective listening, characterized by genuine curiosity, sincere interest, and the explicit ability to remain quiet and fully absorb what is communicated. Authentic empathy underpins this skill, helping leaders read emotional cues accurately, regulate emotional responses thoughtfully, and foster meaningful human connections.

Applying principles of Nonviolent Communication (NVC), empathetic listening emphasizes respectful understanding without immediate judgment or response. By adhering to Covey’s principle—seeking first to understand before being understood—leaders cultivate trust and open dialogue, creating an environment where individuals feel seen, heard, and genuinely valued.

The competency element “Attentive and Empathetic Listening” is enriched by its relation to theoretical models such as Schulz von Thun’s “Four Sides of a Message”, various types (loops) of feedback, and profiling tools like the PCM Profile or the Needs Profile.

Assertiveness involves clearly and respectfully expressing personal and team needs, ensuring these are transparently communicated and adequately addressed. It requires a combination of visibility and authenticity, enabling leaders to speak openly about expectations and boundaries while remaining genuine and approachable. This transparency is supported by accountability and self-activation, empowering individuals to take ownership of their positions and actively engage in productive dialogue.

At its core, assertiveness entails balanced risk-taking and courage, enabling leaders and team members alike to address sensitive topics confidently, directly, and constructively. Incorporating principles of Nonviolent Communication (NVC), assertiveness focuses on articulating needs respectfully and empathetically, fostering healthier, more collaborative relationships within the team and beyond.

The competency “Assertiveness” is closely linked to theoretical frameworks such as the Thomas-Kilmann Conflict Mode Instrument (TKI) and is integral to effectively applying Nonviolent Communication (NVC).

Facilitating open dialogue and feedback involves proactively establishing an environment of psychological safety, enabling transparent, two-way communication among team members. Leaders adopting this approach see themselves explicitly as mediators and facilitators, utilizing structured mediation and moderation techniques to support team learning and collaborative decision-making processes.

Clear, adaptive communication—including tonal awareness, non-verbal cues, and context-sensitive adjustments—is fundamental, complemented by a high degree of rhetorical intelligence. This openness and transparency encourage authentic engagement, shared humor, and enjoyment within the team, further strengthening internal networking and relationship-building skills.

Ultimately, by consistently fostering open channels for feedback and dialogue, leaders reinforce trust and psychological safety, empowering team members to contribute openly and constructively to both individual and collective goals.

The competency “Facilitating Open Dialogue and Feedback” builds upon the foundational concept of Psychological Safety and relates closely to theoretical perspectives on humor, which foster dialogue at eye level, reduce tension and stress, and positively enhance bonding within teams.

Service-Oriented leadership prioritizes the individual needs of team members, fostering a supportive, empowering, and collaborative environment that balances personal well-being with overarching project objectives. Leaders adopting this approach demonstrate role agility, flexibly adapting between horizontal leadership practices—such as servant or facilitative leadership—and result-oriented responsibilities.

Core practices include actively encouraging autonomy, meaningful contributions, and self-organized work styles, thus avoiding micromanagement. Leaders provide psychological and spatial safety, recognizing and accommodating individual professional and personal circumstances, and promoting emotional security and stability. By leveraging coaching, teaching, and positive psychology, they encourage personal growth, self-help through peer resolution, and professional development, while also rewarding achievement and recognizing individual strengths explicitly.

Ultimately, service-oriented leaders build trust relationships, team spirit, and high-performing teams by leading authentically by example, motivating with genuine enthusiasm, and ensuring active engagement, empowerment, and meaningful career progression for all team members.

The competency “Service-Oriented Leadership” closely aligns with the established leadership model known as “Servant Leadership,” emphasizing the importance of addressing individual values, motives, needs, knowledge, and skills, as well as respecting the reciprocity between leader and team.

Systems-oriented problem solving involves a holistic approach that emphasizes identifying connections and root causes within complex project environments. By aligning project goals with broader organizational strategy, leaders practice strategic decision-making that carefully balances immediate operational demands with deeper relational needs. Central to this capability are analytical skills and structured problem-solving frameworks that support comprehensive assessment of information, thoughtful input gathering, and informed decision-making processes.

This skillset relies on systemic thinking and situational awareness to understand potential challenges thoroughly and anticipate resistance, drawing on organizational change management expertise to navigate structural issues effectively. Coupled with curiosity, creativity, and a willingness to encourage innovation, leaders adopt an adaptive practical intelligence—often referred to as “horse sense”—which enables pragmatic flexibility and improvisation, grounded in mastery of foundational methodologies.

Moreover, being AI-savvy yet critically reflective of technological interventions underpins this skill. Leaders must continuously evaluate the appropriateness and human-centered relevance of digital tools, ensuring technology complements rather than complicates solutions. Ultimately, this integrative mindset of big-picture thinking ensures decisions are both strategically sound and practically effective.

The competency “Systems-Oriented Problem Solving” strongly relates to Senge’s Systems Thinking concepts, particularly through elements such as Mental Models and Team Learning. Additionally, it incorporates executive functions like Reflecting on Proceedings, Taking Decisions, Creative Thinking, and Logical and Strategic Thinking. Artificial Intelligence further enriches this competency, as it inherently employs a systems-oriented approach in problem-solving processes.

Visionary and growth-oriented leadership involves strategically aligning team efforts with clear, purpose-driven objectives, explicitly connecting day-to-day activities to meaningful, long-term organizational goals. Leaders adopting this mindset practice patience and strategic decision-making, ensuring their vision integrates seamlessly with organizational strategy and ethical values. They foster a culture of accountability and stewardship, actively encouraging innovation and creativity that directly contributes to sustainable growth.

Central to this approach is a strong commitment to continuous professional and personal development, supported by strategic networking that builds long-term partnerships and opportunities. By prioritizing future-oriented team and organizational development, visionary leaders cultivate an environment in which individuals and teams are inspired and empowered to achieve meaningful, ethically aligned outcomes. 

Trust serves as the foundational basis for the competency “Visionary and Growth-Oriented Leadership,” linking it closely to theoretical frameworks related to trust and psychological safety. Additionally, Senge’s concept of “Building a Shared Vision” adds a systems-oriented dimension, complemented by cultivating a Beginner’s Mind—as described by Kabat-Zinn—which emphasizes openness to learning, innovation, and collaborative vision-building.

Creating clear paths to action means systematically transforming team and stakeholder needs into well-defined, structured steps, ensuring clarity and alignment around shared objectives. By explicitly articulating intentions, expectations, and responsibilities, leaders provide strategic and structural support, which reduces misalignments related to goals, roles, processes, and expectations.

This approach emphasizes reliable accountability, ensuring that responsibilities are transparently assigned through effective delegation and outcome-oriented leadership. Clear action plans, combined with structured processes, help project managers prioritize tasks and balance immediate operational pressures against the necessity for sustainable solutions and conflict resolution.

At its core, this skillset promotes clarity, reliability, and strategic prioritization through structured time management practices. By maintaining a vision- and purpose-oriented perspective, leaders foster alignment within the team and organization, enabling consistent, effective action toward defined goals and outcomes.

The competency “Creating Clear Paths to Action” aligns closely with Senge’s systems-oriented concepts, particularly Personal Mastery and Continuous Improvement. Additionally, it incorporates executive functions such as Setting Targets and Acting on a Plan, Deploying Attention, and Retentivity.
 

What matters most?

9 out of the 10 competency elements were subject to the 4th hypothesis of this thesis, with people involved in projects (in different roles) asked to rank them. 

The weighting of the stakeholder perspectives in evaluating the skills and traits for needs-focused leadership is set to emphasize an operational focus. Therefore, the ratings provided by Project Managers are given the highest weighting (45%), as they hold primary responsibility for daily project execution, directly influencing team dynamics, motivation, and operational efficiency (Yang, et al., 2011). Project Sponsors receive the second-highest weighting (30%), reflecting their strategic oversight and critical role in aligning leadership effectiveness with organizational objectives and long-term goals (PMI, 2018). Finally, Project Team Members are weighted at 25%, acknowledging the importance of their direct experience with leadership behaviors, yet recognizing the more subjective and individually varied nature of their assessments (Conway & Huffcutt, 1997)

It is important to note that the competency elements, and even more their components, differ in terms of their developmental timelines. Some components, understood as “skills,” are relatively straightforward to cultivate and thus particularly suitable for targeted personal development and training. In contrast, other components require extensive time and effort to develop—often exceeding the duration of a project or even an individual’s tenure—making them crucial considerations in recruitment and selection processes.

Special attention should be given to technically oriented project managers who lack these essential traits. In such cases, deliberately evaluating whether to staff the project with a more suitable candidate is advisable. Furthermore, organizations might benefit from reconsidering the general roles and responsibilities assigned to individuals whose profiles strongly emphasize technical expertise at the expense of critical interpersonal or leadership competencies.

It is important to emphasize that ranking competencies by priority does not imply that an organization can or should sacrifice one skill or trait in favor of another. As repeatedly highlighted throughout the interviews and survey responses—and further underscored by the consistently small effect sizes observed in the ranking analyses—all competencies can be considered equally important in practice. The prioritization primarily serves to design and implement tailored development strategies, while distinguishing between traits and skills supports targeted efforts and effective resource allocation in individual and organizational development initiatives.

Using AI to Continuously Develop Interpersonal Skills

In the evolving landscape of leadership and project work, interpersonal skills are no longer a static asset but a dynamic capability requiring regular reflection, feedback, and growth. AI-based tools can serve as powerful partners in this developmental journey, offering structured, personalized, and responsive support.

By integrating regular self-assessment sessions—daily, weekly, or after key project milestones—AI tools can function like a digital reflective journal. These check-ins help leaders stay emotionally aware, recognize emerging patterns in their interactions, and track progress over time. They reinforce momentum in both motivation and mediation, supporting long-term development rather than one-off improvements.

AI can help leaders strengthen mindful decision-making by identifying potential cognitive biases—such as confirmation bias, anchoring, or overconfidence—in written communications, meeting summaries, or decision logs. These real-time nudges prompt deeper reflection and encourage leaders to re-evaluate assumptions, integrate alternative perspectives, and make more balanced decisions.

Targeted growth requires targeted input. AI tools can analyze a leader’s behavior patterns or learning history and curate relevant materials, including short articles, real-world case studies, or micro-courses. Whether the focus is on de-escalating conflicts, enhancing emotional intelligence, or embracing servant leadership, the AI ensures that learning remains context-relevant, timely, and aligned with specific interpersonal development goals.

Needs-focused Leaders = Servant Leaders?

Several interviewees and literature suggest overlaps between needs-focused leadership and Servant Leadership. However, setting aside Greenleaf’s original concept and related frameworks, the author associates the term “servant” primarily with a fixed role—implying a static rather than adaptive mindset—and the notion of exclusively serving others’ needs.

A key distinction highlighted in this thesis is the importance of self-empathy: genuine empathy and effective mediation begin by establishing one’s own emotional integrity. Much like the safety instruction in commercial aviation—to put on one’s own oxygen mask first before assisting others—leaders must first assess their emotional state, manage personal triggers, and ensure internal stability before effectively addressing others’ needs. This self-awareness helps avoid unintentionally becoming part of conflicts and aligns closely with assertiveness, balancing everyone’s needs, including the leader’s own.


Another crucial point is that not all needs must or can be fully satisfied. Often, explicit acknowledgment and emotional validation alone can calm tensions. While “servant” might imply a mandate to fully meet others’ needs, a “needs-focused” approach explicitly includes emotional validation, clearly distinguishing between empathy and immediate solutions.

Yet, a critical boundary remains: extensive focus on individual needs risks blurring the line between leadership and therapeutic roles (“red-couch scenarios”). Clearly identifying and respecting these boundaries is itself empathetic, protecting the leader’s emotional integrity and keeping the focus within the project’s scope.

The author deliberated between the terms “needs-oriented” and “needs-focused,” ultimately choosing “needs-focused.” While “orientation” suggests an attitude or mindset, “focus” implies deliberate practice, involving nuanced skills such as differentiating stimuli, identifying triggered judgments, interests, values, and underlying needs, and managing resulting emotions effectively.
 

1.1	MOVE Trust Model (trust tripod)

Trust develops gradually through demonstrated reliability, authenticity, transparency, and consistent alignment between words and actions. Trust, in turn, reinforces psychological safety, openness, and collaboration, directly influencing motivation and ensuring sustained project success. Given the inherent fragility of trust, any breaches of integrity can swiftly erode it, highlighting the importance of continuous, intentional actions to maintain and deepen relational trust among team members.

Grounded in the theoretical work of researchers like Amy Edmondson, trust and psychological safety are overarching themes throughout this thesis. They are embedded both explicitly and implicitly in procedural elements—such as emotional frontloading—and in the empathetic, secure execution of the process and its individual components. These concepts also underpin key competencies from the MOVE Competency Model, including Facilitating Open Dialogue and Feedback, Attentive and Empathetic Listening, Service-Oriented Leadership, and Visionary and Growth-Oriented Leadership. Rather than isolated states, trust and psychological safety serve as foundational principles.

The critical link between trust and psychological safety emerged consistently across the qualitative interviews and literature review. These insights informed the development of the MOVE Trust Model, which integrates academic research with empirical findings. Trust acts as the essential “glue” connecting needs-focused behaviors from the process model with the proactive leadership roles defined in the competency model.

The model was developed by analyzing trust-related statements from interview transcripts, clustering them thematically, and examining their underlying logic. These empirically derived insights were then validated and enriched through relevant academic research on trust (Mayer, et al., 1995; Kramer & Tyler, 1996, pp. 116-117; 265-269; Schoorman, et al., 2007). (Mayer, et al., 1995) (Kramer & Tyler, 1996, pp. 116-117; 265-269) (Schoorman, et al., 2007) 
The resulting comprehensive synthesis prioritized frequently emphasized trust-building elements from both empirical and theoretical sources. To ensure practical applicability, the model specifically focuses on active input factors—attitudes and behaviors that individuals can consciously adopt—rather than outcomes or static conditions. This final refinement underscores the model’s robustness, clarity, and real-world usability.

The trust model works like a tripod table, that needs three equally strong and stable legs: Openness, Reliability, and Good Faith.

OPENNESS:
Transparent communication
Open expression of one’s thoughts and feelings
Acceptance of others’ perspectives
Proactively addressing difficult topics
Active, unbiased listening

RELIABILITY:
Integrity (alignment of words and actions)
Keeping agreements and commitments
Predictable and consistent behavior
Creating reliable structures and frameworks
Constructive handling of mistakes

GOOD FAITH: 
Offering trust proactively (“trust first”)
Positive mindset towards others
Genuine concern for others’ well-being
Willingness to show vulnerability and accept potential disappointment
Initiating shared positive experiences
