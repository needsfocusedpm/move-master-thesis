# 13 Methodological and Conceptual Limitations

Source type: Master’s Thesis  
Author: Daniel Hendling

---

•	Gender Distribution: Nearly two-thirds of participants identified as male (64.5%), potentially limiting the generalizability of results and underrepresenting the perspectives of female and non-binary professionals.
•	Experience Level: Participants were predominantly experienced project professionals, with nearly half (46.3%) reporting over 20 years of project experience. This focus on highly experienced individuals may limit the applicability of findings to younger or less experienced professionals.
•	Role Distribution: The sample showed an uneven distribution across project roles, with project managers forming the majority (52.0%). The dominance of this role could bias results, as project team members (22.9%) and sponsors or higher-level decision-makers (25.2%) are comparatively underrepresented, despite possibly differing perspectives.
•	Age Distribution: The average age of respondents was approximately 47 years, indicating a relatively mature participant base. Thus, findings may not fully reflect the attitudes or perceptions of younger project professionals or emerging generations within the field.
•	Sampling Method: The use of convenience sampling via the author’s professional networks further limits generalizability. This non-probability sampling approach may result in participants sharing similar characteristics, experiences, or attitudes, which might not accurately represent the broader population of project management professionals.
•	Researcher Bias: There is a potential risk of researcher bias, including confirmation bias, due to the author’s prior experiences, personal perspectives, and expectations regarding needs-focused leadership. This bias may have inadvertently influenced survey design, question phrasing, or interpretation of results.
•	Language Bias: The survey was conducted in English, while many participants are not native English speakers. Language barriers may have influenced their understanding of survey items and potentially affected the accuracy and reliability of responses.
•	Complexity and Phrasing of Questions: Individual feedback highlighted potential issues regarding the complexity or phrasing of certain survey questions. This might have led to misunderstandings or misinterpretations of survey items, potentially affecting the accuracy of responses.
•	Ranking Question Limitations: Several participants indicated that ranking the leadership aspects was challenging, stating either that “all aspects were equally important and none should be ignored,” or that “the ranking depends heavily on context.” This suggests that a forced ranking may not fully capture participants’ nuanced views and may limit the interpretability of the ranking results.
•	Order Effects: The nine ranking items were presented in a fixed order rather than randomized. Non-randomization could have led to order effects, where participants’ responses are influenced by the positioning of items.
•	Lack of Psychometric Validation: The items used in the survey were developed based on the author’s expertise and literature knowledge but were not formally validated through established psychometric procedures (e.g., factor analysis, reliability and validity testing). Thus, the survey items may not meet formal psychometric standards, potentially affecting the robustness and interpretability of the results.
•	Small to Moderate Effect Sizes: The observed statistically significant effects, while meaningful, were generally small to moderate in magnitude. Thus, despite statistical significance, the practical impact or real-world relevance of these findings may be limited and should be interpreted cautiously.
•	Lack of Group Differentiation: The exploratory nature of the analysis did not support the identification of statistically significant participant subgroups. As a result, all item responses were interpreted as existing along a continuum without clear clustering or group-based distinctions. This limits the ability to draw conclusions about how different stakeholder groups may perceive or prioritize leadership elements differently.
•	Use of AI Tools: AI-supported transcription and analysis may have introduced minor inaccuracies. However, the resulting efficiency and depth of insight contributed positively to the overall quality of the research.
•	Geographical Distribution in Qualitative Sample: The interviewees were internationally distributed, covering a range of countries across Europe, North and South America, Africa, Asia, and Oceania. However, despite this global reach, the selection was not evenly balanced across regions, with a stronger representation from Europe and Western countries. As the sample was selected purposively from the author’s professional network, the perspectives may reflect culturally similar contexts and leadership understandings, potentially limiting the generalizability to other cultural or regional settings.
•	Geographical Distribution in Quantitative Sample: The survey included participants from 74 different countries, reflecting a broad international reach. However, the sample is heavily skewed toward certain regions, particularly Austria (41.7% of valid responses) and Germany (12.4%), which together account for over half of all valid responses. This regional concentration may limit the representativeness of global perspectives and cultural diversity, especially from underrepresented areas such as Africa, South America, and parts of Asia.
