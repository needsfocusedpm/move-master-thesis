Thesis Abstract

Projects inherently involve constraints, notably in human aspects such as leadership, motivation, and conflict mediation. Despite the increasing recognition and expectation of people-oriented leadership, many project managers continue to struggle in applying a truly human-centered approach. Common issues include an inadequate awareness of the connection between motivation and conflict resolution, insufficiently developed personal mindset or professional skillset for addressing stakeholder needs, limited allocation of time for deep stakeholder engagement, an overreliance on hierarchical, technical management styles, superficial handling of conflicts, and skepticism towards approaches like Nonviolent Communication due to perceived gaps in scientific validation. Additionally, organizational cultures often impede the integration of needs-focused leadership into existing standards and processes.

To address these challenges, this thesis develops and presents three interrelated models: a Process Model, guiding project managers to deliberately address stakeholder needs for improved motivation and effective conflict mediation at critical project lifecycle stages; a Competency Model, outlining essential mindset and skillset elements beneficial for recruitment, staffing, personal development, and repositioning; and a Trust Model, emphasizing psychological safety as foundational for successful collaboration. These models are grounded in extensive theoretical insights from business management and psychology, enriched by empirical data from 53 qualitative interviews across 20 countries, and a quantitative survey with 827 participants from 74 countries.

Practical guidelines illustrate how these models can be integrated within both project-specific contexts and broader organizational governance structures. Finally, opportunities for further research are identified, suggesting potential applications in areas such as functional business leadership and organizational change management.

Context and Thesis Setup

1.	Introduction and Context

Projects are temporary endeavors aimed at achieving a specific outcome, often linked to a desired effect or benefit. They typically involve cross-functional teams operating within a temporary, “projectized” structure, led by a project manager responsible for establishing the project framework, assembling the team, fostering its development into a high-performing unit, and ensuring motivation and effective conflict mediation. 

Several key aspects distinguish projects from the functional elements of an organization, such as operational teams within a company’s permanent structure:

•	Projects are time-bound (Patzak & Rattay, 2009, p. 20) and execution-driven (Gareis & Gareis, 2017, p. 29), requiring a steady work pace (Fischer & Charef, 2021, pp. 18-23), which is primarily established and maintained by the project manager.
•	Project managers must strategically manage time as a resource (Patzak & Rattay, 2009, p. 32), with high expectations regarding the justification of resource use, particularly in terms of human effort and availability.
•	As lateral leaders without hierarchical authority, project managers rely heavily on human-centered leadership skills to guide their teams effectively. (Koçak, 2019, pp. 657-677) At the same time, they often lack essential social skills, limiting stakeholder engagement and project success. (Aleman, 2023, pp. 55-58)
•	Project teams often form at the project’s outset, bringing together individuals with no prior experience working together in this specific setting. They must navigate various stages of team development before becoming a high-performing unit. (Stein, 2025)
•	Projects inherently involve risks, including stakeholder-related challenges such as low motivation or team conflicts. Project managers must proactively anticipate foreseeable risks while also responding effectively to unforeseen challenges. (Patzak & Rattay, 2009, pp. 77-79)
•	Project processes are inherently time-dependent, following a structured lifecycle that includes an initiation or startup phase, a planning phase, an execution phase with interim closures, and a final closing phase—each requiring distinct management considerations. (Patzak & Rattay, 2009, pp. 27-29)

Effective stakeholder management and team dynamics are critical to project success. This thesis examines how a needs-focused leadership approach can bridge the gap between theory and practice, leading to the development of a model that integrates motivation, mediation, and actionable strategies to improve project outcomes. The model considers the unique characteristics of projects along with their broader context and environment.

1.2	Problem Statement & Initial Working Theses

Despite these advances, many project managers still struggle to translate theoretical insights on stakeholder needs, motivation, and conflict mediation into concrete, day-to-day practices. This often results in wasted time and resources, causing projects to be perceived as “lacking success” despite meeting their objectives. While numerous studies highlight the importance of these factors, an integrative approach that systematically connects them within a structured framework remains lacking. Such an approach could enhance their practical application in both project execution and the broader organizational environment (Organizational Project Management).

As a starting point, the following assumptions and working theses are examined:

•	Some project managers are unaware of the importance of a needs-focused approach in fostering motivation and mediating conflicts, lacking an understanding of the connection between the two.
•	Some project managers may lack the necessary mindset (recruitment, personal development) or skillset (training) to effectively engage with stakeholder needs, raising the question of what specific elements these should include.
•	Some project managers do not allocate sufficient time to engage with stakeholder needs, yet the underlying reasons remain unclear, making it difficult to develop effective tools and techniques to support them.
•	Some project managers adopt a predominantly technical approach, often relying on a hierarchical “command and control” style, with limited emphasis on the human aspects of project leadership.
•	Some project managers address motivation and conflicts only superficially, lacking tools and techniques to uncover the underlying needs behind stated positions or surface-level stakeholder interests.
•	Needs-focused approaches, such as Nonviolent Communication, are often disregarded in leadership development programs due to a perceived lack of scientific validation.
•	The organizational project management culture may hinder the effective application of needs-focused leadership approaches or fail to integrate them into existing standards and processes.

Projects and Success Framework

A project is a temporary organization for executing a relatively unique, short- to mid-term, strategically important business process of moderate scope. The more novel the goals and tasks, the higher the associated risk, as little prior experience may be available. (Gareis & Gareis, 2017, p. 33)

A project life cycle consists of phases from start to completion, forming the framework for project management. These phases can be sequential, iterative, or overlapping, regardless of the project’s specific work. (PMI, 2023, p. 14) While every project is unique, certain elements remain consistent:

•	Initiation (Start-Up Phase): The team is formed, objectives are defined, and an initial working structure is established.
•	Planning Phase: Project details are planned, typically at a high level for the entire project and in greater detail for immediate phases. While planning is an ongoing process, much of the work occurs early in the project through progressive elaboration.
•	Execution Phase (including Monitoring & Controlling): Planned tasks are executed while addressing emerging challenges and adapting as needed. Formal plans, such as schedules, are monitored and compared against baselines, while other key aspects, including team collaboration and communication, are continuously assessed.
•	Closing Phase: Finalization occurs at the project’s end, with smaller closure activities following each project lifecycle phase.

Each phase has distinct characteristics, with uncertainty highest at the beginning and decreasing as work progresses. Consequently, project managers must adapt their leadership approach to meet the evolving needs of the team at different stages.

Companies may follow a formal model in which projects progress through defined management stage or phase gates (PMI, 2023, p. 18) at key phases—such as initiation, planning, execution, and closure. For example, a project may need to meet specific requirements during the initiation phase before moving into detailed planning. Similarly, certain planning elements must be in place before execution can officially begin, such as product development, which typically requires the most workforce. The checkpoints between these management stages, known as stage gates, serve as approval points that a project must pass through to advance.

While project lifecycle phases vary to some extent for each project, a stage model is applied uniformly as a governance standard across all projects within an organization. 

2.2	Project success

For a long time, project success was defined primarily by adherence to scope, time, and cost targets. Even today, performance is still frequently assessed against initial baselines—often overlooking the fact that these were either unrealistic from the outset or have been legitimately adjusted during the course of the project (Flyvbjerg, 2011, pp. 321-344). 

Over time, the concept of “project success” has evolved from a narrow technical view to a broader managerial perspective that also considers constraints, leadership, and overall business value. (Gareis & Gareis, 2017, p. 53)  This includes the consideration of all stakeholder interests, including the team members and their satisfaction. (Patzak & Rattay, 2009, p. 33)

The latest practical definition by the Project Management Institute says that project success means “Delivered value that was worth the effort and expense” (PMI, 2024). With regards to the leadership of the project manager towards the project team members, the associated report identifies typical performance levers (grouped into schemes), such as 

•	Minimum start-up difficulties
•	Well-managed stakeholder expectations incl. their alignment
•	Project team resilience
•	Effective communication and coordination
•	The acceptance of failure
•	Cross-functional collaboration
•	Authority and influence of the project manager
•	Effective removal of impediments/blockers
•	Capable project manager (with relevant experience)
•	Well-established communication plan and process
•	Effective involvement of key stakeholders
•	Conflict is well managed

with 

•	Employee Experience
being among the top 5 performance schemes for achieving success. 

There are several theoretical approaches to understanding and practicing management, each offering distinct perspectives:

•	Functional approaches focus on breaking down management into distinct tasks such as planning, organizing, and controlling. While effective for structuring responsibilities, they tend to overlook human dynamics.
•	Empirical approaches emphasize learning from real-life case studies and practical experience rather than universal theories. This encourages context-specific solutions but can lead to overgeneralization or neglect of changing conditions.
•	Behavioral approaches center on the human element of organizations, analyzing communication, motivation, group dynamics, and interpersonal behavior. These are crucial for understanding team development and leadership.
•	Systemic-evolutionary approaches view organizations as social systems that evolve through communication, shared roles, and cultural patterns. They emphasize reflexivity and the observer’s influence, drawing from constructivist and second-order cybernetics.
•	Decision theory approaches conceptualize management as a sequence of informed decisions made under uncertainty. They encourage logical structuring, quantitative evaluation, and cost-benefit analysis as core competencies in problem-solving.
•	System-oriented approaches, building on systems theory, view management within the broader context of the organization as an interconnected system in interaction with its environment. Using models from systems thinking, management is seen as a regulatory function that transforms inputs into outputs and coordinates flows of information, energy, and resources.
(Patzak & Rattay, 2009, pp. 36-40)

This thesis does not follow a single theoretical approach but instead adopts an integrative perspective that combines classical, behavioral, systemic, and empirical views on management.

Successful teams thrive on strong cohesion, shared purpose, and intrinsic motivation. What makes them effective is not just individual talent, but the way members interact and engage with one another. Clear goals that are understood and embraced by everyone provide direction, while a transparent distribution of roles and responsibilities builds trust and clarity. Leadership plays a crucial role—not by dominating, but by facilitating dialogue and fostering a sense of ownership across the team.

An open and respectful atmosphere allows for honest communication, where all voices are heard and valued. Conflicts are not ignored but addressed directly and constructively, strengthening collaboration rather than undermining it. Motivation emerges naturally in such environments, driven by mutual commitment, external recognition, and a shared belief in the team’s purpose. When decision-makers are actively involved and everyone feels empowered, motivation and conflict management become part of the team’s DNA. (Patzak & Rattay, 2009, p. 66)

Throughout the history of practical project management standards and frameworks, the emphasis on conflict management and motivation has been recognized but often remains insufficiently detailed. The PMI’s first global standard (PMI, 1987) identifies these as key skills, but without further elaboration—a trend that continues in the latest version (PMI, 2021), which still lacks specific guidance for practitioners. The PMI’s Competency Development framework (PMI, 2017) offers some additional insights but stops short of providing practical, hands-on applications. 

Similarly, the IPMA highlighted conflict management and motivation in their first IPMA Competence Baseline (IPMA, 1998) and expanded on these topics in their Individual Competence Baseline 3.0 (IPMA, 2006), though primarily at a conceptual level. Current standards from the International Standards Organization (ISO, 2020), the UK’s PRINCE2 framework (PeopleCert International, 2023), and the PM² methodology of the European Commission (CoEPM², 2018) also reference these areas but without offering actionable tools or frameworks.

Human Factors & Team Dynamics

Projects are ultimately human endeavors. While tools, plans, and methodologies provide structure, the true engine of any project is the people involved—how they interact, collaborate, and respond to challenges. Project managers are therefore not only responsible for delivering outcomes but also for fostering the social and emotional environment that enables those outcomes. 

In (matrix-based) project environments, such as those with dual reporting lines and overlapping authority, conflicts are not only more frequent but also more complex and people-centered (El-Najdawi & Liberatore, 1997, pp. 25-31). This requires emotional intelligence (EQ) and refined soft skills. Effective communication, empathy, and adaptive leadership empower project managers to motivate their teams, manage stakeholder relationships, and effectively handle conflicts. (Perkins, 2023)
	
Motivation, communication, emotion, and conflict dynamics play pivotal roles in shaping both the performance and well-being of project teams. This chapter explores the psychological and interpersonal dimensions of project work, emphasizing how understanding human needs, emotions, and behaviors supports sustainable collaboration, trust, and effective leadership. By integrating insights from psychology, leadership theory, and personal experience, this chapter frames project execution as not just a technical process—but a deeply human one.

3.1	People and Needs

Projects are fundamentally built around people and are inherently a social construct. The project manager’s role extends beyond execution to include managing the project team and ensuring team members’ well-being (PeopleCert International, 2023, p. 319). At their core, projects aim to implement solutions, relying on collaborative efforts and a steady work pace to achieve defined goals within a set timeframe. Ultimately, a project is about people executing work, which can be broken down into two primary elements:

•	People: Those performing the work.
•	Execution: The work itself and the pace at which it occurs.

While definitions of project success vary, the most widely accepted and clearest baseline is that a project can be considered successfully executed if the agreed work has been completed. However, this raises the question of which factors can limit success, particularly regarding the people involved. Based on Daniel Hendling’s experience, two critical factors stand out:

•	The motivation of the people involved (the project team).
•	The quality of conflict management within the team (or with other stakeholders).

A lack of motivation often slows the overall pace of work (Larsson, et al., 2018, pp. 275-288), just as unresolved conflicts disrupt progress. Conversely, when motivation is high and conflicts are managed effectively, the likelihood of maintaining a steady pace—and thus achieving project success—increases. (Irwan, 2024, pp. 481-489)

Projects can be viewed through multiple perspectives, particularly when considering the roles within the organization. Drawing from personal experience as a project manager, Daniel Hendling highlights the dual role a project manager must play:

•	Motivator: Ensuring team members remain motivated.
•	Mediator: Helping resolve conflicts to sustain collaboration.

These roles contribute to maintaining the pace of execution and ensuring sustainable “movement” throughout the project. 

However, from Daniel Hendling’s perspective, the element of human needs is often overlooked in traditional project management. Several recurring challenges highlight this:

•	In conflicts, participants often focus on debating their opposing positions and interests, expressing emotions without revealing the underlying needs, leaving project managers with few tools to mediate effectively.
•	Team members may lack motivation but struggle to articulate what could act as a motivating factor or why it matters.
•	While project managers may generally understand the mindset (e.g., being positive and motivating) and skillset (e.g., leadership skills, business acumen, managing transitions) needed for success, they may lack awareness of the specific elements required to adopt a truly “needs-focused” approach during project execution.

This underscores the importance of equipping project managers with tools and techniques to address human needs effectively, ensuring both individual and collective success.
 

3.2	Team development

The sometimes called “Tuckman ladder” is a model developed by Bruce W. Tuckman, showing the phases a team needs to pass through as it develops into a performing team. While the initial model consisted of four distinct phases (Tuckman, 1965), he later added the fifth (Tuckman & Jensen, 1977).

•	Forming: Characterized by orientation, testing, and dependence. Group members attempt to understand the group’s purpose, test boundaries, and rely on leaders or established norms.
•	Storming: Marked by intragroup conflict, emotional responses to task demands, and resistance to group influence. Members challenge authority and vie for positions, leading to polarization and tension.
•	Norming: A phase of cohesion where group members establish norms, accept each other, and develop harmony. There is open exchange of interpretations and opinions, leading to better collaboration.
•	Performing: The stage where the group structure becomes a tool for task completion. Members adopt functional roles, and energy is channeled toward productivity and achieving group objectives.
•	Adjourning: The process of group dissolution, where members prepare for disengagement, reflect on their experiences, and deal with the emotional and logistical aspects of the group’s termination.

In reality, teams continuously evolve, and each change can shift them to a different Tuckman Stage. A team that is comfortably Norming or Performing may revert to Storming when a new member joins or if a team member becomes disengaged. Effective project leaders anticipate these shifts and actively support the team in returning to Performing as quickly as possible. (WCU, 2024)

Although Tuckman’s model remains one of the most widely cited group development frameworks, other similar models exist. Since no single model is inherently superior, the choice depends on the team’s needs, leadership style, and context. (Vaida & Serban, 2021, pp. 91-110) For practical reasons and intuitive applicability, Daniel Hendling has decided to continue referencing Tuckman’s well-known model.

3.3	Emotion

Emotion can be defined as an instinctive or intense mental feeling, characterized by agitation or an excited state of mind. (Oxford University Press, 2025) Emotions are brief, involuntary experiences involving sensations and responses triggered by personally significant events. They arise automatically through an unconscious appraisal of our environment, temporarily narrowing our perception to information consistent with the feeling. Once individuals become aware of an emotional episode, they can consciously reappraise the situation. Emotional experiences integrate universal, evolutionarily inherited themes with culturally learned aspects. Importantly, our desire to experience (or avoid experiencing) specific emotions significantly motivates behavior. Moreover, emotional expressions provide clear, universally recognizable signals to others. (Ekman, 2003, pp. 234-235)

Examples, grouped into categories, include:

•	Anger: aggravated, annoyed, bitter, furious, grouchy, hostile, irritated, outraged, resentful, spiteful, vengeful
•	Sadness: anguished, dejected, depressed, disappointed, dismayed, gloomy, glum, hopeless, hurt, melancholy, sorrowful
•	Fear: afraid, apprehensive, anxious, edgy, frightened, jumpy, nervous, panicked, tense, terrified, uneasy, worried
•	Shame and guilt: ashamed, chagrined, contrite, embarrassed, guilty, humiliated, mortified, regretful, remorseful, repentant
•	Disgust: contemptuous, disdainful, revolted, scornful
•	Surprise: amazed, astonished, shocked
•	Love: adoring, affectionate, caring, compassionate, friendly, infatuated, passionate, sympathetic, tender, warm 
•	Happiness: blissful, cheerful, content, delighted, elated, enthusiastic, excited, glad, gleeful, joyful, satisfied, thrilled
•	Other: bashful, bored, calm, interested, proud, reluctant, sceptical, suspicious

They can be further broken down into sensations in the body, thoughts in the mind, and urges (also called “action tendencies”) (Baer, 2018, pp. 72-74). While the expression of emotions varies across cultures (Meyer, 2015, p. 204), psychologist Paul Ekman has conducted extensive cross-cultural research on emotional expression, emphasizing the interpretation of body language. While Ekman initially developed his own list of basic emotions through his research (Ekman Group, 2025), a later global survey revealed a broader scientific consensus around five universally recognized emotions (Ekman, 2016):

1.	Sadness 
2.	Anger
3.	Fear
4.	Disgust
5.	Enjoyment

Anger often serves as a protector of hidden emotions, such as fear, sadness, or shame, which lie beneath the surface–a concept coined “The Anger Iceberg”. Recognizing anger as a signal for unexpressed feelings and unmet needs allows for deeper understanding and connection. (Benson, 2024)

How our face looks, how we behave, the tone of our voice… Our body language communicates to others how we are feeling, allowing the other to understand what’s happening inside us (Baer, 2018, p. 77).

3.4.1	Health over illness

Understanding the factors that contribute to well-being is essential for cultivating resilience and motivation, particularly in demanding or high-stress environments.

Salutogenesis, a theory developed by medical sociologist Aaron Antonovsky, offers a perspective seldom associated with psychology and motivation. Originating in the medical field, salutogenesis focuses on what promotes health and well-being rather than what causes disease, contrasting with the pathogenesis approach. While pathogenesis examines the origins and causes of illness to treat or mitigate its effects, salutogenesis emphasizes the origins of health and well-being. (Eriksson, 2022, p. 11)

The central concept of salutogenesis is the Sense of Coherence (SOC) (Eriksson, 2022, p. 65), consisting of these three elements:

•	Comprehensibility – Seeing the world as structured, predictable, and understandable.
•	Manageability – Believing one has the resources and support to meet life’s demands.
•	Meaningfulness – Finding purpose and value in life’s challenges. 

In the context of motivation, a strong SOC is highly relevant because it equips individuals with the ability to approach challenges with a sense of confidence and purpose. People with a strong SOC are more likely to perceive obstacles as manageable and meaningful, fostering resilience and intrinsic motivation. This perspective aligns well with the needs of project managers and team members, who often face stress and adversity. By developing a strong SOC, they can remain motivated to act, sustain effort in challenging situations, and effectively navigate stressors while maintaining well-being.

Building upon this foundation, recent research has extended salutogenesis into organizational contexts through models such as the Systemic Salutogenic Interaction Model (SSIM) (Eberz & Antoni, 2016). The SSIM expands on Antonovsky’s original framework by emphasizing that well-being and motivation in workplaces result from interactive, reciprocal dynamics between leaders and employees rather than unilateral influences. Specifically, it introduces the concept of Work-related Sense of Coherence (Work-SoC), emphasizing that both leaders and employees mutually influence each other’s sense of coherence through their interactions.

The SSIM identifies three key pathways for enhancing well-being and motivation in organizational settings:

1.	Reciprocal Interaction Dynamics: Both leaders’ and employees’ behaviors influence each other’s psychological health, motivation, and overall coherence experience. Positive interactions create reinforcing cycles of health and motivation, whereas negative behaviors can trigger harmful reciprocal effects (Eberz & Antoni, 2016, p. 267).
2.	Contextual Influences: The model emphasizes that organizational factors such as task structures, resources, cultural norms, and leadership expectations significantly shape interactions and ultimately influence individuals’ Work-SoC (Eberz & Antoni, 2016, p. 268).
3.	Personality Factors: Individual traits (e.g., emotional stability, conscientiousness) can moderate how leaders and employees experience and cope with organizational stressors, thereby influencing their Work-SoC (Eberz & Antoni, 2016, p. 270).


Thus, the SSIM highlights the necessity of creating organizational conditions and fostering interaction patterns that not only strengthen individual sense of coherence but also establish a shared, mutually supportive environment. Integrating these insights into practical leadership approaches and team dynamics can sustainably enhance resilience, intrinsic motivation, and overall well-being in project-oriented settings.

3.4.2	Human needs

Fulfilling human needs is a fundamental driver of motivation and behavior, shaping the ways individuals strive to achieve personal and collective goals. 

When discussing human needs, one of the most prominent figures referenced is Abraham Maslow, who introduced the Hierarchy of Needs, a psychological theory that organizes human needs into five levels, arranged in a pyramid (Maslow, 2014, pp. 62-74). Human beings are motivated to have their needs fulfilled (Maslow, 2014, p. 85).

SELF-ACTUALIZATION NEEDS: 
Fulfillment of one’s potential, personal growth, and self-realization.
Examples include creativity, problem-solving, and pursuing intrinsic passions.

ESTEEM NEEDS:
Lower esteem needs: Desire for respect, recognition, attention, and prestige from others.
Higher esteem needs: Desire for self-respect, confidence, achievement, independence, and mastery.

LOVE & BELONGING NEEDS:
Friendship, intimacy, family, social connections.
Includes both giving and receiving affection and belonging to a group or community.

SAFETY NEEDS:
Security, stability, protection from harm.
Includes physical safety (protection from dangers, illness, and accidents) and psychological safety (order, predictability).

PHYSIOLOGICAL NEEDS:Basic survival needs: 
food, water, shelter, sleep, oxygen, etc.
Most fundamental and pre-potent, as their lack dominates all other motivations.

Despite its widespread influence, Maslow’s hierarchy has been criticized for its lack of empirical grounding, vague definitions, and a culturally narrow foundation that limits its universal applicability. (Winter, 2016) The model proposes that people generally aim to fulfill lower-level needs before pursuing higher-level ones, with each level building upon the last. However, Maslow himself emphasized that this progression is not as rigid as the pyramid structure suggests—individuals can, and often do, pursue higher-level goals even when some basic needs remain unmet. (Maslow, 2014, p. 79)

Nevertheless, Maslow’s hierarchy remains widely applicable today due to its fundamental insight into human motivation across diverse contexts such as business, education, healthcare, and organizational ethics. Its lasting relevance stems from the model’s ability to clearly articulate universal human needs, allowing flexible adaptation to evolving societal conditions and contemporary challenges. (IvyPanda, 2019)

Daniel Hendling has developed his own model of human needs, based on other models and on his practical experience, removing any idea of a hierarchy or dependencies and bringing it into a structure to allow fast identification and analysis.

3.4.3	The fully functioning person

The American psychologist Carl Rogers sees two personal goals a person has: The human organism “functioning freely” and the existential quality of satisfying living. (Rogers, 2020, pp. 167-175) His concepts of these goals circle around moving away from 

•	Facades (false fronts we are hiding behind, and starting to express our true feelings)
•	Oughts (rejecting imposed ideas about or expectations of ourselves that we’re trying to follow or achieve)
•	Meeting expectations (breaking free from conformity pressures, may it be society or the organization, in order to act as a free individual)
•	Pleasing others (stopping to base our actions on approval or acceptance and living based on our own needs and values)

and instead moving towards

•	Self-direction (taking own choices, becoming the primary authority of what’s important to us – and actively shaping the personal meaning and experience of our choices, even in constrained or externally controlled contexts) (Goldsby, et al., 2021, p. 3)
•	Being process (accepting life as a dynamic flow in which we evolve, instead of fixed elements or states)
•	Being Complexity (living the full range of our emotions and reactions, integrating all of our aspects—even the contradictory ones—stimulates creativity by enhancing cognitive flexibility, yet simultaneously leads to self-regulatory resource depletion) (Stollberger, et al., 2023, p. 1015)
•	Openness to Experience (fully processing inner emotional states and outer experiences, seeing them as resources rather than threats; this trait positively predicts upward job changes into managerial and professional positions, like the one of a project manager) (Nieß & Zacher, 2015, p. 13)
•	Acceptance of Others (without judgment or demands; experiencing positive regard from leaders or peers boosts vitality, significantly enhancing job performance and prosocial team behaviors) (Shefer, et al., 2018, pp. 75-76)
•	Trust of Self (believing in our unique perspective and daring to being ourselves in every situation; closely related to authenticity—expressing one’s true thoughts, feelings, values, and behaviors without conforming to organizational expectations) (Thomas, 2024)

3.4.4	Valence and Expectancy

In his “Expectancy Theory”, Victor H. Vroom sees two key elements to be essential with regards to motivation, with “instrumentality” being the extent to which the idea of a result is directly related to the desired outcome.

•	Valence: The preference a person has for (or against) a specific outcome, with an anticipated satisfaction (or dissatisfaction) associated with this outcome. In other words: How much I want to achieve something (positive valence).
•	Expectancy: The idea that a certain action will lead to a specific result. Or: How likely it seems to me to be achieving what I want to achieve with the action taken.

Motivation, which he calls “force”, increases with higher valence and/or higher expectancy. If one of either is zero, there is no motivation. (Vroom, 1995, pp. 17-23) His framework also includes the idea that performance is a function of ability and motivation. (Vroom, 1995, p. 237)

Performance = ⨍(Ability x Motivation)

3.4.5	Achievement, Power & Affiliation

David McClelland sees three main motives at the core of motivation:

•	The Achievement Motive: The intrinsic drive to accomplish challenging tasks, improve performance, and take personal responsibility for success, measured through spontaneous thought patterns and reflected in behaviors that prioritize goal attainment and progress. (McClelland, 1987, pp. 223-229)
•	The Power Motive: The drive to influence, control, or impact others, groups, or the broader environment, either for personal gain or to achieve organizational and societal goals. (McClelland, 1987, pp. 268-280)
•	The Affiliation Motive: The desire to establish, maintain, or restore positive, affective relationships with others, driven by sensitivity to social cues and a preference for harmonious and meaningful interpersonal connections.
(McClelland, 1987, pp. 346-348)

Later research finds that managers typically exhibit distinct motivation profiles based on McClelland’s theory, and those predominantly motivated by power—especially in socially responsible ways—are associated with greater organizational effectiveness. (Andersen, 2018)

In their “Self Determination Theory” (SDT), Richard M. Ryan and Edward L. Deci define three basic needs that, if being fulfilled, lead to motivation and well-being:

•	Autonomy: The need to self-regulate one’s experiences and actions, characterized by volition, congruence, and alignment with authentic interests and values, where behaviors are self-endorsed rather than controlled by external or nonintegrated forces.
•	Competence: The need to feel effective and masterful in one’s interactions with the environment, driven by an inherent striving for curiosity, learning, and skill development, which is nurtured by optimal challenges and positive feedback but undermined by excessive difficulty, criticism, or negative social comparisons.
•	Relatedness: The need to feel socially connected and, characterized by experiencing care from others, contributing meaningfully to social groups, and feeling integral to relationships and broader communities.
(Ryan & Deci, 2017, pp. 10-11)

Extending this framework, autonomy-supportive leadership, rooted in SDT, provides actionable methods—such as offering choice, structured feedback, and optimal challenges—that leaders can use to practically enhance employees’ autonomy, competence, relatedness, motivation, and creative performance. (Zakaria & Zhang, 2014, pp. 135-147)

Expanding SDT to teams, Grenier, Gagné, and O’Neill (2024) propose a model where supportive interactions among team members fulfilling autonomy, competence, and relatedness foster collective motivation. Specifically, they identify an interpersonal feedback loop, where consistent need-supportive behaviors lead to team motivation convergence, and team identification, emphasizing how team members internalize shared values. This approach highlights the importance of leadership promoting an environment that nurtures intrinsic team motivation, enhancing overall team effectiveness and goal attainment. (Grenier, et al., 2024, pp. 1833-1865)

3.4.6	Drivers

The American psychologist Taibi Kahler has found that people have unconscious motivators he calls “drivers”, that influence their behavior and motivation. These are:

•	Be perfect
•	Please others
•	Hurry Up
•	Be Stong
•	Try Hard
(Kahler, 2008, p. 21)

Understanding the different drivers can help the project manager identify what motivates an individual (or himself). 

3.4.7	Flourishing

Positive Psychology is a branch of psychology that focuses on studying and promoting the positive aspects of human life, such as positive emotion, well-being, and “flourishing”, how Martin E.P. Seligman calls it (Seligman, 2013, p. 13). His PERMA Model is a theory on well-being and consists of five elements:

•	Positive emotion: The emotion one has when the goal of happiness and satisfaction has been achieved, such as pleasure, ecstasy, comfort, warmth etc.
•	Engagement: Being actively and deeply involved in tasks, moving forward – being “in a flow”. The element of “Flow” is also a concept described by Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi. It is about being fully involved in something where skill and challenge are balanced. (Csikszentmihalyi, 2002)
•	Relationships: Other people being the “best antidote to the downs of life and the single most reliable up”. It needs to be understood that this only refers to “positive” relationships, not the opposite.
•	Meaning: Sense of purpose, with a contribution to something larger of higher meaning.
•	Accomplishment: Mastering something and achieving one’s goals.
(Seligman, 2013, pp. 16-21)

With “flourishing”, Seligman describes the state where the following “core features” and at least three of the “additional features” are present (Seligman, 2013, p. 27).

Core Features:
Positive emotions
Engagement, interest
Meaning, purpose

Additional Features:
Self-esteem
Optimism
Resilience
Vitality
Self-determination
Positive relationships

While Seligman’s original PERMA model outlines five core elements of wellbeing, the extended PERMA+ framework incorporates additional factors that contribute significantly to psychological wellbeing. These include optimism, physical activity, nutrition, and sleep. The “+” thereby broadens the model’s scope beyond psychological states and social connections to include foundational aspects of physical self-care, fostering a more holistic and sustainable approach to human flourishing. 

Building upon the extended PERMA+ framework, the PERMA+4 model further enriches the concept by explicitly integrating four essential elements specifically relevant for work-related contexts. These additional elements are Physical Health, emphasizing optimal biological and psychological functioning; Mindset, highlighting growth orientation, resilience, and psychological capital; Work Environment, recognizing the role of physical space and atmosphere in influencing well-being; and Economic Security, underscoring the impact of financial stability on psychological health. (Donaldson, et al., 2022)

Recent research indicates that the PERMA model also applies in organizational contexts. Ho and Chan (2022) found that perceived organizational support significantly promotes employee flourishing via psychological capital (hope, efficacy, resilience, optimism), reflecting how organizational culture and support—core aspects of organizational project management (OPM)—influence employees’ psychosocial well-being. (Ho & Chan, 2022, p. 922)

3.4.8	VIA Strengths

In the early 2000s, social sciences researchers identified a shared framework of 24 character strengths that represent aspects of human personality. These strengths are present in everyone, but in varying degrees, giving everyone a unique character strengths profile. The 24 strengths are organized into six broad virtue categories that are universally recognized across cultures and nations (VIA Institute on Character, 2025) and can be used for self-reflection, relationship-building, leadership development or coaching.

Empirical research highlights that specific VIA character strengths—especially perseverance, honesty, self-regulation, prudence, teamwork, kindness, leadership, and curiosity—are strongly linked to improved job performance and organizational support, particularly when employees regularly use these signature strengths in their roles. (Harzer & Ruch, 2014)

Wisdom: Creativity, Curiosity, Judgment, Learning, Perspective
Courage: Bravery, Perseverance, Honesty, Zest
Humanity: Love, Kindness, Social Intelligence
Justice	: Teamwork, Fairness, Leadership
Temperance: Forgiveness, Humility, Prudence, Self-Regulation
Transcendence: Beauty & Excellence, Gratitude, Hope, Humor, Spirituality

Rooted in philosophical traditions such as Aristotelian ethics, these character strengths differ notably from broad personality traits, specifically targeting morally valued attributes that define ethical behavior and what constitutes a ‘good person.’ Empirical research has demonstrated their practical applicability across contexts, showing that intentional cultivation of strengths enhances individual and organizational outcomes including job satisfaction, resilience, and flourishing. Character strengths, thus, not only facilitate fulfilling human needs but serve as robust tools for navigating adversity and achieving sustained personal and professional growth. (Advani & Mergenthaler, 2024)


Mediation and Conflict

Mediation involves the intervention of a third party to help resolve conflicts and foster collaboration by addressing interpersonal dynamics, understanding differing perspectives, and guiding individuals or teams toward constructive solutions. It serves as an act of agency or action as an intermediary, functioning as a means of action or a medium of transmission to achieve resolution and understanding that conflicting parties may struggle to achieve on their own. (Oxford English Dictionary, 2025)

It is important to understand that with a project manager as a mediator, this “third party” does indeed have some kind of authority over the people involved, so in this case we are to some extent talking about a “managerial approach and procedure” of mediation taken (Moore, 2014, p. 9), with the project manager possibly exerting some power or the risk of becoming involved in the conflict.

3.5.1	The inner team

Introspection plays a crucial role in understanding and managing internal conflicts, as highlighted by Friedemann Schulz von Thun’s “Inner Team” model, which emphasizes self-reflection as a dialogue between the diverse voices within one’s mind. Its core elements are:

•	Plurality: The inner team consists of multiple voices or parts that interact, sometimes in harmony and sometimes in conflict, mirroring the dynamics of a real team.
•	There’s a Leader: The team leader, or “team captain,” is responsible for uniting the inner team, creating synergies, and transforming chaotic inner dialogues into productive collaboration. This is essential when there are inner conflicts.
•	Conflicts: Inner conflicts are inevitable and essential, but they must be acknowledged and resolved.
•	Team Development: Not all inner team members are equally visible, and sidelining or suppressing certain parts can harm inner harmony, making their integration vital for the “team development” of the inner team.
•	Adaptation: Depending on the context and situation, different members of the inner team step forward, requiring flexible adjustments to align with life’s varying demands.
•	Configuration: Ensuring the inner team’s alignment with situational needs involves understanding the context and achieving a balance that fosters congruence and effective self-management.
(Thun, 2023, pp. 21-22)

The way the members of the inner team communicate with each other can be analyzed using his “four sides of a message” model, explained in chapter 3.6.2. (Thun, 2023, p. 39)

3.5.2	Understanding

Understanding the dynamics of a conflict is supported by applying concepts such as the Conflict Model developed by Kenneth W. Thomas and Ralph B. Kilmann (“Thomas-Kilmann Conflict Mode Instrument – TKI”). It allows to analyze conflicts from two different perspectives:

•	The assertiveness of a party.
•	The cooperativeness of a party.

Depending on the degree there is a different typical conflict management style observed. (Kilmann, 2023, p. 5)

Low Assertiveness and low cooperativeness means Avoiding
Low Assertiveness and high cooperativeness means Accomodating
High Assertiveness and low cooperativeness means Competing
High Assertiveness and high cooperativeness means collaborating
A balance of all means compromising

Under high stress, assertiveness develops into attacking behavior, while cooperativeness into retreating behavior (Kilmann, 2023, p. 19)  – observations, that can be viewed as related to responding to trauma. 

The four Fs of trauma response are: Flight (mobilization), freeze (immobilization), fight (offensive) and fawn (defensive).

3.5.3	Conflict escalation

Conflicts, if remaining unmediated and consequentially unresolved follow a certain scheme identified by the researcher Friedrich Glasl.

Temporary missteps and tension arise
Awareness of existing tension creates strain

DEBATE (2):
Polarization in thinking, feeling, and wanting; black-and-white thinking
Tactics: Quasi-rational, verbal aggression
Speaking to the audience, scoring points through third parties
Temporary subgroups form around positions
Discrepancy between “overtone and undertone”
"Superior versus inferior" dynamic(see Transactional Analysis)

ACTIONS (3):
“Talking no longer helps” – therefore: actions!
Strategy of creating faits accomplis
Discrepancy between verbal and non-verbal behavior, with non-verbal dominating
Danger: Misinterpretations 
“Pessimistic anticipation” lead to mistrust and acceleration
Formation of group boundaries, cohesion, and role crystallization
Empathy is lost

IMAGES & COALITIONS (4):
Stereotypes, clichés, image campaigns, and rumors focus on knowledge and abilities
Parties maneuver each other into negative roles and engage in conflict
Recruitment of supporters and formation of symbiotic coalitions
Perceptual fixation creates self-fulfilling prophecies.
Punitive behavior is framed to allow plausible deniability
Double binds arise through paradoxical instructions

In his scheme Glasl names a “Superior vs. inferior” position taken, referring to the model of “Transactional Analysis” by Eric Berne (International Transactional Analysis Association, 2025). 
In this model, Berne explains that in a communication situation (incl. conflict) every party is in one of three “ego” states:

•	Parent
•	Adult
•	Ego

When the communication flows between different states or levels – leading in essence to a “difference of eye level” – this may lead to or further fuel a conflict. He also mentions “double-bind,” which is a concept describing situations where an individual is confronted with two or more conflicting messages, creating a paradoxical dilemma in which responding to one message inherently negates the other. This predicament is compounded by the fact that the individual cannot escape or resolve the conflict without facing negative consequences. (Gibson, 2024)

3.5.4	The importance of a third person

Conflicts can be constructive and lead to growth if participants work cooperatively, set aside distrust, and find solutions that address their needs and interests. However, many struggle to achieve this without help and often require a third party to assist in reaching mutually acceptable resolutions. (Moore, 2014, p. ix) A mediation approach is taken when for instance

•	Parties’ emotions or expression of negative feelings about the situation or toward each other are intense and are preventing a focused or calm discussion or agreement.
•	There is a lack of trust and respect between or among disputants that is hindering productive talks.
•	Communication between parties is poor in quantity or quality, and they cannot improve it on their own.
•	Misperceptions or stereotypes are hindering productive exchanges.
•	There are multiple issues in dispute, and disputants disagree about whether or how each should be addressed or resolved.
•	Disputants are stuck in bargaining over positions, each of their preferred solutions, and are unable to identify each other’s interests and develop mutually acceptable interest-based solutions.
•	Parties do not have an effective negotiating process, are using the wrong one, or are not using a potentially viable procedure to its best advantage.
•	Parties lack trust in each other and are concerned that the settlement will not be implemented as agreed.
(Moore, 2014, pp. 16-17).

3.5.5	An alternate to war or surrender

In some cases, conflicts may benefit from strategic actions taken by one party to de-escalate tensions and build trust gradually. This approach, rooted in good faith, emphasizes the importance of transparency and genuine intentions in fostering mutual understanding. Analyzing the tensions between the USA and Russia, the American Charles E. Osgood has developed a model as a third alternative to either “winning” (war) or “losing” (surrender). His GRIT model (Graduated and Reciprocated Initiatives in Tension Reduction), which acts to “de-escalate” a conflict, is based on these elements:

•	Unilateral Initiatives: One side takes the initiative to de-escalate tensions through small, measurable, and controlled steps, even without requiring prior agreement from the opposing party, signaling goodwill while maintaining strength and security.
•	Reciprocity: The model relies on the likelihood that the opposing party will reciprocate with similar de-escalatory actions, creating a “spiral of trust.”, encouraged through consistent, transparent actions.
•	Graduated Steps: Actions are incremental, making them less risky and more feasible for both sides to implement without fear of exploitation.
•	Tension Reduction: GRIT focuses on reducing psychological and environmental tensions, which are seen as major contributors to irrational decision-making and unstable situations.
(Osgood, 1965, pp. 85-98)

3.5.6	The ABC model of situation, thoughts, and feelings

The ABC model, developed by psychologist Albert Ellis, explains emotional distress by showing how emotions (C) are not directly caused by an event (A) but are instead mediated by our thoughts and interpretations (B).
•	A - Activating event: A situation or event that happens (e.g., someone ignores the other).
•	B - Beliefs/Interpretations: The thoughts or interpretations we have about the event (e.g., “They don’t like me” or “They’re preoccupied”).
•	C - Consequences: The emotions and behaviors that result (e.g., sadness, anger, or self-doubt).
(Segal, et al., 2013, pp. 161-164)

By identifying, analyzing, and challenging the B (beliefs/interpretations)—often occurring subconsciously as immediate explanations we create to make sense of events—one can influence emotional and behavioral consequences (C) and respond more effectively. These beliefs frequently include cognitive distortions, which are irrational thought patterns like overgeneralization or catastrophizing that intensify emotional distress. Challenging beliefs involves actively disputing their accuracy and rationality by considering evidence both supporting and contradicting these thoughts. (Dan & Sekoul, 2021)

This process helps “peel back” the layers of thoughts, positions, interests, and values to uncover the core needs of another person.

Empirical studies highlight the effectiveness of the ABC model in real-life contexts. For instance, a study with Norwegian adolescents showed that teaching students the ABC model significantly reduced anxiety, depression, and dysfunctional thoughts, while increasing hope and self-esteem (Sælid & Nordahl, 2017). 

3.5.7	A model for empathy

Building on strategies like mediation and tension-reduction models, which focus on resolving conflicts and fostering trust, Marshall B. Rosenberg takes the concept a step further by emphasizing the role of human needs and emotions in collaboration. His approach, Nonviolent Communication (NVC), provides a framework for understanding and addressing conflicts through empathy, helping individuals express their perspectives honestly while fostering mutual understanding and connection. In his model he differentiates between

•	The concrete actions we observe that affect our well-being
•	How we feel in relation to what we observe
•	The needs, values, desires, etc. that create our feelings
•	The concrete actions we request to enrich our lives
(Rosenberg, 2015, p. 7)

Daniel has further elaborated this model to better understand its components and to apply it to different situations of interpersonal collaboration. In addition to the external observation explained in the NVC process there is the element of thoughts, ideas and imagination that might play a role. Individuals often think in judgements: Positions in the sense of being right or wrong, which Rosenberg calls “Evaluation” (Rosenberg, 2015, p. 26). From methods such as the “Power & Interest Matrix” in Project Stakeholder Management we understand interests as desires (see also: WIIFM, “What Is In For Me”), with can be understood to be further related to our values and at the lowest level our individual needs.

(from outside to inside)
Observations, Stimuli: Hearing, Perception, Experience, Trigger
Positions: judgment, right and wrong, ok and not ok
Imagination: thoughts
Interests: what one wants to achieve
Feelings: Emotion
Values: Common or otherwise manifested ideas of what one values
Needs: as the most inside and abstract level

The extent to which these needs are being met defines our emotional reaction, may it be positive (good feelings) or negative (bad feelings) (Rosenberg, 2015, p. 44). In other words, the needs are stimulated leading to a certain feeling. This function is by some called a “trigger”, or “button(s) being pushed”.

3.5.8	Understanding cultural differences

While Nonviolent Communication (NVC) focuses on understanding and addressing individual needs and emotions to resolve conflicts and build empathy, cultural differences add an additional layer of complexity to interpersonal dynamics. Geert Hofstede’s cultural dimensions and Erin Meyer’s insights in the “Culture Map” highlight how deeply ingrained cultural norms influence communication, values, and expectations. Recognizing these differences is essential for applying NVC effectively in diverse settings, where empathy must extend across cultural boundaries to foster mutual understanding and collaboration.

Geert Hofstede defines culture as "the collective programming of the mind that distinguishes the members of one category of people from another". (Hofstede & Hofstede, 2010, p. 6) He refers to the following elements, which can be different in different cultures:

•	Power Distance: The extent to which less powerful members of organizations and institutions accept and expect that power is unequally distributed. (Hofstede & Hofstede, 2010, p. 55) 
•	Individualism and Collectivism: Individualism refers to societies in which the bonds between individuals are loose, while collectivism refers to societies in which people are integrated into strong, cohesive groups from birth. (Hofstede & Hofstede, 2010, p. 89) 
•	Masculinity and Femininity: Masculinity stands for a society that values competitiveness, assertiveness and material success, while femininity stands for a preference for relationships, modesty, caring for the weak and quality of life. (Hofstede & Hofstede, 2010, p. 138) 
•	Uncertainty Avoidance: The extent to which members of a culture are uncomfortable with uncertainty and ambiguity, leading them to support beliefs and behaviors that promise certainty and conformity. (Hofstede & Hofstede, 2010, p. 188) 
•	Long- and Short- Term Orientation: Long-term orientation focuses on the future, values thrift and perseverance, while short-term orientation focuses on the present or past and values respect for tradition and fulfillment of social obligations. (Hofstede & Hofstede, 2010, p. 239) 

One topic discussed in of this thesis is Humor, which is highly culture specific. For example, in Dutch culture, “friendly insults” like “You scoundrel” express warm sympathy, while in Indonesia, where maintaining face is crucial, such remarks are taken literally. (Hofstede & Hofstede, 2010, pp. 390-391)
Inspired by Hofstede’s and other existing culture models and based on her own experience and research, Erin Meyer in her “Culture Map” model lists the following elements to being different per geographic culture, and by that, possibly per individual working in a culturally diverse project team:

Low-context
Good communication is precise, simple, and clear. Messages are expressed and understood at face falue. Repetition is appreciated if it helps clarify the communication.	

High-context
Good communication is sophisticated, nuanced, and layered. Messages are both spoken and read between the lines. Messages are often implied but not plainly expressed. (Meyer, 2015, p. 39)

Direct negative feedback
Negative feedback to a colleague is provided frankly, bluntly, honestly. Negative messages stand alone, not softened by positive ones. Absolute descriptors are often used (totally inappropriate, completely unprofessional) when criticizing. Criticism may be given to an individual in front of a group.

Indirect negative feedback
Negative feedback to a colleague is provided softly, subtly, diplomatically. Positive messages are used to wrap negative ones. Qualifying descriptors are often used (sort of inappropriate, slightly unprofessional) when criticizing. Criticism is given only in private. (Meyer, 2015, p. 69)


Applications-first
Individuals are trained to begin with a fact, statement, or opinion and later add concepts to back up or explain the conclusion as necessary. The preference is to begin a message or report with an executive summary or bullet points. Discussions are approached in a practical, concrete manner. Theoretical or philosophical discussions are avoided in a business environment.	

Principles-first
Individuals have been trained to first develop the theory or complex concept before presenting a fact, statement, or opinion. The preference is to begin a message or report by building up a theoretical argument before moving on to a conclusion. The conceptual principles underlying each situation are valued. (Meyer, 2015, p. 96)

Egalitarian
The ideal distance between a boss and a subordinate is low. The best boss is a facilitator among equals. Organizational structures are flat. Communication often skips hierarchical lines.	

Hierarchical
The ideal distance between a boss and a subordinate is high. The best boss is a strong director who leads from the front. Status is important. Organizational structures are multilayered and fixed. Communication follows set hierarchical lines. (Meyer, 2015, p. 125)

Consensual
Decisions are made in groups through unanimous agreement.

Top-down
Decisions are made by individuals (usually the boss). (Meyer, 2015, p. 150)

Task-based
Trust is built through business-related activities. Work relationships are built and dropped easily, based on the practicality of the situation. You do good work consistently, you are reliable, I enjoy working with you, I trust you.

Relationship-based
Trust is built through sharing meals, evening drinks, and visits at the coffee machine. Work relationships build up slowly over the long term. I've seen who you are at a deep level, I've shared personal time with you, I know others well who trust you, I trust you. (Meyer, 2015, p. 171)

Confrontational
Disagreement and debate are positive for the team or organization. Open confrontation is appropriate and will not negatively impact the relationship.

Avoids confrontation
Disagreement and debate are negative for the team or organization. Open confrontation is inappropriate and will break group harmony or negatively impact the relationship. (Meyer, 2015, p. 201)

Linear-time
Project steps are approached in a sequential fashion, completing one task before beginning the next. One thing at a time. No interruptions. The focus is on the deadline and sticking to the schedule. Emphasis is on promptness and good organization over flexibility.

Flexible-time
Project steps are approached in a fluid manner, changing tasks as opportunities arise. Many things are dealt with at once and interruptions accepted. The focus is on adaptability, and flexibility is valued over organization. (Meyer, 2015, p. 227)

While Meyer’s research as such may not typically be regarded as a fully “scientifically validated”, her research draws heavily from existing cultural frameworks, such as those by Edward Twitchell Hall, Geert Hendrik Hofstede, Alfonsus (Fons) Trompenaars, Charles Hampden-Turner, Richard Eugene Nisbett which are more extensively validated through empirical data.

Effective intercultural communication is vital to minimizing misunderstandings. Cultural dimensions significantly shape conflict management approaches, necessitating adaptive leadership or ‘switch leadership’ to respond to evolving cultural contexts within a project. Appreciating culture as a multilayered construct—including national, organizational, and individual dimensions—enables deeper insight beyond superficial stereotypes. Project managers must proactively engage with diverse cultures, critically reflect on their assumptions, and practically embrace diverse cultural practices to enhance mutual understanding, empathy, and effective collaboration. (Isern, 2016)

Trust, as outlined under 3.5.10, emerges as a central facilitator in intercultural project settings, significantly enhancing transparency, effective communication, and team cohesion. Building trust actively across cultural boundaries transforms diversity from a potential source of misunderstandings into a key asset for project success and collaborative excellence. (Lückmann & Färber, 2016) 

3.5.9	Differences between generations

Building on the importance of understanding cultural differences, generational differences add another layer of complexity to interpersonal and organizational dynamics. As multiple generations work side by side, each brings distinct needs, values, and expectations shaped by their unique experiences and societal influences. Recognizing and addressing these generational differences is crucial for fostering effective collaboration, engagement, and motivation in diverse teams and workplaces.

Four generations are currently working together on the German labor market (baby boomers, generations X/Y/Z), all of whom have different needs, wishes and expectations of the labor market and employers. (Hesse & Mattmüller, 2019, p. 55)

The changing elements include:

•	Trend towards individualization: Generation Z places particular value on personal autonomy and self-realization, which increases the focus on individual needs and interests.
•	The new search for meaning: Modern employees are increasingly looking for jobs that not only offer economic security, but also fulfill a deeper, social or personal meaning.
•	The increasing rejection of responsibility: Many young career starters are showing a growing reluctance to take on long-term and far-reaching professional responsibility.
•	The indecisiveness of the new generation: The well-educated young generation has access to numerous career opportunities, which often leads to indecision and frequent job changes.
•	Hierarchy is losing respect: Flat hierarchies and communication at eye level are increasingly preferred by the younger generations over traditional, rigid organizational structures.
•	Focus on comfort: The desire for a comfortable working environment with a good work-life balance and flexible working conditions is becoming increasingly important for many employees.
•	The protean career as the career path of the future: The protean career is characterized by adaptability and self-determination, whereby individuals adapt their career paths flexibly to personal goals and changes. It therefore has no "common thread" in the narrower sense, it is not linear.
(Hesse & Mattmüller, 2019, p. 86)

3.5.10	Error, Psychological Safety and Trust

Projects are inherently uncertain, and the human dimension, including interpersonal dynamics, introduces additional layers of what might be termed “social uncertainty.” Consequently, unexpected events inevitably occur—some leading to outcomes perceived as negative, undesirable, or entirely unforeseen. These occurrences can be understood as errors, and thus it can be stated that errors are a natural component of project work.

However, the mere occurrence of errors is not the decisive factor influencing project outcomes; rather, it is the environment in which errors are addressed that makes the difference. Research has demonstrated that teams often perceived as “better” might initially appear to report more mistakes. Upon deeper investigation, it becomes clear that such teams do not necessarily commit more errors but instead foster an atmosphere that encourages openness in reporting and discussing them. This supportive environment is characterized by psychological safety, where trust and candor enable teams to acknowledge mistakes transparently and learn constructively from them. Thus, the true marker of high-performing teams is not the absence of errors, but their willingness and ability to openly confront and collaboratively learn from these inevitable incidents.  (Edmondson, 2024, pp. 12-15)

Amy Edmondson defines Psychological Safety as an environment where team members feel comfortable taking interpersonal risks, admitting mistakes, and openly discussing errors. Extensive research across multiple fields now confirms that psychologically safe teams perform better, innovate more, experience less burnout, and, in healthcare settings, even achieve lower patient mortality rates. Psychological safety enables groups to fail well by promoting openness, candor, and the willingness to ask for help, ultimately enhancing learning, growth, and overall team effectiveness. (Edmondson, 2024, pp. 15-16)

Moreover, emotional intelligence significantly strengthens trust within project teams. Leaders who effectively manage their own emotions and perceive team members’ emotional states foster environments characterized by openness, clear communication, and effective collaboration, thus providing ideal conditions for psychological safety and candid error reporting. (Castro, et al., 2022)

Psychological safety itself depends fundamentally upon trust. Trust, as a foundational element of successful relationships, collaboration, and leadership, fosters open communication, reduces interpersonal conflict, and cultivates an environment in which individuals feel secure enough to contribute honestly and innovate without fear of negative consequences. The Integrative Model of Organizational Trust by Mayer, Davis, and Schoorman (Mayer, et al., 1995, pp. 709-734) provides a valuable framework for understanding trust in organizational contexts. According to this model, trustworthiness rests upon three key factors:

•	Ability: Demonstrating competence in a specific domain.
•	Benevolence: Showing genuine goodwill toward others.
•	Integrity: Consistently adhering to principles accepted and valued by others.

Recent empirical findings validate the significance of Ability, Benevolence, and Integrity as foundational dimensions for building and maintaining trust within project teams, strongly supporting Mayer et al.’s Integrative Model of Organizational Trust. Additionally, trust in the project manager was empirically confirmed to significantly correlate with overall project success, emphasizing trust as a crucial factor for achieving positive project outcomes. (Castro, et al., 2022)

In practical terms, trust involves a willingness to be vulnerable, confidently expecting that others will act in ways that meet our needs and expectations. Such trust is evidenced by risk-taking behaviors—actions like delegating critical tasks, openly sharing sensitive information, or depending upon team members to follow through reliably, despite uncertainty or potential negative outcomes. Thus, in project contexts, trust functions as the crucial relational underpinning that enables psychological safety and transforms error management into an opportunity for continuous learning and improvement. (Edmondson, 2019, pp. 16-17)

Research explicitly demonstrates that a project manager’s emotional intelligence (EI)—their capacity to understand and manage emotions—is a powerful predictor of trust within teams, reinforcing the critical role emotionally intelligent leadership plays in effective team collaboration and successful project outcomes. (Castro, et al., 2022)

Although trust and psychological safety share much common ground, they are not interchangeable. Psychological safety is experienced at a group level: team members typically hold shared perceptions regarding how safe it feels to speak openly, admit errors, or express concerns. Trust, conversely, exists within interpersonal relationships between individuals or between an individual and an organization, making it more specific and personal; for instance, one might trust one colleague explicitly yet have reservations about another. 

Additionally, psychological safety describes a temporally immediate experience related to anticipated interpersonal reactions—it concerns whether others will offer understanding and support in response to one’s immediate actions or admissions. Trust, by contrast, relates to expectations about whether others will behave reliably or fulfill promises in future interactions. In other words, trust involves extending the benefit of the doubt to others, whereas psychological safety involves expecting others to extend that same benefit of the doubt to oneself. (Edmondson, 2019, pp. 16-17)

Job satisfaction alone was found not to significantly predict project success. Instead, trust was identified as a far more influential interpersonal factor within project environments, underscoring the importance of actively fostering trust within project teams to achieve successful outcomes (Castro, et al., 2022). Given the inherent temporary nature of projects, active and rapid cultivation of trust is particularly essential for enabling effective collaboration and ensuring project success. 


AI and Needs-Focused Leadership

3.6.9	Harnessing AI 

Artificial Intelligence (AI) can play a pivotal role in augmenting a project manager’s capacity to motive and mediate effectively, as well as strengthen the mindset and skillset elements. By serving as an adaptive coach, analytical tool, and even a neutral mediator, AI applications open new possibilities for leaders to deepen self-awareness, identify team members’ needs, and create tailored strategies that address conflicting interests.

AI as a Personal Coach or Mentor

One of the most direct applications of AI in developing a needs-focused leadership style is using AI-powered tools as a virtual coach or mentor:

•	Guided Self-Reflection: AI chatbots or platforms can prompt leaders with reflective questions that enhance emotional integrity and resilience. For example, the project manager can periodically “check in” with an AI tool to articulate current challenges, emotional states, and any “buttons” being pushed.
•	Mindset Development: Through interactive dialogues, AI can help leaders examine their values, biases, or assumptions, encouraging them to adopt more assertive or empathic stances consistent with a needs-focused mindset.
•	Feedback Simulation: AI-driven role-plays can simulate team interactions, offering insights into how particular communication styles may affect others. This simulation helps project managers refine their approach before they face real-life situations.

AI Support in Identifying and Analyzing Needs

The model proposed emphasizes isolating the core needs and distinguishing them from feelings or external triggers (stimuli):

•	Needs Profiling: AI-based questionnaires or sentiment analysis tools can collect qualitative data from team members about their needs, values, and emotional states. The system then highlights patterns or clusters of similar needs.
•	Emotional and Stimuli Separation: By parsing language in text or transcripts, AI can help differentiate between the emotional language (e.g., frustration, excitement) and related triggers or stimuli. This clarity gives the project manager a more focused starting point for defining strategies.
•	Conflict “Button Mapping”: In moments of tension or conflict, AI can be used to map “which buttons are being pushed,” correlating team members’ emotional responses with the needs they perceive as unmet. This provides a structured framework for the project manager to mediate effectively.

Generating Ideas for Meeting Needs

Once needs are identified and analyzed, AI can offer a wide array of suggestions on how to address them:

•	Customized Strategies: Drawing on large databases of leadership practices and psychological research, AI may propose activities, changes in team routines, or communication strategies that align with each individual’s identified needs.
•	Balancing Competing Needs: Through algorithmic comparisons, AI can examine overlapping or conflicting interests within the team and propose integrative solutions that account for shared values or complementary objectives.

AI-Enhanced Collaboration and Neutral Mediation

AI can also serve as an objective “third party” in conflicts or negotiations:

•	Facilitation Tools: AI-driven chat platforms can keep discussions organized, flagging inflammatory language or repeated misunderstandings. This helps ensure a conversation remains civil and needs-focused.
•	Neutral Perspective: By analyzing both sides’ input, AI can surface common ground or make real-time suggestions, mitigating emotional escalation and guiding participants toward constructive outcomes. This not only supports the mediation process but also reinforces trust, especially when human facilitators might unintentionally show bias.
•	Predictive Insights: In ongoing conflicts, AI can offer predictive analysis, highlighting early-warning indicators that unresolved issues might intensify. The project manager can then proactively re-engage the mediation cycle.

Ongoing Dialogue and Self-Awareness

Integrating AI into the mindset and skillset broadens a leader’s capacity for growth:

•	Continuous Check-Ins: Scheduling regular self-assessment sessions with an AI tool—similar to a daily or weekly journal—reinforces the concept of sustaining momentum in motivation and mediation.
•	Cognitive Bias Alerts: As part of fostering mindful decision-making, AI can detect potential biases (e.g., anchoring, overconfidence) by reviewing project-related communications or documentation. This nudges leaders to re-examine assumptions or consider alternative viewpoints.
•	Skill-Building Resources: An AI assistant can curate learning materials—articles, case studies, micro-courses—that target skill gaps (e.g., conflict de-escalation, servant leadership concepts), ensuring that the project manager’s professional development remains ongoing and relevant.


Mindset and Skillset

Self-Awareness and Emotional Integrity

A key element of a needs-focused leadership style is the ability to maintain personal clarity and emotional integrity in interactions with others. Assertiveness supports this by upholding one’s own rights and boundaries, while equally respecting the needs and boundaries of others.

In the TKI Conflict Model, Assertiveness is one of the two fundamental dimensions and plays a key role in achieving a collaborative style of conflict resolution. It reflects the degree to which an individual strives to fulfill their own needs. (Kilmann, 2023, p. 4) The American Psychologist and Psychotherapist Manuel J. Smith described in his “Bill of Assertive Rights” ten core rights that empower individuals to take control of their lives and interactions by asserting their own needs and boundaries without guilt:

1.	You have the right to judge your own behavior, thoughts, and emotions, and to take the responsibility for their initiation and consequences upon yourself.
2.	You have the right to offer no reasons or excuses for justifying your behavior.
3.	You have the right to judge if you are responsible for finding solutions to other people's problems.
4.	You have the right to change your mind.
5.	You have the right to make mistakes—and be responsible for them.
6.	You have the right to say, "I don't know."
7.	You have the right to be independent of the goodwill of others before coping with them.
8.	You have the right to be illogical in making decisions.
9.	You have the right to say, "I don't understand."
10.	You have the right to say, "I don't care."
(Smith, 1975, pp. 28-71)

In leadership and professional environments, assertiveness is shown to strengthen interpersonal communication, reduce conflict, and foster mutual respect. Assertive leaders are better equipped to express criticism constructively, manage emotional responses, and set clear expectations without aggression. Furthermore, assertiveness training improves self-esteem, resilience, and decision-making, all of which are essential traits for effective leadership and collaborative team dynamics. Assertive behavior promotes psychological well-being and enhances social competence—critical foundations for maintaining both personal authenticity and organizational harmony. (Peneva & Mavrodiev, 2013) 

Additionally, assertiveness, particularly when combined with cooperativeness, significantly improves group satisfaction and positively influences how leaders are perceived by their teams. Leaders who effectively blend assertiveness with cooperative strategies are seen as collaborative, respectful, organized, and decisive, creating an environment conducive to high levels of team satisfaction and effective performance. (Lambertz-Berndt & Blight, 2016)

Another important element of the needs-focused leader is Resilience, which is the capacity of a dynamic system to successfully adapt to disruptions that threaten its function, viability, or development (Masten, 2016, p. 27). It examines how individuals can recover and grow stronger after adversity, with factors fostering resilience (among others) such as

•	Close relationships with other capable adults (attachment, social networks)
•	Close friendships and romantic relationships (attachment, peer and family systems)
•	Intelligence and problem-solving skills (learning and thinking systems of the central nervous system)
•	Self-control, emotional regulation, foresight (self-regulation systems of the central nervous system)
•	Achievement motivation (coping motivation and reward systems)
•	Self-efficacy (coping motivation)
•	Trust, hope, and the belief that life has meaning (spiritual and cultural belief systems)
•	Effective environment, collective efficacy (social environment)
(Masten, 2016, p. 148)

The concept of thriving describes an individual’s ability not only to recover but also to surpass their original levels of functioning, effectively growing stronger through repeated exposure to stressful experiences. Thriving emerges from transformational experiences, characterized by cognitive shifts that redefine an individual’s sense of self, priorities, and overall life purpose following profound adversity. It involves acquiring new knowledge, skills, and increased confidence, enhancing one’s capability to handle future stressors effectively.

Core traits consistently associated with resilience and thriving include positive self-esteem, hardiness, strong coping skills, a sense of coherence, self-efficacy, optimism, strong social resources, adaptability, risk-taking, low fear of failure, determination, perseverance, and a high tolerance for uncertainty.

Among these attributes, hardiness deserves special attention due to its critical role in buffering the effects of stress. Hardiness encompasses a commitment to meaningful purpose, a belief in one’s ability to influence outcomes, and a mindset oriented toward learning from experiences. Hardy leaders typically appraise stressful situations as less threatening, leverage effective coping strategies, and sustain a robust sense of personal control, thereby enhancing their resilience capacity significantly. (Ledesma, 2014)

Recent systematic research emphasizes resilience as a critical leadership capability across various organizational contexts, highlighting its positive impact not only at the individual but also at the team and organizational level. Psychological resilience in leadership has been empirically linked to improved work performance, enhanced job satisfaction and employee engagement, greater overall well-being and mental health, and strengthened leadership capacities

Moreover, resilience is increasingly recognized not merely as a static personality trait but as a capability that can be deliberately developed through targeted interventions. These include leadership coaching, mindfulness training, structured reflection on challenging experiences, and fostering supportive work environments characterized by authentic and empowering leadership behaviors.

A particularly valuable approach to building resilience in leaders involves adopting a paradoxical perspective—recognizing adversity simultaneously as a potential source of stress and as an opportunity for growth and learning. This “both-and” mindset supports leaders in reframing challenges constructively, enhancing their adaptive responses, and enabling them to better manage complexity and uncertainty. Such resilient attitudes foster perseverance under high pressures and facilitate more effective use of available resources, ultimately enabling leaders and their teams to thrive even in turbulent conditions. (Yu, et al., 2022)

The Connor-Davidson Resilience Scale (CD-RISC) is a validated instrument designed to quantify resilience by assessing an individual’s ability to cope with adversity and thrive in challenging situations. Developed to measure resilience across clinical and non-clinical populations, the CD-RISC is a 25-item scale scored on a 5-point Likert scale ranging from “not true at all” to “true nearly all of the time.” Higher scores reflect greater resilience.

The scale examines several key dimensions of resilience, including adaptability, confidence, emotional regulation, optimism, perseverance, and the capacity to find meaning or support through relationships and belief systems. For example, it evaluates aspects such as one’s ability to adapt to change, achieve goals, handle unpleasant feelings, maintain close relationships, and rely on spiritual or cultural beliefs for strength. 

In the context of leadership, the CD-RISC can be a valuable tool to assess and support the development of resilience among team members. It can help identify areas of strength and areas needing improvement, guiding leaders in tailoring their support to the needs of individuals. Furthermore, it provides a structured way to evaluate the impact of interventions aimed at enhancing resilience, making it a practical addition to research and practice in needs-focused leadership. (Connor & Davidson, 2003, pp. 76-82)
A supporting mechanism to maintain emotional integrity is the concept of Self-Affirmation, developed by the American social psychologist Claude Steele. He proposes the existence of a self-system that fundamentally explains ourselves, and the world at large, to ourselves, to maintain a coherent self-perception. The purpose of these continuous explanations and rationalizations is to sustain a sense of the self as adaptively and morally adequate—competent, good, coherent, stable, unified, capable of free choice, and in control of important outcomes. This mechanism allows us to retain (or regain) integrity by focusing on other self-valued elements at a global level, instead of analyzing and solving any little issue or conflict. Interestingly the affirmation does not need to be related to the same context. In other words: People cope with a particular threat by reinforcing different, unrelated self-aspects. (Steele, 1988, p. 262)

Recent research confirms that these personality traits significantly influence leadership effectiveness and employee job performance. What type of person am I?

While personality models vary in their scientific validation and acceptance, the vast number of such models available supports self-awareness and personal development. By helping individuals identify their strengths, values, interests, positions, needs, and competencies, these models can play a vital role in fostering a needs-focused leadership approach. 

Some of the most well-known models include 

•	the Big Five Personality Traits (Five-Factor Model), recognized for its strong scientific foundation
•	the DISC Personality Assessment (Marston, 1928, pp. 107-111), commonly applied in organizational contexts for improving communication and leadership
•	and the Process Communication Model (PCM) by Taibi Kahler, which identifies six personality types (Kahler, 2008, pp. 40-42) and focuses on communication styles, motivational needs, and stress behavior.

While the Myers-Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI) (The Myers-Briggs Company, 2025) is widely used for personal development and career guidance, it lacks sufficient psychometric reliability and validity, rendering it unsuitable for rigorous psychological assessment (Boyle, 1995), demonstrates only a weak statistical relationship with leadership-related behaviors, thereby limiting its predictive usefulness in organizational contexts (Zárate-Torres & Correa, 2023), and ultimately fails fundamental criteria of scientific validity, including empirical support, internal consistency, and testability (Stein & Swan, 2019).

Among the various models discussed, Daniel Hendling particularly highlights the PCM model due to its numerous elements relevant to needs-focused leadership. PCM emphasizes that everyone possesses a unique combination of six personality types. Importantly, PCM describes personality types within people rather than categorizing people into fixed types. Each personality type is equally valuable—none is inherently better or worse, smarter or less intelligent, or acceptable than any other.

Each type features specific motivators, characterized by unique strengths, psychological needs, perceptions, and preferred communication channels. Understanding and respecting these differences is essential for effectively connecting with others, fostering motivation, and resolving conflicts. (Kahler Communications, 2025)

•	Thinker: Perceives through thinking. Identifies and classifies people and objects logically. Organized, responsible, and precise. Needs recognition for achievements and structured time.
•	Persister: Perceives through opinions and makes judgments by evaluating people and situations. Dedicated, observant, and conscientious. Expresses beliefs clearly. Needs recognition for convictions and achievements.
•	Harmonizer: Perceives through feelings and emotions. Compassionate, sensitive, and warmhearted. Creates harmony and cares deeply for others. Needs personal recognition and sensory stimulation.
•	Promoter: Perceives through actions. Charming, adaptable, and persuasive, interacts directly and decisively. Thrives on excitement.
•	Rebel: Perceives through reactions, expressing immediate likes and dislikes. Playful, humorous, enjoys living in the moment. Needs contact and interaction.
•	Imaginer: Perceives through inaction, requiring external motivation to act. Capable of seeing the big picture and skilled at practical tasks when guided. Needs solitude and quiet reflection.
(Kahler, 2017, pp. 58-60; 137)

Recent studies highlight the ongoing relevance and practical utility of the Process Communication Model (PCM), particularly in high-stress professional contexts. Healthcare professionals report sustained use of PCM for effective communication, conflict management, and stress reduction, positively influencing professional and personal relationships (Andrew, et al., 2021).

A large-scale efficacy survey further confirmed PCM’s lasting impact, with the majority of users regularly applying PCM concepts years after initial training. PCM remains widely used across various contexts, including team dynamics, leadership, and personal interactions, emphasizing consistent practice to enhance effectiveness. (Kahler Communcation, 2021) 

Overall, PCM continues to provide essential tools for improving communication, managing interpersonal conflicts, and fostering effective leadership.

In this thesis, personality profiling tools are utilized to systematically identify and analyze stakeholder needs. One such tool, the Process Communication Model (PCM), provides a scientifically validated framework with strong empirical support, offering robust insights into individual needs and communication patterns.

In contrast, Daniel Hendling’s Spectrum of Needs model is a pragmatic, experience-based approach designed explicitly for immediate practical use. Although it lacks formal empirical validation, this model offers intuitive, flexible, and context-specific guidance at no cost. While not intended to categorize personality “types” in the strict sense, it still provides valuable insights into individual preferences by highlighting their most prominent needs. An accompanying online tool enables users to create personalized “need profiles,” which can then be further analyzed through self-reflection, during coaching sessions, or with the assistance of AI.

3.6.2	Communication and Feedback

Effective leadership and collaboration hinge on communication and feedback. These elements are crucial for understanding and addressing individual and team needs, fostering trust, and resolving or preventing conflicts. 

Understanding other people’s needs and feeling needs exceptional Communication Skills, with Listening being one of the key elements. With his “Theory U” C. Otto Schamer identifies four levels of listening. Each level builds on the previous, moving from reinforcing the known, to observing and questioning, to emotionally connecting, and finally to co-creating and transforming. The fourth level, generative listening, is marked by a profound change in the listener, leaving them with a new sense of self and purpose. (Scharmer, 2016, pp. 11-12)

DOWNLOADING: The shallowest level, where listening serves to confirm existing beliefs and judgments. It’s habitual and doesn’t involve openness to new insights.
FACTUAL LISTENING: A shift to objective observation, focusing on new and disconfirming data. This mode is essential for science and involves suspending personal judgments to engage with external facts.
EMPATHIC LISTENING: The listener connects emotionally and intuitively with another person or living system. This involves an open heart and the capacity to perceive the world through the perspective of the other. It requires letting go of one’s agenda to fully understand the other’s experience.
GENERATIVE LISTENING: Listening transcends the present moment and connects to the “emerging field of the future.” It involves open heart and will, creating a space where transformative insights and profound self-awareness emerge. At this level, the listener and the situation transform together, creating a sense of connection with a larger purpose or authentic self.

When a project involves people with diverse personalities, attitudes, and communication styles, the risk of misunderstandings increases. To ensure effective communication and mutual understanding, it is crucial to prevent misunderstandings that could lead to motivational issues or conflicts. This becomes particularly problematic when the source of the misunderstanding is not the content of the message itself, but the choice of words and their perceived meaning. The same statement can be interpreted in entirely different ways depending on the communication style of those involved, further complicating collaboration. 

The German psychologist Friedemann Schulz von Thun discovered that every message could have four distinct dimensions of meaning:

•	Factual content: The factual information conveyed in the message, focusing on objective data or the topic being communicated, such as “The traffic light is green.”
•	Self-Revelation: The information the sender reveals about themselves, whether intentionally or unintentionally, reflecting personal traits, emotions, or intentions, such as “The sender is attentive and perhaps in a hurry.”
•	Relationship: The part of the message that indicates what the sender thinks of the recipient and their relationship, often expressed through tone, wording, or non-verbal cues, such as “The sender thinks the recipient needs assistance.”
•	Appeal: The aspect of the message intended to influence or motivate the recipient to act or respond in a specific way, often implicitly aiming to achieve a desired outcome, such as “Pay attention to the traffic light.”
(Schulz von Thun, 2023, pp. 27-33)

It is important to recognize which side of a message is intended by the sender and which side is perceived by the receiver to avoid misunderstandings, foster effective communication, and develop awareness of whether certain “mouths” (how we express) or “ears” (how we interpret) are overused or underdeveloped in interactions.

Effective listening closely relates to giving and receiving feedback effectively. Although feedback’s concept and purpose may initially seem straightforward, various complexities can arise. Factors such as cultural differences in feedback styles can complicate the process (Meyer, 2015, p. 62), and feedback discussions may surface dissatisfaction or previously hidden conflicts.

Feedback can broadly be categorized into two distinct perspectives:

•	Feedback for the sake of the recipient: Intended to help the recipient learn and grow, provided specifically upon invitation and delivered in a descriptive, supportive manner. The recipient controls timing, format, and source, usually expressing appreciation afterward.
•	Feedback for the sake of the sender: Primarily driven by the sender’s need to express their own emotional discomfort or irritation, characterized by negative emotions like anger. This form requires conscious emotional validation to manage and resolve underlying emotional tensions (Salters-Pedneault, 2022). 

In practical settings, the coexistence of these two perspectives can lead to misunderstandings or conflict, especially when recipients expect neutral, constructive feedback while senders express critical or emotionally charged comments.

There are different types of feedback, which might be understood to form “loops.” Single-loop feedback involves making immediate adjustments to one’s behavior to correct an issue. Double-loop feedback goes further by challenging and potentially transforming underlying structures or strategies. Finally, triple-loop feedback (or “super-vision”) adds another layer: it requires ongoing awareness of how one’s attention, strategies, actions, and effects on the outside world interrelate in real time. This deeper awareness allows leaders to integrate multiple perspectives—technical, emotional, and relational—thereby enabling more holistic and transformative responses. (Torbert, 2004, pp. 18-21)

In recent years, especially since the Covid-19 crisis, the use of virtual communication tools (e.g., video calls) has increased. Video-based teams, while able to accomplish tasks similarly to co-located teams in quantitative terms, often report feeling less socially bonded and confident in their teammates. Consistent research indicates that virtual teams experience lower levels of trust compared to their in-person counterparts. Although visual cues available through video communication (seeing teammates’ faces) help mitigate this deficit the nuanced camaraderie and cohesion typical of face-to-face interactions remain diminished (Baker, 2018). These findings underline the continued importance of in-person communication for building strong relationships and trust within teams.

Alongside video calls, text messaging tools like WhatsApp and MS Teams have also seen increased adoption, particularly due to several practical factors:

•	Members frequently working remotely from home.
•	Teams distributed across multiple locations, especially in international projects.
•	Participants collaborating asynchronously across varying time zones.
•	Individual or generational preferences favoring these tools, driven by features like easy document and media sharing, and convenient asynchronous interactions.

Despite traditional beliefs that richer communication channels inherently produce superior outcomes, recent studies indicate that instant messaging can be equally effective, underscoring the adaptability and practicality of digital communication in contemporary collaboration contexts. (Tang & Bradshaw, 2020) 

However, while the distinct advantages of face-to-face communication remain clear, integrating text-based communication alongside video or audio channels has been shown to further enhance virtual team performance and satisfaction by clarifying information and facilitating better overall communication. (Shwartz-Asher & Ahituv, 2020)

3.6.3	Cognitive Practices and Decision-Making

Jon Kabat-Zinn describes Mindfulness in the way that it means paying attention in a particular way:

•	On purpose
•	In the present moment
•	Without judgment

Other words for this practice would be “awareness” or “heartfulness” (Kabat-Zinn, 2020, p. xxxvii). As the developer of the “Mindset-based Stress Reduction” program he sees these key attitudes as the foundation of mindfulness practice:

1.	Non-judging: becoming aware of the mind’s habitual tendency to label experiences as good, bad, or neutral and intentionally suspending judgment to observe what unfolds with impartiality and clarity.
2.	Patience: The wisdom to accept that things unfold in their own time, allowing us to be fully open to each moment, including challenges, without rushing or resisting the natural process of life.
3.	Beginner’s mind: The openness to see everything as if for the first time, free from preconceived notions, allowing us to fully experience the richness and uniqueness of each moment.
4.	Trust: Cultivating confidence in one’s own intuition, feelings, and wisdom, taking responsibility for being authentic and honoring personal experiences, while recognizing external guidance as a supportive tool in the personal journey.
5.	Non-striving: The practice of letting go of the urge to achieve specific outcomes, focusing instead on fully accepting and being present with things as they are, allowing natural unfolding to occur without force or effort.
6.	Acceptance: Seeing things as they truly are in the present moment, without denial or resistance, creating the conditions for healing, growth, and appropriate action while staying open and receptive to change.
7.	Letting go: The practice of releasing attachments to thoughts, feelings, and experiences—whether pleasant or unpleasant—allowing things to be as they are and observing them without clinging or resisting.

In addition to elements such as non-harming, generosity, gratitude, forbearance, forgiveness, kindness, compassion, empathic joy, and equanimity. (Kabat-Zinn, 2020, pp. 21-31) 

He also highlights the importance of what he calls “problem-focused coping” and “emotion-focused coping”, emphasizing the importance of addressing both the external problem and the internal emotional response. Problem-focused coping involves identifying the issue, breaking it into manageable parts, and taking practical actions to resolve it, while also understanding when non-doing is the most appropriate response. Emotion-focused coping involves bringing mindful awareness to one’s feelings, observing them without judgment, and reframing them with self-compassion and a broader perspective. Combining these approaches allows for balanced, mindful responses to emotional pain and challenging situations, fostering clarity, resilience, and effective action. (Kabat-Zinn, 2020, pp. 420-427) While these elements stem from Kabat-Zinn’s stress reduction program, they can enhance a needs-focused approach in project leadership by fostering clarity, emotional intelligence, and alignment with team and stakeholder needs.

Empirical evidence further supports this alignment. Mindfulness significantly enhances leaders’ emotional intelligence, self-regulation, and stress resilience, improving their capacity to manage relationships and perform effectively under stress. Leaders trained in mindfulness experience increased empathy and compassion, leading to stronger and more constructive relationships with their employees. Mindfulness strengthens practical leadership competencies such as managing ambiguity, effective communication, and the ability to adopt diverse perspectives, thus aligning well with contemporary, dynamic work environments. (Tenschert, et al., 2024)

In their book about Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy (MBCT) Zindel Segal et.al. describe two distinct mental modes, each having a different impact on awareness and empathy:

The Driven-Doing mode is fundamentally goal-oriented, focusing on closing the gap between how things are and how we want them to be. This mode constantly monitors discrepancies, evaluating progress toward achieving goals. It often operates with a narrow attention span, fixated on specific aspects of the present that relate to goal achievement, while largely ignoring the broader richness of the moment. Its time focus is primarily on the past, through analysis, or the future, through planning, leaving little room for fully experiencing the present. In this mode, thoughts and feelings are treated as accurate reflections of reality and are often judged as “good” or “bad,” which can perpetuate rumination and negative emotional states, particularly when applied to internal, self-related goals. (Segal, et al., 2013, pp. 68-72)

In contrast, the Being mode is rooted in acceptance and allows experiences to unfold without the need to evaluate or change them. It emphasizes being fully present in the moment, embracing the full depth and richness of what is happening here and now. Unlike the Driven-Doing mode, it treats thoughts and feelings as transient mental events rather than fixed realities, fostering a sense of freedom and responsiveness. The Being mode reduces the tendency to cling to or avoid emotions, promoting a balanced, non-judgmental awareness. (Segal, et al., 2013, pp. 72-74)

When it comes to focusing on human needs, the Being mode enhances a needs-focused leadership approach by cultivating non-judgmental awareness and presence, empowering leaders to more effectively recognize, understand, and authentically respond to the needs of both themselves and others.

The Five Facet Mindfulness Questionnaire (FFMQ) is a self-report tool that measures five core aspects of mindfulness: 

•	Observing: Observing refers to the deliberate awareness of sensory experiences, such as bodily sensations, sounds, smells, and visual details, as well as noticing how emotions and external stimuli affect thoughts and behaviors.
•	Describing: Describing involves the ability to clearly articulate emotions, thoughts, and bodily sensations, even during challenging moments, while overcoming difficulties in finding the right words.
•	Acting with Awareness: Acting with awareness focuses on staying present and attentive during activities, avoiding distractions, automatic behaviors, and mindless rushing through tasks.
•	Nonjudging of Inner Experience: Nonjudging of inner experience emphasizes accepting emotions and thoughts without labeling them as good or bad, avoiding self-criticism or judgment about having “inappropriate” feelings.
•	Nonreactivity to Inner Experience: Nonreactivity to inner experience involves observing and acknowledging thoughts and emotions without being overwhelmed or compelled to react, allowing for emotional stability and calmness.
(Baer, et al., 2006, pp. 27-45)

It uses 39 items (in its original form) to assess how present and aware we are in everyday life, how openly and without judgment we acknowledge our thoughts and emotions, and how we choose our actions (rather than just reacting on autopilot). Its scoring process combines directly scored and reverse-scored items, offering insight into the individual’s current mindful state as well as the potential impact of any previous mindful practices. Research suggests that regular mindfulness practice correlates with improved wellbeing, resilience, and positive mood regulation. (Chowdhury, 2019)

Cognitive debiasing refers to the deliberate and systematic process of identifying, understanding, and reducing the influence of cognitive biases—mental shortcuts or patterns of thinking that can lead to errors in judgment, reasoning, and decision-making. Biases can manifest as a tendency to favor or dislike a person, thing, or idea, often as a result of preconceived opinions, partiality, or prejudice. These preferences or attitudes can affect outlook and behavior, particularly by inhibiting impartial consideration or judgment. (Oxford University Press, 2025)

Cognitive debiasing seeks to address these biases by fostering awareness, implementing strategies to mitigate their effects, and promoting objective analysis. This process is not about eliminating biases entirely—which may be impractical due to their unconscious nature—but about limiting their influence to enhance fairness, rationality, and integrity in decision-making. Strategies for cognitive debiasing include metacognitive practices, structured decision-making tools, and feedback mechanisms, as well as collaboration with diverse teams to uncover blind spots.

Importantly, cognitive debiasing holds particular value in social contexts, where biases can significantly disrupt interpersonal interactions, empathy, and communication. Addressing biases in these contexts enhances social-emotional well-being, reduces social anxiety, and strengthens overall emotional health. Research demonstrates that even brief, focused educational interventions that teach individuals about cognitive biases and practical mitigation strategies can lead to substantial and lasting reductions in biases. Such interventions have proven effective for several months afterward, underscoring their potential to create long-term improvements in both individual and collective social functioning. (Birch, et al., 2025)

Important biases to be aware of—and ideally overcome—include:

•	Strategic misrepresentation: The tendency to deliberately and systematically distort or misstate information for strategic purposes. Aka political bias, strategic bias, or power bias. (Flyvbjerg, 2021, p. 534) It may undermine trust and transparency, thereby hampering genuine identification and understanding of stakeholders’ needs.
•	Halo effect (exaggerated emotional coherence): The tendency to like (or dislike) everything about a person—including things one has not observed. (Kahnemann, 2024, pp. 94-98) It may lead leaders to superficial judgments, reducing their ability to discern and genuinely address individual stakeholder needs.
•	Confirmation bias: Confirmation bias is a tendency of both intuitive and reflective thinking to search for, recall, and favor information that supports existing beliefs, expectations, or the way a question is framed — often at the expense of critical or disconfirming evidence. (Kahnemann, 2024, pp. 93-94) It may limit openness to alternative perspectives, impairing an accurate understanding of stakeholder needs.
•	Anchoring: The tendency to rely too heavily, or “anchor,” on one trait or piece of information when making decisions, typically the first piece of information acquired on the relevant subject. (Flyvbjerg, 2021, pp. 540-541) It may limit leaders’ openness to new information about stakeholder needs, preventing adaptive and responsive decision-making based on an evolving understanding of these needs.
•	Overconfidence bias: The tendency to have excessive confidence in one’s own answers to questions. (Flyvbjerg, 2021, pp. 538-540) It may lead leaders to overestimate their understanding of others’ needs, reducing empathy and hindering genuine inquiry into stakeholder perspectives.
•	Escalation of commitment: The tendency to justify increased investment in a decision, based on the cumulative prior investment, despite new evidence suggesting the decision may be wrong. Also known as the sunk cost fallacy. (Flyvbjerg, 2021, pp. 541-542) It may encourage leaders to persist with ineffective strategies or assumptions about stakeholder needs despite contrary evidence, thus blocking the flexibility and responsiveness essential to needs-focused approaches.
•	Negativity bias: The cognitive tendency where negative events and feedback affect individuals more strongly and persistently than equivalent positive events. (Pilat, et al., 2021) Similarly, negative events, emotions, and feedback have stronger and more lasting effects on individuals than positive counterparts. (Baumeister & Finkenauer, 2001)
•	Pessimism bias: The cognitive tendency to overestimate the likelihood of negative outcomes while underestimating positive ones. (Pilat & Krastev, 2021) 
•	Loss aversion: The psychological tendency where losses feel emotionally stronger than equivalent gains, causing people to avoid risks even when potential gains outweigh potential losses. (Kahnemann, 2024, pp. 323-326) As with the negativity and pessimism biases, leaders may overly prioritize avoiding negative outcomes, restricting them from pursuing opportunities beneficial to stakeholder needs.
•	Spotlight effect: The cognitive tendency to overestimate how much one’s actions and appearance are noticed and evaluated by others, especially in potentially unfavorable situations. This can lead leaders to become overly self-conscious, misdirecting attention towards managing their own perceived image rather than genuinely focusing on understanding and addressing stakeholders’ actual needs. (Birch, et al., 2025)
•	Curse of knowledge bias: The inclination to assume that others possess or understand one’s own level of knowledge. Leaders experiencing this bias may overestimate stakeholders’ comprehension or acceptance of specialized information, hindering clear communication, creating misunderstandings, and impairing accurate identification and responsiveness to stakeholders’ true needs. (Birch, et al., 2025)
•	Hostile attribution bias: The propensity to interpret ambiguous or neutral social cues as intentionally aggressive or hostile. This bias may cause leaders to prematurely attribute negative intentions to stakeholders’ actions or comments, resulting in misinterpretations, conflict escalation, and impaired relationships, thereby obstructing accurate and empathetic recognition of stakeholders’ genuine needs. (Birch, et al., 2025)

Similar to cognitive debiasing—and equally important—is managing cognitive distortions. These are thought patterns that alter our perception of reality, often leading us into overly negative thinking. Common examples include:

•	All-or-nothing thinking: Viewing situations or people strictly as good or bad, with no nuance.
•	Overgeneralization: Drawing broad conclusions from a single negative event, potentially leading to anxiety or avoidance behaviors.
•	Filtering: Focusing exclusively on negative aspects while ignoring positive details.
•	Discounting the positive: Actively dismissing positive experiences as mere luck or coincidence rather than personal achievement.
•	Jumping to conclusions: Interpreting situations negatively without evidence, often escalating into catastrophizing.
•	Magnification: Exaggerating negatives and minimizing positives.
•	Emotional reasoning: Judging reality based solely on emotional reactions (e.g., feeling guilty means you must be bad).
•	‘Should’ statements: Rigidly adhering to beliefs about what one “should” do, leading to unnecessary guilt or stress.
•	Labeling: Defining someone based on isolated actions rather than context.
•	Personalization: Taking excessive personal blame for negative events or outcomes.
(Jones, 2022)

3.6.5	Understanding Humor in the Workplace

There are various types of humor, which can be categorized into four main groups:

•	Incongruity theories focus on humor's cognitive aspects, suggesting laughter arises from an unexpected clash between competing ideas or expectations. The greater the discrepancy, the funnier the situation, provided the incongruity is harmless. Examples include jokes or physical humor, such as slipping on a banana peel, causing laughter through unexpected outcomes (Plester, 2016, pp. 18-20)
•	Superiority theories propose that humor emerges from feelings of superiority when ridiculing or mocking others’ misfortune or embarrassment. Originating from philosophers like Plato, Aristotle, and notably Hobbes, these theories emphasize aggression and playful dominance. Although associated negatively with cruelty, they highlight humor’s complexity and dual nature. (Plester, 2016, pp. 20-22)
•	Relief or release theories, closely associated with Freud, view humor as a psychological outlet relieving tension through laughter. Freud argued that jokes involving taboo topics, like sexuality or aggression, safely release repressed emotions, providing relief and pleasure. Contemporary interpretations suggest that targeted humor can foster inclusion and camaraderie despite potential offense. (Plester, 2016, pp. 22-25)
•	Relational humor emphasizes humor’s social dimension, highlighting interactions between two or more people. Shared humor fosters community, pleasure, and bonding. Humorists often use humor altruistically to enhance relationships and strengthen social connections. (Plester & Inkson, 2019, p. 32)

A good sense of humor is beneficial for effective leadership, complementing communication, motivation, and conflict management skills. Supervisors perceived as having a positive sense of humor typically receive higher ratings in leadership effectiveness, employee satisfaction, and interpersonal skills. Conversely, inappropriate humor negatively affects leadership ratings. (Martin, 2007, pp. 367-368)

Workplace humor provides many previously discussed benefits: improved morale, enhanced group cohesion, better interpersonal relationships, and relief from stress or challenging circumstances through “gallows humor.” Shared laughter positively influences workplace dynamics, offering health benefits and serving as a coping mechanism to reframe negative experiences.

Humor, particularly self-enhancing humor, can also effectively reduce negative thinking patterns (cognitive distortions) by helping individuals adopt a cheerful outlook, thereby reducing emotional distress and promoting psychological resilience. (Rnic, et al., 2016)

While humor might be seen as a distraction or waste of productive time, research indicates that employees naturally engage in non-work activities regularly, and humor positively contributes during these moments. Although humor’s direct impact on productivity remains unclear, strong evidence suggests it significantly enhances workplace effectiveness, satisfaction, and group performance. (Plester & Inkson, 2019, pp. 117-123)

Humor also serves as a valuable indicator of organizational culture, reflecting shared values, norms, and behaviors, and revealing whether an organization emphasizes collaboration, competitiveness, or mutual support. Analyzing workplace humor thus provides important insights into organizational culture and dynamics. (Martin, 2007, p. 365)

Humor in workplaces is governed by informal, culturally shared boundaries defining acceptable behavior. Employees commonly recognize when humor becomes inappropriate, using expressions like “crossing the line.” These implicit boundaries are dynamic and context-dependent. Humor exceeding these boundaries—such as sexual, political, or racist jokes—can trigger strong emotional responses and necessitate boundary renegotiation. (Plester & Inkson, 2019, p. 87) 

Finally, managers, including project managers, must recognize humor’s inevitability and the balance of benefits and risks outlined previously. They must clearly understand their responsibilities—carefully observing humor, promoting its positive use, intervening when necessary, and fostering a respectful, inclusive, and productive workplace culture. (Plester & Inkson, 2019, pp. 12-13)

3.6.6	Neurodiversity on the rise

Neurodivergence describes individuals whose brains function differently from societal norms. Importantly, terms like “neurodivergent” do not mean “abnormal,” and “neurotypical” should not imply “normal.” Similarly, terms like “autistic” are preferred over phrasing such as “person with autism” to avoid framing neurotypes as disorders (Wendler, 2023).

The NHS estimates that one in seven people in the UK are neurodivergent, encompassing conditions like autism and ADHD, which affect how the brain processes information. However, demand for services is overwhelming, with 205,000 patients on NHS waiting lists for autism referrals and ADHD diagnosis delays of up to 10 years in some areas. While government initiatives aim to improve employment outcomes, barriers remain in translating awareness into meaningful inclusion (Borrett, 2024).

Neurodiversity considerations affect several aspects mentioned, such as self-awareness, communication, decision-making, and project execution. Neurodivergent individuals may face unique challenges that cut across boundaries of mindset and skillset, requiring tailored leadership approaches.

Building psychological safety is essential for supporting neurodiverse employees, with manager training and equipping frontline leaders to navigate employee experiences and provide appropriate supports being critical (McDowall, et al., 2023, p. 6). Communication challenges with neurodivergent individuals, such as those with autism, often stem from factors like information overload, intensive conversations with few breaks, excessive questioning, reliance on body language, and noisy environments (National Autistic Society, 2020). Those with ADHD may experience difficulties with concentration, organization, impulsivity, emotional regulation, and hyperactivity, making communication and focus challenging (ADHD Aware, 2025).

Additionally, many neurodivergent individuals are accustomed to “masking” or “holding themselves together” in social or professional settings—often due to years of navigating environments that did not accommodate their needs. This habitual self-suppression can make it more difficult for them to open up or express needs openly, even in teams with high psychological safety. Leaders should be aware that genuine unlocking may require extra patience, trust-building, and explicit permission to not conform.

Although this topic merits a more in-depth exploration than provided here, recognizing the distinct needs and perspectives of neurodivergent individuals can strengthen a leader’s ability to foster a truly inclusive, needs-based environment. Project managers who acknowledge and adapt to neurodiverse challenges are better positioned to motivate their teams effectively and mediate conflicts constructively.

3.6.7	Mental Health Awareness

Mental Health Awareness is increasingly recognized as an essential competence for effective leadership. While leaders frequently assume coaching roles, fostering both personal and professional growth among their team members, they must simultaneously remain mindful of clear interpersonal boundaries. Project managers, however, often hesitate to engage deeply with emotional dynamics, fearing they might inadvertently step into therapeutic roles or become overwhelmed by personal emotional issues. This tension underscores the need for structured approaches that allow leaders to responsibly address mental health without compromising professional boundaries.

Training in Mental Health First Aid (MHFA) proves invaluable in navigating these challenges. MHFA courses, increasingly available internationally, are akin to traditional physical first aid but specifically address mental health conditions such as depression, anxiety disorders, trauma, psychosis, and substance use disorders. Research highlights that mental health training significantly improves organizational outcomes, including enhanced staff recruitment and retention, better business performance, and reduced long-term sickness absence. (Hassard, et al., 2024)

Further studies emphasize that effective manager mental health training should focus on building managerial confidence and reducing stigma to positively influence workplace practices (Bryan, et al., 2018). Leaders trained in MHFA develop essential skills enabling them to strategically assess when deeper emotional interventions—often termed "red couch discussions"—are beneficial or when simpler solutions suffice for immediate operational needs. Such strategic judgment safeguards leaders' emotional integrity and helps maintain clear professional boundaries.

Moreover, effective mental health-sensitive leadership has been shown to positively influence teamwork by reducing interpersonal conflict and enhancing cooperation, especially during crises (Paganin, et al., 2023). Leaders must finely tune their awareness to distinguish professional openness from personal privacy and therapeutic intervention. This distinction represents empathy and a crucial leadership competency.

Leaders’ own health orientation significantly impacts their teams. A leader's self-care habits strongly correlate with their staff-care orientation, influencing team engagement and reducing exhaustion through improved management of job resources and demands (Grimm, et al., 2021). This underscores the value of leaders modeling health-oriented behaviors, reinforcing a culture of wellness within organizations.

In conclusion, Mental Health Awareness training (particularly MHFA) equips leaders to maintain emotional integrity, respect necessary professional boundaries, and foster healthier workplace environments. It supports leaders in sensitively and effectively managing team emotional dynamics, benefiting both individual team members and organizational performance.

4.1.3	Motivation

This chapter examines how Motivation can be effectively built and sustained within project teams, emphasizing trust as the foundational element. It further highlights needs-focused leadership strategies that proactively address individual motivations, and discusses emotional frontloading, a strategic investment in building early emotional connections to enhance long-term team cohesion and efficiency.

Trust

Trust within teams is primarily built through reliability, honesty, authenticity, transparency, and integrity. These foundational qualities, while time-consuming to establish, can be rapidly lost, requiring continuous care and vigilance. As one interviewee succinctly put it: “Trust is earned in drops and lost in buckets.”

Trust directly fosters psychological safety, a condition where team members feel genuinely supported, understood, and valued. Leaders influence this atmosphere through consistent emotional responses, authentic behavior, and explicitly addressing biases and stereotypes. A deliberate reflective pause before acting or responding emerges as an essential leadership behavior, effectively managing emotional triggers and enhancing informed decision-making.

Such a trust-rich environment not only facilitates successful conflict mediation by encouraging open dialogue and early conflict detection but also allows humor to naturally thrive, signaling comfort and intimacy among team members. Additionally, trustful environments are particularly crucial for neurodivergent team members, as they provide the safety needed to communicate unique needs openly.

Finally, ethical awareness in leadership practices is essential; manipulation risks severely undermining carefully built trust, highlighting the necessity for responsible and conscientious leadership behaviors.

The MOVE model strategically integrates trust-building into its mindset and skillset, recognizing trust as foundational for sustainable team effectiveness and project success, with psychological safety enabling team members to openly address their concerns. Central to this is the deliberate reflective pause, fostering emotional integrity by allowing effective management of emotional triggers and reinforcing trust. 

The model treats trust-building as a strategic, long-term investment, enhancing project outcomes through stronger team cohesion, resilience, open communication, and humor. By explicitly fostering trust and psychological safety, the MOVE model promotes inclusive collaboration, especially benefiting neurodivergent team members who thrive in environments accommodating their unique needs.

Needs-Focused Leadership and Motivation

Effective team motivation hinges on explicitly addressing and proactively fulfilling both personal and professional needs. Team members are inherently complex, with fluctuating needs shaped by their momentary context. Recognizing and adapting to these dynamic needs—particularly fundamental psychological motivators such as recognition, autonomy, mastery, and personal growth—leads to more sustained motivation compared to superficial incentives.

Proactively identifying and addressing these deeper needs early on, a process coined as “Emotional Frontloading”, establishes resilience that simplifies conflict resolution, making it more efficient and less disruptive. Non-Violent Communication (NVC) emerges as a particularly practical and effective tool, enabling leaders to understand and respond empathetically to team members’ underlying motivations, thus enhancing psychological safety and reducing the likelihood of conflicts.

Explicitly communicating the project’s relevance and clearly aligning it with the organization’s strategic goals is critical, as it substantially boosts motivation by helping team members connect their efforts to meaningful, overarching objectives. Equally, equitable treatment across team members fosters a sense of fairness and trust, further deepening team cohesion and commitment.

Organizational constraints, such as limited direct managerial authority in matrix environments, require leaders to rely on proactive motivational strategies, including informal interactions and intentional team-building activities, to maintain motivation consistently. Additionally, value-driven management approaches support this effort by translating intangible perceptions of motivation into clear, measurable outcomes. Such transparency reinforces trust, strengthens psychological safety, and sustains long-term motivation and stakeholder satisfaction. 

The MOVE model is explicitly designed as a circular framework, prioritizing ongoing reassessment of team members’ emotional states and dynamic needs over linear checklists. Central to its approach are authentic emotional integrity and genuine empathy, fostering deeper psychological connections beyond surface interactions. By proactively addressing motivational needs, the model enhances emotional resilience and simplifies conflict resolution. 

Inclusivity and equitable treatment are explicitly integrated, offering adaptive flexibility beneficial for neurodivergent team members whose capacities vary. Recognizing psychological safety as foundational, the model emphasizes open communication and trust, while acknowledging organizational power constraints impacting motivational strategies. These insights form the basis for clear hypotheses for empirical validation, alongside a defined skillset crucial to effectively implementing the MOVE approach.

 
Emotional Frontloading and Early Relationship-Building

Emotional frontloading refers to strategically investing substantial initial project time into building emotional connections, trust, and a shared awareness of individual team members’ personal needs, values, and expectations. This approach, derived from the general principle of “front-loading” in project planning, improves motivation by establishing common ground among team members and proactively reduces conflicts, misunderstandings, and motivational issues throughout the project lifecycle.

Such emotional activities should ideally precede traditional technical planning steps, such as scope definition or resource allocation, to lay a solid emotional and relational foundation. A psychologically safe environment, characterized by openness and absence of fear regarding negative consequences, is essential for effective communication and conflict resolution.

The use of formalized tools like a “Team Charter,” which explicitly sets communication rules, collaboration expectations, and shared values, provides clear guidance during difficult phases. Initial kick-off meetings, preferably conducted in person, should explicitly address emotional expectations, individual communication preferences, and stakeholder needs, setting the stage for long-term emotional alignment.

Allocating initial time—potentially up to 70% of early project planning—to emotional frontloading pays off by enhancing overall project efficiency. Strategic team member selection, balancing professional expertise with interpersonal compatibility, further supports team cohesion. Systematically integrating emotional frontloading across all team development phases—forming, storming, norming, and the final project stage—ensures sustained motivation, clear communication, and proactive conflict management.

Emotional frontloading is explicitly integrated into the MOVE model as a foundational precondition for successfully applying a needs-focused, emotionally aware approach in project management. By investing significant initial effort into building emotional connections, trust, and shared awareness of team members’ needs, project leaders establish a strong foundation for effective collaboration and conflict resolution. 

Central to this approach is clearly articulated psychological safety, which enables open communication and proactive engagement, empowering team members to address concerns without fear of negative consequences. Furthermore, the MOVE model explicitly maps Tuckman’s team development phases—forming, storming, norming, and performing—to its own activities, providing structured guidance for developing cohesive, emotionally attuned, and high-performing project teams.

Proactive and Preventive Conflict Management

Conflicts in project settings typically stem from unclear or misaligned goals, roles, processes, and expectations. Organizational structures lacking clarity exacerbate these issues, causing frequent renegotiation and increased stress. Additionally, overlooking team diversity—including cultural and neurodivergent differences—often escalates conflicts, highlighting the importance of proactive awareness and inclusive communication.

Proactive early detection and direct communication about potential conflicts prevent escalation. Project leaders bear the primary responsibility for addressing conflicts swiftly and transparently, emphasizing the importance of viewing conflict management as a core leadership duty rather than an occasional task. Formal mediation, structured similarly to proven mediation frameworks, ensures systematic, sustainable resolution, although teams should initially attempt conflict resolution independently, using self-generated strategies.

Regular project meetings that integrate explicit conflict management discussions facilitate ongoing awareness and timely intervention. Teams benefit from generating their own conflict-resolution approaches, enhancing autonomy and reducing reliance on management intervention. Leveraging experiences and insights through structured knowledge management systems greatly supports preventive conflict management.

Beneficial conflicts, particularly those revolving around technical or innovative debates, can positively impact creativity and productivity if handled respectfully. Clearly communicating strategic goals and aligning them with project activities further prevents conflicts stemming from misunderstandings or misalignment.

The MOVE Model integrates formal mediation frameworks, ensuring project leaders develop structured conflict resolution skills, informal moderation abilities, and effective early conflict detection. It further empowers teams by fostering self-help capabilities, promoting autonomous conflict resolution methods, and reducing dependency on managerial intervention. Explicitly incorporating intercultural competence and enhanced neurodiversity awareness, the model encourages proactive handling of cultural differences and sensitive responses to neurodivergent behaviors, thereby minimizing misunderstandings and conflict. 

Additionally, project managers are guided to embrace proactive mediation and facilitation roles, viewing conflicts positively as opportunities for growth, creativity, and stronger cohesion. Ethical, Psychological, and Societal Considerations of AI Use

AI can operationalize empathy into observable behaviors, enabling convincingly empathetic interactions. Experts caution against developing psychological dependencies on empathy-enhancing AI tools, particularly in leadership contexts, as these might inadvertently hinder leaders from cultivating their own genuine emotional intelligence.

AI also faces critical limitations in interpreting nuanced contextual cues, including unsaid or unwritten elements, often resulting in inaccuracies (“hallucinations”). These limitations pose substantial risks, especially in sensitive leadership scenarios requiring deep contextual understanding.

Biases embedded in AI training datasets, particularly relating to race, culture, and gender, affect AI’s performance in culturally diverse settings. The phenomenon of Anthropomorphism, where humans emotionally bond with machines, is expanding, reshaping human-machine relationships and potentially influencing interpersonal dynamics. Expectations from AI differ notably depending on professional roles and contexts, highlighting the complexity of its integration. Given the uncertain long-term societal impacts of pervasive AI adoption, experts advocate adaptive governance frameworks and continuous regulation (“society in the loop”) to proactively address unforeseen negative consequences.

Ultimately, AI should serve primarily as a supportive “co-pilot,” carefully avoiding becoming a central mechanism in emotional management. Preserving genuine emotional development, self-regulation, and human relational interactions remains essential. Therefore, rigorous psychological health training, emotional self-awareness, and resilience are critical to successfully navigate and adapt to an AI-driven environment.

Socio-Technical Integration and the Future of Human-Robot Collaboration

Interviewees anticipate a collaborative future where humans and AI-enabled robots operate as co-intelligent team members, including within managerial functions. AI emerges as an indispensable support for managerial decision-making through advanced analytics and storytelling, while humans maintain ultimate ethical responsibility.

Personalized AI assistants are expected to become widespread, providing individually optimized support through advanced memory capabilities and enhanced contextual understanding. However, the rapid pace of technological evolution implies continuous professional learning and adaptation, potentially requiring reskilling as frequently as every six months.

Effective integration of robotics into teams depends on socio-technical alignment, emphasizing participative, co-creative processes. These processes must carefully balance human elements, including distinct team perspectives and expectations, alongside technical capabilities to ensure acceptance and mitigate resistance.

Furthermore, robots can be designed with incentives and disincentives to foster empathetic, trustworthy behaviors, positioning mutual trust between intelligent robots and humans as essential for successful collaboration. With increasing capabilities, robots may gain decision-making autonomy in more complex scenarios, potentially surpassing traditional ethical frameworks such as Asimov’s laws of robotics. 


1.	A robot must not injure a human being, or allow a human being to come to harm.
2.	A robot must obey the orders given by human beings unless they conflict with the First Law.
3.	A robot must protect itself as long as such protection does not conflict with the First or Second Laws.

This advancement raises critical ethical considerations and necessitates balanced human oversight and a careful reassessment of moral responsibilities.

The MOVE element explicitly integrates AI proficiency, emphasizing the importance of equipping leaders with skills to effectively utilize AI tools for enhancing emotional intelligence, self-awareness, and adaptive management. Concrete examples of AI applications have been embedded within the model, clearly illustrating AI’s practical value in supporting leadership and interpersonal dynamics. 

Additionally, integrating co-intelligent AI into stakeholder mediation enhances needs identification and analysis through structured emotional profiling and empathy-supporting robotics, ensuring human judgment is augmented by precise, personalized AI insights. However, given the uncertainties regarding long-term psychological impacts and broader societal implications of widespread AI reliance, rigorous longitudinal research, careful monitoring, and adaptive governance are critical to proactively managing potential risks.

Analysis and Results 

For statistical analysis, analyses of variance (ANOVA), Friedman tests, and correlation analyses were conducted. Descriptive and inferential statistics were computed using IBM SPSS Statistics (Version 29) and JASP (Version 0.19.3) (IBM Corp., 2023), while power analyses were performed using the software G*Power (Version 3.1) (Faul, et al., 2009).

An a priori power analysis was conducted with G*Power to determine the required sample size for a one-way ANOVA with three groups. Assuming a medium effect size (f = 0.25), a significance level of α = .05, and a desired statistical power of 1 – β = .95, the necessary total sample size was calculated as n = 252. The actual achieved sample size (n = 827) clearly exceeded this requirement.

An additional a priori power analysis was conducted for a Friedman test involving nine repeated measures. Assuming a medium effect size (f = 0.25), a significance level of α = .05, a desired statistical power of 
1 – β = .95, and a correlation of r = .50 between repeated measures, the required sample size was calculated as n = 160, which was also comfortably achieved by the study’s actual sample size.

All three relationships are statistically significant (p < .001), and the correlation values (around .49–.53) indicate moderate-strength positive relationships (see Table 3). The internal consistency of the scale measuring perceived needs-focused leadership was good, with Cronbach’s α = .83 (George & Mallery, 2003). Since the scales assessing turnover and project success each consisted of only two items, internal consistency was evaluated using the Pearson correlation between the two items. The correlations were acceptable (r = .67) for turnover intention and good (r = .73) for project success. (Eisinga, et al., 2013)

An ANOVA was conducted to analyze differences between the three project roles (Project Managers, Project Team Members, and Project Sponsors) for the variables Needs-Focused Leadership Effective, Turnover, Project Success, and Motivation.

No significant differences were observed between the three roles regarding the perceived Needs-Focused Leadership Effectiveness. Correspondingly, all post-hoc comparisons were non-significant, F(2, 824) = 2.30, p = .101, η² = .006.

Project Managers rated the impact on Turnover significantly lower compared to Team Members and Sponsors. No significant difference was found between Team Members and Sponsors, F(2, 824) = 7.57, p < .001, 
η² = .018.

Project Managers rated the impact on Project Success higher than Sponsors but lower than Team Members. Team Members rated the impact highest among the groups, F(2, 824) = 17.45, p < .001, η² = .041.

Team Members provided significantly lower ratings regarding the effectiveness of needs-focused leadership on Motivation compared to Project Managers and Sponsors. No significant difference in ratings was found between Project Managers and Sponsors, F(2, 824) = 32.74, p < .001, η² = .074).

Effect sizes (η²) indicate small to moderate effects, with the strongest differences observed in perceptions of Motivation.

Four separate Friedman tests revealed significant differences (all p < .001) in the rankings of the nine mindset and skillset elements across the overall sample (W = .10, N = 758), as well as within the subgroups of project managers (W = .11, N = 386), team members (W = .07, N = 174), and project sponsors (W = .10, 
N = 198). All differences exhibited consistently small effect sizes.

Interpretation of Results

The survey received a total of 1,249 responses, of which 854 were fully completed, resulting in a completion rate of 68%. This high completion rate indicates strong acceptance and relevance of the topic among participants.

The empirical findings support all four hypotheses, indicating statistically significant relationships and meaningful insights regarding needs-focused leadership:

•	Hypothesis 1 (Motivation and Job Satisfaction): Supported by the data, as higher perceived effectiveness of needs-focused leadership significantly correlates with increased team motivation 
(r = .526, p < .001, see Table 4).

•	Hypothesis 2 (Turnover): Supported by the data, as increased perceived effectiveness of needs-focused leadership significantly correlates with lower team member turnover (r = .492, p < .001, 
see Table 4).

•	Hypothesis 3 (Project Success): Supported by the data, as perceived needs-focused leadership effectiveness positively and significantly correlates with higher perceived project success (r = .493, 
p < .001, see Table 4).

•	Hypothesis 4 (Relative Importance of Mindset and Skillset Elements): Supported by the data, as the Friedman tests revealed significant ranking differences among the nine elements defining the needs-focused leadership approach, both overall (p < .001, W = .10) and within each project role subgroup. However, these differences were consistently small, suggesting broad agreement across roles with only subtle variations in perceived importance.

Participants were given the opportunity to mention additional leadership skills they deemed important but were not explicitly listed among the original nine ranked elements. The responses can broadly be categorized as follows:

•	A substantial proportion of respondents either did not have any additional skills to add or listed attributes that were essentially variants or refinements of the existing nine skills. For instance, several participants emphasized elements such as emotional intelligence, active listening, and empathetic communication—qualities already integrated into existing categories like “Attentive and Empathetic Listening” and “Emotional Balance.”
•	Many participants indicated that ranking the nine elements was challenging due to their perceived equal importance or situational dependency, reinforcing the interpretation that respondents saw each of these items as distinct yet practically interconnected and equally essential.
•	A smaller, but still notable number of participants added elements that were substantively distinct from the original nine, highlighting broader leadership perspectives such as visionary guidance, strategic alignment, fostering innovation, and promoting long-term, ethically-oriented growth. The latter set of inputs provided sufficient justification for introducing a new, distinct 10th element: "Visionary & Growth-Oriented Leadership". 

The ANOVA comparing perceptions across project roles (Project Managers, Team Members, Sponsors) provided additional nuance. While the general perception of the effectiveness of needs-focused leadership did not differ significantly between roles, there were statistically significant, role-specific differences regarding its perceived impact on turnover, project success, and motivation:

•	Turnover: Project Managers rated the impact significantly lower than Team Members and Sponsors.
•	Project Success: Team Members rated the impact highest, while Sponsors rated it lowest.
•	Motivation: Team Members rated the impact significantly lower compared to Project Managers and Sponsors.

Effect sizes (η²) for these differences were small to moderate, with the strongest differences observed in perceptions of motivation.

Overall, the data strongly confirm the perceived effectiveness and importance of needs-focused leadership for enhancing team motivation, reducing turnover, and improving project success. While there is widespread consensus on its general value, slight role-specific variations in perceptions—particularly evident regarding the relative importance of individual leadership skills and mindsets—suggest tailored approaches might further enhance its practical implementation.

MOVE model description

As the model evolved based on insights and patterns identified in the interviews, key requirements were validated and refined, resulting in several updates and improvements. Additionally, the final model was updated by recognizing insights derived from the quantitative element of the research (web survey):

•	The initial model treated “motivation” and “mediation” as separate processes but was later integrated into a single core process, with distinctions maintained to address specific differences. This integration reflects findings that teams motivated through a needs-focused approach are less likely to experience conflicts, and when conflicts occur, they resolve them more efficiently. Conversely, resolving conflicts through needs-focused mediation was found to enhance motivation, underscoring a logical interplay between the two elements.
•	The initial separation between “mindset” and “skillset” elements was reconsidered based on interview feedback indicating overlap and indistinct boundaries, leading to their integration.
•	Following recommendations from interviewees to simplify the skills framework, a core set of nine skills was established and validated through a web-based survey. An additional tenth skill emerged from an open question in the survey, resulting in a refined, ten-skill model structured for clarity and comprehensibility.
•	Further details and explicit references to established theoretical frameworks, such as Tuckman’s team development phases and project lifecycle stages, have been integrated into the model without altering its fundamental structure, enhancing theoretical alignment and practical relevance.
•	Recognizing the dynamic nature of needs management, the model has shifted from a linear, checklist-oriented approach to a circular and iterative one. This change explicitly emphasizes periodic structured interactions—such as retrospectives, phase-gate reviews, pulse-checks, and regular emotional check-ins—to sustain momentum and ensure continuous responsiveness to evolving team and project needs.
•	The mediation component has been refined into a structured, formal, mediation-inspired process that emphasizes emotional integrity (self-awareness, emotional clarity, stability) before engaging in emotionally charged interactions. Additionally, the model explicitly integrates “needs validation and acknowledgment” as an essential step prior to exploring practical solutions, reinforcing emotional effectiveness and stability.
•	Clear criteria and signals were established to identify when internal mediation limits are exceeded, indicating the necessity of involving external mediation support. Additionally, the importance of a safe, confidential environment facilitating emotional openness during mediation has been explicitly articulated.
•	To ensure practical applicability, structured guidelines for implementing the MOVE model at both project and organizational levels were included:
o	At the project level, the model supports project staffing and HR development with explicit connections to project lifecycle stages and stage-gate processes, ensuring consistency throughout project execution.
o	At the organizational level, the model provides integration points for HR and recruiting, organizational project management (OPM), project management offices (PMOs), PM standards, PMO maturity development, and organizational change initiatives.
•	Finally, the model was refined and extended in three key ways: First, trust emerged as an overarching theme throughout the research, leading to the development of a dedicated trust model. Second, the competency framework was expanded into a standalone competency model. And third, “MOVE” was established as the overarching title for the entire framework—reflecting the shared focus of all three components on moving work forward and engaging people meaningfully. A simple, intuitive visual representation was created to support understanding and practical application by managers and project teams.
 

1.1.1	Not just looking: Focusing.

In this thesis, the author deliberately uses the term “needs-focused leadership” instead of “needs-based” or “needs-oriented” leadership. The choice emphasizes that addressing human needs in leadership is not just about direction or foundation, but about a deliberate, systematic engagement with what lies beneath the surface. “Focused” highlights the importance of discerning subtle motivations, differentiating between emotional layers, and considering diverse perspectives rather than responding to general or superficial notions of need.

At the core of this approach lies the principle of focusing: a deliberate, investigative stance aimed at uncovering the essential motivations behind behavior. Rather than merely acknowledging needs at face value, needs-focused leadership involves looking beyond what is immediately visible to gain clarity about what truly matters.

This depth of focus enables leaders to engage more authentically with stakeholders, ensuring that their actions align with core human needs rather than surface-level symptoms.

A Focus on Feelings

The extent to which needs are fulfilled or—especially in conflict situations—violated or threatened, is directly reflected in an individual’s emotional state, their feelings. These feelings, expressed through body language, tone of voice, and facial expressions, function like an emotional “traffic light,” providing others visible clues about someone’s internal emotional experience. However, feelings often have multiple layers that need to be carefully uncovered and interpreted.

In contexts such as demotivation or conflict, the most immediately recognizable feeling is usually anger. This makes Gottman’s Anger Iceberg Model particularly valuable. In this model, anger represents only the visible tip of the emotional iceberg—easy to see but challenging to interpret accurately, as the deeper underlying emotions remain hidden. Understanding this model allows individuals to explore beneath the surface, identifying core emotions outlined by Ekman, which the author interprets as “water-line level emotions”. While these emotions are still part of the visible portion of the iceberg, requiring slightly more focus to recognize compared to the immediately evident “tip” of anger, they remain relatively universal and similarly understood across different cultures. Nevertheless, they are still not as deeply hidden, articulated, or personally nuanced as the layers that lie even further beneath. 

Moving beyond Ekman’s initial insight, it becomes possible to differentiate emotions even more finely, as demonstrated by researchers such as Nummenmaa and Baer, who identified more than eight core feelings and numerous nuanced emotional states.

Thus, focusing on feelings—essential to understanding underlying needs—means precisely recognizing, specifying, and validating emotions. 

A Focus on Needs

When aiming to understand people, it makes sense to start from a broader, more general perspective and gradually move closer to uncover what’s beneath the surface. Initially, it’s valuable to understand how individuals perceive life itself. Antonovsky and Eriksson refer to this as the Sense of Coherence (SOC). Simply put, SOC captures how someone makes sense of life’s challenges, seeing them as understandable, manageable, and worthwhile.

Taking a step deeper, it becomes helpful to identify specific "drivers" or internal messages that guide behavior, as described by Kahler. These drivers, such as the urge to be perfect or to please others, shape our day-to-day actions and reactions, often subconsciously influencing our emotional responses.

Going further, we can explore deeper personal motives. According to McClelland, fundamental motives like Achievement, Power, and Affiliation guide our choices and interactions, while Ryan highlights motives such as Autonomy, Competence, and Relatedness. Recognizing these core motives helps clarify why individuals pursue certain goals and relationships.

Next, understanding needs in greater depth brings us to Maslow’s hierarchy, ranging from basic physiological and safety needs to higher-level needs like belonging, esteem, and ultimately self-actualization. Rogers emphasizes internal psychological conditions necessary for personal growth, such as Self-direction, Openness to Experience, Acceptance of Others, and Trusting oneself. Being attuned to these aspects means recognizing someone’s capacity for authenticity, openness, and genuine connection.

The closer and further one looks, the more nuanced and differentiated the picture becomes. Additional elements, such as the various strengths outlined in the VIA model, can be identified, offering insights into individual characteristics that enable people to thrive. Moreover, the concept of needs grouped into ME, US, and SYSTEM needs, provided by the Spectrum of Needs model, further deepens our understanding by categorizing needs according to different relational contexts—personal, interpersonal, and organizational or systemic.

While focusing on emotions alone can already be challenging, especially during emotionally intense situations, addressing the underlying needs and deeper psychological elements is even more complex. However, by carefully stepping from a broad understanding toward these deeper internal layers, we gain profound insights into human behavior.

A Focus on Strategy and Action

To identify and select the most effective strategy—whether it involves internal thoughts, external actions, or adjustments to our external environment—it is essential to take a careful and similarly focused approach. This ensures that our choices serve not only our own interests but also those of the people involved.

In conflicts or challenging situations, we might experience emotional stress, prompting impulsive reactions, as highlighted by Walker. To handle such situations more effectively, Segal’s ABC model can assist by distinguishing between actual events and our emotional responses to them. Additionally, the decision-making process concerning how much energy to invest in an action—characterized by valence (how strongly we want something) and expectancy (how likely we believe our effort will succeed)—can be extremely rapid, sometimes causing premature action without adequate consideration of important contextual elements, such as culture. Here, Hofstede’s cultural dimensions model provides valuable insights, helping us understand and respect cultural differences and enabling appropriate actions.

Listening closely to our “inner team,” as outlined by Schulz von Thun, is crucial. Recognizing the current escalation level of conflicts, described by Glasl, is also vital to prevent further escalation. Differences in perceived status or “eye-level” communication, as detailed in Berne’s transactional analysis, may exacerbate conflicts, making resolution more challenging. At times, a neutral third party, as recommended by Moore, becomes essential to mediate effectively. Such moderation can help focus not only on solving the problem but also on managing emotions, a principle emphasized by Kabat-Zinn’s mindfulness approach, promoting a “being” rather than a purely “doing-driven” mindset.

Effective conflict resolution further benefits from balancing assertiveness and cooperativeness, as described in the Thomas-Kilmann Instrument. Supporting actions aimed at reaching agreements include active de-escalation and trust-building strategies, outlined in Osgood’s GRIT model. Integrating these strategies with Rosenberg’s Nonviolent Communication method, as well as trust and psychological safety models described by Mayer, Edmondson, and the author’s own trust model, provides a robust framework for successfully navigating and resolving interpersonal conflicts.

All this requires considerable time and effort, as well as a specific "type of person" equipped with the appropriate competencies. This is precisely where the MOVE model becomes relevant: it guides individuals towards a needs-oriented leadership perspective and approach. Grounded in Seligman’s Positive Psychology, the MOVE model emphasizes Motivation and Engagement enhanced through experiences of Flow, positive Relationships, and positive Emotions, ultimately leading to trust-based cooperation, agreement, and overall well-being.

 
1.2	Structure and elements

The MOVE Model is a comprehensive and disciplined framework designed to foster personal growth, resolve conflicts effectively, and support the selection or development of project leaders based on essential competencies for successful leadership. It comprises three interconnected components:

The associated (MOVE) Process Model outlines a structured, needs-focused approach to maintaining motivation within project teams and mediating conflicts as they arise. This integrated model demonstrates the interplay between motivation and mediation, providing clarity and practical guidance for implementation. Regarding motivation, the model emphasizes helping individuals understand and meet their needs, pursue meaningful goals, and sustain satisfaction and purpose. In terms of mediation, it facilitates recognizing differences, resolving conflicts constructively, and navigating complex interpersonal or cultural dynamics. Its clear structure supports seamless integration with existing organizational processes and frameworks.

The associated (MOVE) Competency Model outlines the key competencies essential for effective, needs-focused project leadership. Each competency integrates both developable skills—which can be enhanced through training, coaching, and personal development—and more hard-wired personality traits, which tend to be stable over time and should primarily inform the selection and placement of project leaders. This dual focus ensures that leadership development efforts are both targeted and realistic, while also supporting thoughtful staffing decisions.

The associated (MOVE) Trust Model outlines the key elements that form the foundation for establishing and sustaining a needs-focused, cooperative team environment—acting like the “glue” that holds everything together. It identifies three equally important components, with trust being at risk the moment even one of them becomes unstable.

Importantly, the MOVE Model is not intended as an exclusive leadership approach; rather, it complements the broader set of competencies required of contemporary, adaptive leaders. Its effectiveness depends on integrating the model with the leader’s individual personality traits, the specific project context, and the organization’s culture.

A foundational assumption of the MOVE Model is that project leaders already possess essential core competencies in project management. To reference these baseline competencies, the widely recognized “Talent Triangle” developed by the Project Management Institute (PMI)—the world’s largest professional association for project management—serves as a practical orientation. Although this model was developed primarily from practical experience rather than scientific research, its widespread acceptance and application within the professional community provide a meaningful framework to define the fundamental competencies assumed by the MOVE Model.

1.3	Implementation Guidelines

This chapter outlines how the MOVE model can be practically applied—both within individual projects and across the broader organizational environment. It offers hands-on strategies for embedding needs-focused leadership into day-to-day practice, supporting trust, emotional alignment, and sustainable team performance.

1.3.1	In a project

Emotional Frontloading

Effective team collaboration and project success begin with Emotional Frontloading—a deliberate and time-intensive initial process. At its core is the creation of an environment defined by psychological safety, characterized by openness, emotional alignment, and a constructive attitude toward failure. This means fostering a climate that carefully balances tolerance for mistakes with reliability and accountability. Within this context, humor plays a notable role by reducing tension and facilitating more natural, inclusive interactions. Especially in intercultural teams, Emotional Frontloading becomes even more essential, as emotional alignment requires greater sensitivity and care.

At the outset of the project, a dedicated session—often referred to as Workshop Zero or Emotional Kick-off—can be used to operationalize Emotional Frontloading. During this session, agreeing on shared ground rules and clearly defining common team values are essential steps toward emotional alignment. Clarifying individual roles, working styles, levels of autonomy, and task orientations—and linking them explicitly to broader strategic goals—ensures consistent direction and coherence within the team. Instruments such as a Team Charter or Teaming Agreement can be used to formalize these understandings and strengthen mutual accountability.

A valuable complementary step is the open exchange or co-creation of personality profiles during informal team-building activities. This promotes deeper mutual understanding and enhances the team’s ability to anticipate and proactively address potential conflict dynamics. Similarly, incorporating emotional “lessons learned”—such as personal motivation triggers or recurring sources of friction from past experiences—provides the team with concrete, preventive strategies for navigating future challenges.

To deepen individual engagement, project managers can also develop a personalized “project story” for each team member. This narrative links the project’s purpose, goals, and expected contributions to the individual’s unique needs, values, and strengths. Whether expressed verbally or in writing, these individualized perspectives help each team member see how the project connects to what matters most to them—fostering emotional resonance, ownership, and long-term motivation.

In this context, integrating the MOVE Trust Model into the Team Charter or as part of the team’s set of ground rules offers particular value. It provides a structured framework for understanding, building, and maintaining interpersonal trust—an essential foundation for psychological safety and sustainable collaboration.

Ultimately, prioritizing Emotional Frontloading equips teams with a strong emotional foundation, boosting their resilience, adaptability, and effectiveness across the entire project lifecycle.

Project Phases, Stages, and Emotional Checkpoints

Projects are typically structured into phases, each beginning with dedicated setup activities—most notably during initial project initiation—and concluding with retrospective reviews. These transition points, marking the end of one phase and the beginning of another, are ideal opportunities for targeted, needs-focused actions.
At the start of each phase, teams should conduct an emotional check-in, establishing alignment not only on factual content and goals but also on emotional readiness and team cohesion. Likewise, phase conclusions should feature reflective reviews, asking questions such as “How have we done?” and “What’s next?”—considering emotional aspects alongside technical or performance-related criteria. Integrating emotional frontloading into these checkpoints enhances team stability, motivation, and long-term effectiveness.

Organizations employing a structured stage-gate model often formalize these emotional check-ins and check-outs. In such environments, projects must fulfill specific criteria or deliverables to advance from one phase to another. Consequently, emotional checkpoints might become mandated components, explicitly outlined by organizational guidelines, ensuring that emotional and interpersonal aspects receive consistent attention throughout the project’s lifecycle.

Figure 31 illustrates an example of a project focused on relaunching a corporate intranet. At the top, typical project management stages are depicted, followed by the corresponding lifecycle phases of this specific project. Additionally, potential team development stages are outlined, distinguishing between the core project team (bottom) and the development subteam, which may operate externally, such as with an IT supplier. The MOVE model can be applied at the following points:

1.	Early in the project, linked to initial planning activities, a comprehensive "emotional frontloading" activity is conducted. This includes defining the project approach, establishing ground rules, and formalizing initial emotional alignment and consensus in a Team Charter. Given the project's focus on organizational and cultural change, a change manager participates from the outset.
2.	At the conclusion of the detailed planning and preparation phase—prior to initiating the project's most critical tasks—an "emotional review" is carried out. This step identifies lessons learned and defines specific actions for the forthcoming phases.
3.	The subteam responsible for technical development undertakes its own emotional setup and check-in. As this team employs an adaptive (agile) approach, distributing several management responsibilities among team members or through dual leadership structures, regular informal "emotional retrospectives" are conducted throughout the development phase.
4.	Toward the end of the development phase, just before an integrated stage where close cooperation between the development subteam and the core project team becomes essential, another emotional review takes place. This includes preparatory and alignment activities to ensure smooth initiation of the next phase.
5.	Upon completion of the main project work, a substantial emotional review is conducted to prepare for the handover to operations. At this stage, emotional alignment explicitly involves members from future operational roles, supported by the organizational change manager.
6.	Once the project concludes, a comprehensive emotional review is executed, engaging stakeholders who participated at various stages throughout the project. This final step involves capturing and documenting best practices and lessons learned for future initiatives.

Project Staffing and MOVE Competencies

Projects aim to involve the best-suited individuals, a principle that applies equally to project managers and team members. When a company or project sponsor seeks a leader capable of employing a needs-focused approach, the MOVE competencies serve as an essential reference framework. Evaluating a potential project leader involves several critical questions: Which competencies does this person already possess, and how developed or robust are they?

Competencies understood primarily as traits, which are challenging and time-intensive to develop, play a particularly critical role. A leader lacking these foundational traits cannot realistically be expected to effectively guide a team with a needs-focused approach. In such cases, it may be advisable to select another, more suitable leader. If this isn’t feasible, stakeholders should consciously adjust their expectations regarding the quality and effectiveness of the project’s leadership.

Dual Leadership Models and Their Limitations

Projects may occasionally face situations where the designated project leader—due to their personality traits or specific skill set—cannot effectively fulfill the requirements of a needs-focused leadership approach. In these cases, some organizations consider adopting a dual leadership model. Under this approach, leadership responsibilities are divided: One person is responsible for performing the task leadership functions and another person for the maintenance leadership behaviors. (Pavitt & Curtis, 1994) 

In practice this may may mean that one project leader primarily manages the technical or product-related aspects, while a second person assumes the role of an “emotional leader,” attending specifically to the emotional and interpersonal dimensions of the project.

A dual leadership model offers a range of advantages that go far beyond a simple division of labor. By sharing responsibilities, co-leaders reduce individual workload and the risk of burnout, enhancing both the sustainability and long-term effectiveness of leadership. Joint decision-making creates a natural system of checks and balances, resulting in more thoroughly considered and balanced outcomes. At the same time, when one leader focuses on social and emotional aspects, team cohesion and trust are strengthened.

Another key benefit is the ability to clearly separate strategic and operational responsibilities. While one leader concentrates on long-term direction, the other manages day-to-day operations—an especially effective setup in complex or fast-changing environments. Co-leadership also serves a symbolic function: visible collaboration at the top signals shared ownership and fosters a more inclusive, participatory team culture.

Moreover, the diversity of perspectives within a leadership duo creates a fertile ground for innovation. Constructive friction between two approaches can generate more creative solutions and bolder initiatives. From a continuity standpoint, the model also provides resilience: if one leader is unavailable, the other can step in, ensuring stable leadership. Finally, internal accountability is strengthened, as mutual oversight helps reduce the risk of unilateral errors and can build greater trust among external stakeholders.

While dual leadership offers substantial benefits, it also comes with inherent challenges that can undermine its effectiveness if not managed carefully. One of the most common issues is role ambiguity: when responsibilities between co-leaders are not clearly defined, confusion, inefficiencies, and decision gaps can quickly emerge. This lack of clarity can frustrate team members and slow down operations.

Decision-making can also become slower, as co-leaders typically rely on consensus, which may delay action—especially under pressure or in fast-moving environments. In cases where alignment is lacking, power struggles or interpersonal conflicts can arise, potentially paralyzing the organization and eroding trust within the team.

Moreover, co-leadership demands ongoing coordination, which increases the communication burden and raises the risk of sending mixed messages to staff. If there’s a perceived imbalance in visibility, workload, or influence, resentment may build—destabilizing the partnership. This is especially problematic in organizations with deeply rooted hierarchical structures, where shared leadership may meet resistance or create confusion among stakeholders.

Another risk is that employees might exploit divisions between co-leaders to advance their own agendas, undermining authority and weakening team cohesion. Over time, many co-leadership arrangements prove fragile, often reverting to single-leader models due to instability, leadership turnover, or shifting strategic priorities. Finally, co-leadership places heightened emotional demands on both leaders; it requires mutual trust, maturity, and a willingness to share power—traits that not every leadership pairing possesses or is prepared to cultivate.

While there is substantial scientific evidence on both the benefits and challenges of dual-leadership models, organizations considering this approach must carefully weigh its advantages and drawbacks—and ensure that the necessary structures and protocols are in place to harness its strengths while minimizing potential risks. (Wendt, 2024) The topic is shaped by ongoing workplace dynamics and remains the subject of active research. While it is relevant, it goes beyond the scope of this thesis. However, it could certainly be explored further in the context of project management.

Needs-Focused Leadership in Self-Organized (Sub-)Teams

In projects employing an adaptive (agile) approach, product or service development is often carried out by self-organized (sub-)teams. Such teams typically function with greater autonomy, making their own decisions about how to perform tasks. Leadership in these contexts tends to be facilitative rather than directive, exemplified by roles such as the Scrum Master in Scrum methodology. This facilitative role aligns closely with the concept of Servant Leadership, where the leader supports and enables the team’s workflow rather than dictating it.

Servant leaders embody many elements associated with needs-focused leadership. However, self-organized teams differ from traditional teams with directive leadership, particularly regarding how leadership responsibilities are distributed. In self-organized setups, many leadership responsibilities—including emotional management and needs-focused actions—are actually carried out by team members themselves rather than solely by the designated leader.

For effective self-organization and emotional alignment (e.g., emotional check-ins and check-outs), it’s beneficial if team members themselves possess the key traits and skills outlined in the MOVE Competency Model. These competencies should not be limited to the facilitator (e.g., Scrum Master), but also be present within the team, enabling more autonomous management of emotional and interpersonal dynamics.

Managerial Mediation, Needs-Focused Leadership, and Boundaries

Needs-focused leadership inherently reduces the relational distance between leaders and team members. While the leader often assumes the role of a coach, supporting individual team members’ personal and professional development based on their specific needs, there is a critical boundary that must not be crossed—namely, avoiding a dynamic that resembles a psychotherapeutic (“red-couch”) setting. Respecting these limits is closely tied to the competency of establishing and maintaining emotional integrity, which requires the leader to remain aware of and adhere to clear interpersonal boundaries.

Similarly, when project managers mediate conflicts within their teams—referred to as managerial mediation—there are inherent boundaries and limitations that must be acknowledged and upheld. Situations may arise where the manager’s neutrality becomes compromised, trust issues emerge, or conflicts escalate beyond the leader’s capacity to effectively mediate.

In these scenarios, involving a neutral third party is essential. Suitable individuals might include uninvolved team members or managers, HR professionals trained in mediation, or external mediators from outside the organization. Regardless of who assumes this mediation role, maintaining neutrality, impartiality, and trustworthiness remains critical.

Importantly, even when mediation responsibilities shift away from the primary project leader, a needs-focused approach should continue to guide the conflict resolution process. Consequently, third-party mediators must embody the competencies outlined in the MOVE Competency Model, ensuring mediation remains consistent, empathetic, and effective while respecting clearly established emotional boundaries.

Competency Development and HR Support

The MOVE Competence Model integrates both skills—trainable behaviors that can be enhanced through targeted development—and traits, which are more stable personality characteristics and better suited for selection and staffing decisions. While both are essential for effective leadership, HR development should primarily focus on skills, as they offer more immediate opportunities for growth and behavioral change.

To foster needs-focused leadership in projects, organizations should prioritize the structured development of skill-related competencies. Some of these may already be covered in existing project management or soft skills programs. However, many essential skills—particularly those linked to emotional intelligence, psychological safety, or needs-focused communication—require dedicated training, coaching, or supervision.

As part of this foundation, training in Mental Health First Aid (MHFA), mental health-sensitive leadership, and basic neurodiversity awareness should become a standard component of any foundational project management curriculum. These competencies equip project managers to recognize early signs of mental distress, respond appropriately, and foster inclusive environments that account for diverse cognitive and emotional needs.

To complement this broader awareness with tools for individual-level insight and intervention, personality models such as the Process Communication Model (PCM) can serve as valuable tools in this process. By offering nuanced insights into communication patterns and stress responses, such profiles help:

•	identify current skill strengths and gaps,
•	guide personalized development plans,
•	and support the design of effective interventions tailored to the individual’s needs and role.

The thesis findings show clear differences in how competencies are prioritized across organizational levels. Executives often value self-regulation and systems thinking, while team members place higher importance on listening, emotional balance, and clarity. These diverging perspectives can create hidden misalignments unless addressed explicitly.

•	Open cross-level dialogues to explore why each group ranks competencies differently. This creates mutual understanding, reduces misinterpretation, and surfaces implicit expectations.

This format promotes horizontal and vertical learning, strengthens mutual appreciation, and aligns leadership practices across the hierarchy.

MOVE Competencies and Organizational Change Management

Focusing on the human side of work is especially critical in Organizational Change Management (OCM). OCM deals with implementing changes—such as modifications to systems, processes, infrastructure, or organizational culture—and aims to help individuals embrace these changes, thereby reducing resistance.

Projects frequently involve change initiatives, with the project leader often tasked to handle change management responsibilities or a dedicated change manager (or change agent) being appointed. While change management is closely related to project activities and their outcomes, it typically extends beyond the project’s conclusion, influencing operational and functional business activities.

Resistance to change often emerges because stakeholders perceive their needs as being threatened or actively compromised. Consequently, the MOVE Process Model—designed with a needs-focused approach—can be effectively applied within change management activities. Similarly, the MOVE Competency Model is directly relevant for professionals working in change management roles, providing a structured foundation for addressing stakeholder needs and facilitating smoother transitions.

1.3.2	In the Organizational Project Management (and beyond)

Organizational Project Management and Needs-Focused Leadership

Organizational Project Management (OPM) encompasses the governance framework, processes, tools, techniques, standards, and guidelines that define how projects are initiated, planned, executed, monitored, controlled, and closed within an organization. In a multi-project environment, OPM involves continuous management of the project portfolio, ensuring efficient use of resources and confirming that the organization is “doing the right things.”

Leadership style and associated organizational culture influence OPM effectiveness. Companies choosing to adopt a needs-focused leadership approach in their projects aim to enhance both individual project outcomes and overall project portfolio performance. To successfully implement this approach, individuals responsible for organizational project management must understand not only the benefits and positive effects of needs-focused leadership but also practical strategies for embedding it into organizational practices.

Integrating Needs-Focused Leadership into Project Governance Standards

Governance standards and processes fundamentally shape how projects are executed within an organization. When introducing a needs-focused leadership approach, companies should integrate the MOVE models into existing governance frameworks in several key ways:

•	Project Management Standards and Processes: The MOVE Process Model can be embedded across various stages of the project lifecycle. Traditional planning processes—usually focused on scope, schedule, and resources—can incorporate explicit emotional frontloading activities. Similarly, regular project reviews or formal stage-gate evaluations can include emotional check-ins and check-outs, generating emotional lessons learned for future initiatives.
•	Skill and Competency Frameworks: Existing competency frameworks for staffing and developing project managers can be expanded by integrating the MOVE Competency Model, tailored to align with the organization’s specific leadership expectations and values.
•	Organizational Body of Knowledge: Companies can develop or enhance an internal repository of best practices, including effective leadership behaviors and needs-focused methodologies, supporting consistent application and continuous improvement of leadership competencies.
•	Operational Guides and Tools: Practical project management guides and operational manuals may include concrete recommendations for applying needs-focused leadership, translating competencies into everyday practice.

In addition to internal governance frameworks, established industry standards such as the Project Management Institute’s Project Management Body of Knowledge (PMBoK Guide) and its associated practice guides, models like PMI’s Talent Triangle, or competency-based frameworks such as IPMA’s Individual Competence Baseline (ICB) could also benefit from integrating the MOVE Process and Competency Models. 

Organizational-level models such as the PMO Value Ring—focused on value-oriented PMO design and service delivery—and PMI’s 2024 PMO Practice Guide—emphasizing customer-centricity, adaptability, and strategic alignment—may likewise be enriched by incorporating needs-focused elements. Especially in areas like stakeholder engagement, service prioritization, and PMO maturity development, a needs-based perspective adds human-centered depth to value generation and reinforces the PMO’s role as an enabler of emotionally intelligent leadership and sustainable collaboration. 

Extending these cross-industry standards with needs-focused elements would enhance their maturity, applicability, and effectiveness, further elevating leadership practices across various organizational and project contexts.

Continuously refining these internal and external standards and guidelines ensures ongoing evolution and maturity of project management capabilities, ultimately strengthening overall organizational performance.

Project Management Offices and Needs-Focused Leadership

Project Management Offices (PMOs) play a crucial role in shaping how projects are executed within an organization. They define governance frameworks and processes, set standards, and clarify expectations regarding the mindsets and skills required from effective project managers. In some organizations, PMOs also maintain a pool of internal project managers, similar to an HR-managed pool of professionals, who are assigned to projects as needed.

Consequently, establishing a needs-focused leadership approach within an organization depends on the PMO professionals’ understanding of this leadership style. PMOs need to clearly identify the specific skillsets, mindsets, and personality traits—outlined in the MOVE Competency Model—required to authentically implement and embody needs-focused behaviors.

Like Organizational Project Management itself, PMOs typically have a certain maturity level, with an ongoing ambition to continually improve their capabilities. Such improvements include increasing their knowledge and understanding of effective project execution, as well as supporting projects in operationalizing new or innovative leadership approaches. In this capacity, PMOs function similarly to mentors, guiding project leaders to effectively adopt and apply a needs-focused leadership style.

Therefore, PMO leaders themselves must thoroughly grasp and champion the concept of needs-focused leadership, as their influence directly affects how effectively and authentically this approach is integrated and practiced throughout the organization.

The Strategic Role of Human Resources in Needs-Focused Leadership

The Human Resources (HR) department plays a critical and strategic role in the successful implementation and ongoing application of needs-focused leadership within an organization. HR’s strategic influence spans multiple areas:

•	Supervision: HR might directly or indirectly provide supervision services to project leaders and teams. Supervision enables leaders and team members to develop and grow in a structured, sustainable manner, benefiting from this type of external development support.
•	Recruiting and Staffing: HR professionals are deeply involved—and sometimes the primary decision-makers—in recruitment alongside functional managers. Their selection decisions strongly shape the type of leaders hired and assigned as project managers, directly influencing the presence of needs-focused leadership competencies within the organization.
•	Learning and Development: HR designs and implements leadership development programs, training, and initiatives that support employees’ personal growth. They may also facilitate coaching, mentoring, or supervision. It’s important to note that according to the MOVE Competency Model, not all competencies can be developed solely through training—some traits necessitate thoughtful staffing, reassignment, or even outplacement decisions.
•	Target and Performance Management: Though challenging to directly quantify needs-focused leadership with conventional KPIs, effective leadership behaviors can be incorporated into performance management strategies. These evaluations may rely on stakeholder feedback, collected throughout or at the conclusion of projects, reinforcing and recognizing desired leadership behaviors.
•	Compensation, Rewards, and Recognition: Linked closely with performance management, HR may structure compensation, incentives, and recognition programs to reward visible and effective application of needs-focused leadership behaviors, thereby reinforcing their importance within the organizational culture.
•	Talent Management: HR actively identifies and supports high-performing employees who naturally embody needs-focused leadership principles, positioning them as internal role models. Conversely, individuals who consistently struggle to develop necessary leadership traits (as opposed to skills) may be candidates for reassignment or, as a final step, outplacement.
•	Organizational Development: HR frequently oversees or supports organizational development efforts, influencing the establishment of roles, competencies, and structures. This responsibility extends to defining project manager roles and supporting the strategic development of Project Management Offices (PMOs), emphasizing the necessary mindsets and skillsets as described in the MOVE models.
•	Corporate Culture Development: HR is instrumental in shaping the corporate culture—including leadership culture. While organizational culture evolves from existing conditions, HR helps define and guide the path toward a desired future state. Through this influence, HR can intentionally embed needs-focused leadership principles into the broader organizational culture.
•	Employer Branding: Closely related to corporate culture, HR defines and communicates the organization’s employer branding strategy and Employee Value Proposition (EVP). Explicitly integrating a needs-focused leadership approach into employer branding can enhance organizational attractiveness, particularly appealing to new generations of talent who increasingly prioritize empathetic and needs-centered leadership.
•	Organizational Change Management: HR often plays a role in the overarching organizational change management, being responsible implementing the change, driving it, develop change agents and so on. The MOVE model addresses human needs, which are similarly addressed in organizational change management, making this tool equally relevant for this environment.

Through these strategic roles, HR contributes to embedding needs-focused leadership across organizational practices, culture, and identity, positioning the company as a forward-thinking and attractive employer for future talent.

Embedding Needs-Focused Leadership into Organizational Culture

Each project tends to develop its own distinct cultural characteristics, especially when involving cross-unit, cross-country, or cross-cultural collaboration. Yet, projects never operate in isolation—they remain intrinsically connected to the broader organizational environment and its established culture. Introducing a needs-focused leadership style within projects, although beneficial on a project-specific level, can initially trigger skepticism or even resistance from stakeholders unfamiliar with human-centered approaches. For instance, functional leaders accustomed to traditional management styles might perceive such empathetic and relationship-oriented practices as signs of managerial weakness.

Therefore, it’s crucial to proactively identify and manage both threats and opportunities linked to adopting this leadership style. Possible conflicts arising from different leadership expectations must be carefully mitigated. Conversely, there is a opportunity for positively influencing overall corporate culture, contributing to the organization’s broader “corporate health.”

Senior leadership plays a decisive role in the successful implementation, application, and organizational acceptance of needs-focused leadership. Senior executives directly impact projects through participation in steering committees and indirectly through their influence on project sponsors, who often report directly to them. The deeper senior leaders understand and actively promote the benefits of needs-focused approaches, the greater the likelihood of successful adoption within individual projects.

Additionally, establishing and maintaining psychological safety and trust—critical preconditions for needs-focused leadership—is essential. The extent of psychological safety and trust embedded within the broader organizational culture directly affects the ability of project teams to achieve the necessary openness, authenticity, and mutual respect. An environment characterized by psychological safety and trust thus becomes both a foundational requirement and an enabler of a genuinely needs-focused leadership culture in projects.

ESG, the “Social” Element, and Needs-Focused Leadership

ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance) criteria are sustainability standards used to evaluate how responsibly a company operates. The “Social” (S) component specifically focuses on aspects such as occupational health and safety, diversity, employee well-being, and corporate social responsibility, aligning closely with initiatives like the UN Global Compact.

Implementing a needs-focused leadership approach within project management supports the ESG “Social” dimension. By emphasizing empathy, psychological safety, trust, and addressing individual stakeholder needs, this leadership style actively promotes organizational health, creating an inclusive and engaging work environment. As a result, it enhances employee satisfaction, strengthens workplace culture, and directly contributes to achieving social sustainability objectives.

 
1.4	MOVE Process Model

A somewhat circular process approach, as seen in models like 

•	PDCA (Plan – Do – Check – Act) (EABPM, 2014, pp. 69-76)
•	Six Sigma: DMAIC (Define, Measure, Analyze, Improve, Control) (ASQ, 2025)
•	Appreciative Inquiry (Discovery – Dream – Design – Destiny) (Cooperrider, et al., 2008, pp. 5-7)
•	ADKAR (Awareness – Desire – Knowledge – Ability – Reinforcement) (Hiatt, 2006, pp. 2-3)

offers several benefits. It promotes continuous improvement by allowing for regular feedback and adjustments. This approach enhances flexibility and adaptability, making it easier to respond to changes and new information. It also fosters a culture of continuous learning and development, encouraging ongoing collaboration and communication. By repeatedly cycling through phases, it ensures that improvements are sustainable and aligned with evolving needs and goals.

Applying the MOVE Model effectively in project contexts requires a mindful integration of structured processes, interpersonal skills, and supportive tools, all aligned closely with the project’s governance framework and organizational culture.

A critical managerial competency is the ability to balance swift intervention with sufficient time for achieving sustainable resolutions. Leaders must respect each step of the process, resisting the temptation to prematurely move toward strategic solutions without adequate reflection. Reflective pauses between steps, allowing space for deeper consideration and specification of elements—such as individual positions, interests, values, emotions, and stimuli—contribute to the effectiveness of the intervention.

Ensuring emotional integrity throughout this process is fundamental, as emotional stability may fluctuate and should be continuously monitored. Authentic engagement and genuine exploration of interpersonal dynamics are essential, since superficial attempts at resolving conflicts can exacerbate existing tensions. Consequently, the mediation component of the MOVE Model requires the creation of safe, confidential environments. Mediators must actively guide individuals toward emotional openness and foster psychological safety, supported by a foundation of trust, aligning closely with principles described in the concept of “emotional frontloading.”

Both formal structured methods—such as personality profiling tools like PCM (Process Communication Model) and Spectrum of Needs, which serve as overarching supportive mechanisms—and informal interactions within dedicated reflective spaces enhance open communication. Regularly scheduled checkpoints—such as stage gates, retrospectives, or pulse-checks—provide structured opportunities for needs assessment, emotional check-ins, and strategic alignment.

Time allocation is particularly crucial, and sufficient time must be consciously provided to manage interpersonal dynamics effectively, while also respecting the inherent constraints of the project’s timeline. While typically conducted by a single project leader, the inclusion of external support through individual or group supervision should be considered. Supervision can contribute to personal development, team cohesion, and sustained effectiveness of the mediation and motivational processes.

Additionally, the project leader’s personality traits and competencies—outlined explicitly in the MOVE Competency Model—must be carefully considered during selection or staffing decisions. Traits that are inherently stable should influence leader selection, while skills that can be developed over time should inform training and personal growth plans. 

The MOVE Model presupposes a directive leadership approach by a single leader, complemented by sufficient flexibility to support varying degrees of self-organization within the team. Higher levels of self-organization imply greater process responsibility for team members, potentially affecting the leader’s mediation and motivational role, as explored in the section contrasting needs-focused leadership and servant leadership approaches.

Finally, the MOVE Model strongly recommends the thoughtful integration of AI as an emotional co-pilot to enhance personalized motivational support, provide neutral and unbiased mediation, simulate conflict scenarios, identify blind spots and biases, support tailored learning and professional growth, and offer personalized psychological assistance. Such AI integration, however, must always be implemented in a balanced and consciously managed manner, complementing rather than replacing human judgment and empathy.
 
1: Establish emotional integrity

Before the project manager can identify and analyze a team member’s needs—either to define motivational strategies or mediate conflicts effectively—he must first establish his own emotional integrity. This involves recognizing possible emotional gaps within himself  (Torbert, 2004, p. 39), and honestly assessing his current emotional state: Is he feeling balanced and well? Is he experiencing any signs of elevated stress? Are his own needs satisfied? Achieving emotional integrity, a state in which one’s own emotional triggers (“buttons”) remain unaffected by the present situation, is an essential precondition for addressing another person’s needs without becoming part of the conflict himself.

In this self-assessment, it is equally important to consider whether any experienced emotional reactions may have been absorbed from others. Emotions are contagious, especially in high-pressure environments. A project manager might unknowingly internalize another person’s frustration, fear, or stress, mistaking it for their own emotional state. Recognizing and filtering out such emotionally transmitted signals helps maintain clarity and neutrality.

In addition to emotional self-awareness, the project manager must also ensure there are no conflicts of interest that could compromise his objectivity. Unrecognized or unmanaged conflicts of interest may lead to unconscious bias or personal involvement in disputes, undermining the project manager’s role as a neutral and trustworthy mediator. This includes the possibility that the project manager is already a party in an emerging. Failing to recognize such entanglements early may erode trust and reduce the effectiveness of any intervention attempted later.

In this context, it is also valuable for the project manager to take a forward-looking perspective: Are there foreseeable dynamics or upcoming interactions that could challenge his emotional integrity or trigger future conflict? Identifying such potential threats in advance—especially those that could intimidate or destabilize one’s emotional balance—helps to proactively strengthen resilience and maintain neutrality under pressure.

This self-awareness also supports “Emotional Frontloading,” a concept explored further in a subsequent section, emphasizing proactive emotional transparency to build trust and psychological safety within teams. Additionally, needs assessment should ideally be reciprocal. Encouraging team members to reflect on and analyze the project manager’s communication style and personal needs fosters mutual understanding, empathy, and enhances team collaboration.

The integrity battery.

The author’s concept of the integrity battery envisions emotional stability and resilience as a mental energy source fueling our capacity to navigate life effectively. This “battery” represents the energy we draw upon to maintain emotional integrity throughout the day.

When we encounter experiences or thoughts that “push our buttons,” energy from the integrity battery is consumed to help preserve emotional balance and stability. This energy sustains us and enables us to extend understanding and compassion, both toward ourselves and others. However, maintaining emotional integrity is not a one-time task; it requires permanent monitoring, as our emotional states can fluctuate continually.

If the integrity battery runs low, our ability to empathize, adopt others’ perspectives, and respond compassionately diminishes. Once completely drained, we lose the capacity to connect with or fully understand the feelings and viewpoints of those around us. To mitigate this, the author recommends “reflective pausing,” allowing sufficient time for emotional recharging and enabling one to re-establish emotional integrity.

Regularly recharging the integrity battery is essential for emotional well-being. It involves addressing the underlying needs connected to our emotional triggers. Methods of self-affirmation—such as mindfulness practices, reflection, and other positive habits—replenish this energy source, restoring emotional stability and supporting meaningful interpersonal connections.

Additionally, using structured personality or needs profiles—such as the Process Communication Model (PCM) or a Needs Profile based on the Spectrum of Needs—can further enhance self-awareness, aid emotional self-assessment, and facilitate targeted interventions to maintain a healthy integrity battery level.

2: Identify needs

This step involves the project manager identifying important needs expressed by a team member. A crucial prerequisite is the ability to clearly differentiate between needs, feelings, and stimuli, actively differentiating and focusing to uncover the specific underlying needs beneath superficial expressions or behaviors. The core question here is: Which pronounced needs should be fulfilled to elicit positive emotions or foster motivation in the team member? It is essential to concentrate explicitly on underlying needs rather than on stimuli—the external factors influencing those needs—as multiple strategies and environments usually exist to fulfill any given need.

In this context, it is also important to consider that the emotion a team member expresses may not always originate from their own unmet need. Emotions are contagious—especially in closely interacting teams—and a person may unknowingly adopt the frustration, fear, or stress of others. This can lead to emotional expressions that reflect the group’s dynamic more than the individual’s internal state. Accurately identifying needs therefore requires sensitivity to these influences and openness to the possibility that a felt emotion may not point to a personal need, but to an absorbed one.

Pronounced needs can be identified through several complementary methods. A foundational prerequisite for all of them is active listening—a deliberate, attentive presence that allows the project manager to truly hear what is being said, notice what is left unsaid, and observe the emotional undercurrents behind verbal expressions. 

Equally important is the adaptation to the team member’s preferred communication style. This involves adjusting tone, pacing, level of directness, and even vocabulary to match how the other person feels most comfortable expressing themselves. In remote or hybrid settings, creating conditions that allow for emotionally attuned communication becomes especially important. Switching on cameras, using high-quality microphones, and complementing spoken dialogue with chat functions or written follow-ups can help minimize misunderstandings and support a more accurate identification of needs.

The following tools and approaches can then be applied in practice to help surface and specify needs:

•	Direct inquiry or educated guessing, ideally supported by framing, advocacy, and illustration.
•	Systematic questioning techniques, designed to methodically uncover deeper or unexpressed needs.
•	Circular questioning, which explores relationships, perceptions, and patterns by inviting perspective-taking (e.g., “What do you think your colleague would say about this situation?”), helping to uncover systemic influences and broaden understanding.
•	Using structured personality or needs profiling tools, such as the Process Communication Model (PCM) or a Needs Profile, to provide additional clarity.
•	Contextual analysis, which helps in understanding the situational environment, historical background, and project-specific factors that shape or influence team members’ needs. This includes checking for signs of the “Garbage Can Phenomenon,” where unresolved issues from past experiences or unrelated contexts may influence current expressions of need.
•	The doubling technique—empathetically speaking for participants—when employed sensitively and with explicit consent, especially in moments of emotional hesitation or difficulty articulating needs.
•	Reflective pausing, including the practice of “sleeping on it,” providing additional time and mental space for deeper insights and clearer specification of needs.
The practices of framing, advocating, and illustrating enhance the clarity and effectiveness of the identification process:

•	Framing explicitly defines the purpose, context, and shared assumptions of discussions. Clarifying the issue, stating goals clearly, and openly sharing one’s perspective prevents misunderstandings and aligns team members toward common objectives.
•	Advocating means explicitly expressing a need, perspective, or proposed course of action. Effective advocacy avoids vagueness and encourages constructive collaboration by clearly articulating personal or team needs.
•	Illustrating supports advocacy by providing concrete examples, context, and specifics, thus reducing ambiguity and potential misinterpretation. Effective communication emerges from balancing advocacy and illustration: advocacy without illustration risks misunderstanding, while illustration without advocacy lacks clear direction.

Using an inquiry—especially one based on an educated guess—is valuable, as it invites either confirmation or disconfirmation. Encouraging team members to disconfirm assumptions when necessary promotes genuine dialogue and facilitates the discovery of common ground and deeper, authentic needs.

(Torbert, 2004, pp. 28-30)

In addition to identifying current needs, project managers should also remain attentive to potential future tensions—such as needs that are not yet expressed but may become critical later. This includes observing early indicators of discomfort, withdrawal, or emotional overcontrol, which might signal a risk of future frustration or conflict. Taking this anticipatory stance helps prevent escalation and supports timely intervention before trust or motivation erode.

It is equally important to recognize that shy or emotionally reserved team members require empathetic validation and gentle encouragement to express themselves openly. The project manager should create a safe, empathetic space that allows every team member to share their true needs comfortably.

Furthermore, the project manager should identify and address overarching team-wide needs—such as clarity, orientation, direction, autonomy, and freedom—that foster resonance, emotional alignment, and meaningfulness within the team, all without overlooking the specific individual needs of each member.

3: Analyze needs

At this stage in the process, the project manager analyzes identified needs by examining key factors, such as:

•	Is the need connected to specific values and interests?
•	Which cultural factors influence how the need is understood and analyzed (considering cultural norms, values, patterns, and behaviors)?
•	What is the priority or degree of prominence of this need?
•	Is there an urgency associated with fulfilling this particular need?
•	Which identified needs might be competing or conflicting with other existing needs?
•	Is the resistance expressed by team members predominantly emotional or technical, as this differentiation offers distinct and targeted intervention strategies?

It is also important to clearly distinguish between the pronouncedness of a need—how strong or dominant it is in a person’s psychological structure—and its current level of fulfillment. A need may be highly pronounced (e.g., a strong need for autonomy or recognition) but currently well met, while another, less pronounced need may be severely unmet and cause disproportionate tension. Accurate analysis depends on differentiating these two dimensions.

During this stage, it is essential to check for possible biases or cognitive distortions—both in the project manager’s own interpretation and in the expressed views of stakeholders. Introducing reflective pauses and peer dialogue can help surface such distortions and correct course early.

To deepen understanding and promote transparency, systems thinking should also be made visible during the analysis phase. By mapping root causes, identifying interdependencies, and visually communicating systemic reasoning—through tools like causal (root-cause) diagrams or influence maps—the project manager not only sharpens their own diagnostic accuracy but also helps team members and experts appreciate the complexity of leadership decisions. 

As this process step involves exploring potential psychological dimensions, it should also include a check for indicators of mental health concerns. Assessing the team member’s current stress level can offer early clues about emotional strain, cognitive overload, or deeper unmet needs. This analytical stage is particularly well-suited to identify situations where issues may exceed standard leadership competencies and require the involvement of trained professionals. When certain signals emerge during the needs analysis, recognizing the need for external mental health support becomes a logical and responsible extension of the process.

Especially if structured tools (such as a PCM profile or a Needs Profile) have generated relevant data, Artificial Intelligence can be employed to further analyze this information, providing deeper insights into underlying patterns and priorities.

Moreover, recognizing the limits of internal mediation is essential. Explicit criteria and signals have been established to identify when external mediation becomes necessary. These criteria include:

•	Neutrality: The project manager becomes personally involved or demonstrates bias.
•	Escalation: Conflicts escalate into hostility or frequent emotional outbursts (see Glasl’s levels of escalation).
•	Repeated failures: Previous mediation efforts have not resulted in sustainable solutions.
•	Trust issues: Team members express distrust or question the credibility of the manager.
•	Complexity: The conflict involves deeper underlying interpersonal or psychological issues.
•	Manager involvement: The manager is directly involved or experiences role conflicts.
•	Repercussions: Team members fear retaliation or have concerns regarding confidentiality.
•	Resource drain: The mediation process excessively distracts the manager, impairing team productivity.

4: Define strategies

The fourth step involves defining specific actions to actively engage and motivate the team member, carefully translating identified needs into clear and structured strategies.

These strategies can be categorized into two types:

•	Internal elements: These include thoughts, shared values (which may be explicitly captured in a team charter), and other positive affirmations that strengthen emotional engagement and intrinsic motivation.
•	External elements: These involve shaping or adjusting environmental conditions, creating favorable situations, or defining concrete activities and actionable tasks.

When developing strategies, it is important not to rush into solutions prematurely, even under pressure. Remembering the value of deliberate pausing is crucial. Purposeful pauses allow deeper reflection, preventing superficial solutions and enhancing the sustainability of chosen strategies.

Additionally, conflicts should not always be viewed negatively; rather, they can be opportunities. Properly identified and managed conflicts can foster innovation, strengthen team cohesion, and boost motivation. Such productive disagreements often surface previously unheard or undervalued ideas, unlocking new potentials and opportunities.

In the case of conflict, it is helpful to aim for a meaningful compromise or a win-win outcome—keeping in mind that emotional validation itself can be part of that “win,” as it triggers positive emotions and fosters emotional relief. If the conflict has led to estrangement or diverging paths, strategies can also focus on restoring emotional alignment, for example by referencing shared positive experiences (“the good times”) and reactivating what once created connection. Reminding each other of past alignment not only taps into emotional memory but also creates a bridge for future collaboration.

An essential aspect of strategy definition is empowering and inspiring team members toward self-help, supporting them to independently discover solutions, even in conflict situations. The project manager acts as a coach, facilitating team members’ capabilities for self-guided conflict resolution and personal growth.

In all cases, the universally effective approach, termed by the author as “Strategy Zero,” should be considered as a foundational step. Strategy Zero involves simply acknowledging and emotionally validating the unmet needs. This validation alone reduces stress and negative emotions, immediately contributing to emotional relief, even before concrete solutions are proposed.

It is important to note that a needs-focused approach does not mean that every need must be met. In practice, some needs may remain partially or temporarily unfulfilled. What matters is how these unmet needs are handled. When individuals feel emotionally validated and seen—despite a lack of immediate resolution—they are more likely to stay engaged and emotionally stable. This ability to “hold” unmet needs without rushing to fix them is a key component of emotionally mature leadership.

Finally, using structured profiles such as the Process Communication Model (PCM) or a Needs Profile can greatly enhance the precision and effectiveness of the developed strategies. These tools provide clarity on individual preferences and emotional patterns, allowing tailored, needs-based interventions.

5: Sustain the momentum

Factors influencing the extent to which team members’ needs are met evolve continuously, particularly when changes occur within the project or its external environment. Therefore, it is essential to regularly reassess how satisfied or motivated team members remain and identify any new developments or shifting circumstances.

Implementing regular “pulse-checks,” structured retrospectives, and dedicated “empathy checkpoints” ensures sustained attention to evolving team needs, fostering continuous improvement and enabling timely adjustments. These checkpoints must be genuine and authentically conducted—any institutionalized processes should always reflect sincere interest and meaningful engagement, rather than superficial routines.

Additionally, integrating these reflective processes strategically with specific points in the project lifecycle—such as routine retrospectives, stage-gate meetings, or phase-end reviews—and aligning them carefully with existing project management frameworks will further support consistent and effective monitoring of emotional integrity and needs satisfaction. Whenever adjustments are required, the cycle can be revisited from step one: establishing emotional integrity. 

1.4.2	AI as a Project Companion

In the context of a specific project, AI can act as a real-time companion for both project managers and team members, enhancing collaboration and preventing interpersonal friction before it escalates. By analyzing team dynamics, communication patterns, and individual behavior profiles, AI can generate hyper-personalized strategies, proposing targeted actions, adjustments in team routines, or communication methods tailored to each person’s identified needs. This creates a leadership environment that feels more responsive, empathetic, and effective.

When tensions arise, AI can assist in balancing competing needs by identifying overlapping or conflicting interests among team members. Through pattern recognition and contextual analysis, it can suggest integrative solutions rooted in shared values or complementary goals—thus supporting the project manager in addressing tensions without taking sides.

AI-powered facilitation tools, especially integrated into chat or meeting platforms, help keep discussions structured and focused. They can detect inflammatory language, recurring misunderstandings, or communication breakdowns, ensuring that the tone remains respectful and the conversation aligned with core needs.

Thanks to its neutral perspective, AI can evaluate input from multiple sides without emotional bias, surfacing potential areas of agreement and offering real-time prompts to de-escalate emotionally charged exchanges. This is particularly valuable in complex or sensitive stakeholder settings, where human facilitators might be perceived as partial.

Beyond immediate facilitation, AI can deliver predictive insights and simulation, flagging subtle early-warning signals that point to mounting tension or unresolved issues. By simulating different conflict trajectories and mapping potential stakeholder responses, it enables proactive intervention—well before minor issues become major barriers to project success.

The model has three main components. Each components consists of a set of competencies.

1. System-Related & Execution Elements:
Creating Clear Paths to Action (Turning needs into structured steps)
Visionary & Growth-Oriented Leadership (Strategic alignment, ethical growth, innovation, and future-oriented team development)
Systems-Oriented Problem Solving (Seeing connections and root causes)

2. Relational Competence:
Service-Oriented Leadership (Prioritizing team members' individual needs)
Facilitating Open Dialogue and Feedback (Two-way communication and transparency)
Assertiveness (Respectfully expressing personal and team needs)
Attentive and Empathetic Listening (Active, respectful understanding of concerns)

3. Personal Mastery: 
Impartial Thinking (Objectivity and fairness in decisions)
Emotional Balance (Resilience, self-awareness, calm under stress)
Identifying & Managing Personal Triggers (Self-reflection, mindfulness to stay neutral)

Identifying and managing personal triggers involves proactive self-reflection and mindfulness, enabling leaders to remain neutral and composed, even in challenging or emotionally charged situations. This skill is grounded in structured reflection, mindful awareness, and early detection of emotional cues—both internally and from others’ body language—facilitating timely intervention and self-management.

Leaders practicing this approach openly recognize, understand, and learn from their personal weaknesses and challenging experiences, viewing conflicts positively as opportunities for growth, creativity, innovation, and enhanced team cohesion. With a genuine willingness to learn, adapt, and maintain humility about their limitations, they leverage tools like personality assessments (e.g., PCM) to deepen self-awareness and inform reflective growth.

The competency element “Identifying and Managing Personal Triggers” incorporates theoretical concepts such as Self-Affirmation (Steele), mindfulness practices described by Kabat-Zinn—particularly focusing on aspects like Acceptance and Letting Go—and executive functions including Frustration Tolerance and Impulse Control.

Emotional balance encompasses maintaining resilience, self-awareness, and composure in stressful or ambiguous project contexts. Leaders practicing emotional balance demonstrate emotional stability, actively managing emotions and impulses through tailored regulation techniques, particularly in high-pressure scenarios. Central to this skillset are emotional intelligence (EQ), explicit self-awareness, and emotional adaptability—including sensitivity toward neurodiversity and a proactive understanding of mental health.

This capability includes tolerance for uncertainty, adaptability, and a positive mindset that reframes conflict as an opportunity for growth, creativity, innovation, and strengthened team cohesion. Integral to emotional balance is competence in Mental Health First Aid (MHFA), including awareness of mental health challenges, recognizing signs of emotional distress in oneself and team members, and knowing appropriate immediate responses. Leaders should be capable of providing empathetic initial support while clearly understanding when to involve professional mental health resources. Protecting personal emotional boundaries while supporting others is equally vital.

Mindful emotion management, patience, and humor sensitivity—leveraging humor constructively without compromising psychological safety—further support emotional integrity and mental health awareness within the team.

Ultimately, emotionally balanced leaders foster authentic human connections, embrace failure positively, and use intuition wisely, cultivating an environment where emotional resilience, optimism, and mental health are actively supported, sustainably enhancing individual and collective performance.

The competency element “Emotional Balance” aligns closely with Masten’s resilience elements, as well as key aspects of mindfulness practices such as Patience, Beginner’s Mind, Trust, and a Non-striving approach.

Impartial thinking emphasizes objectivity, neutrality, and fairness in leadership decision-making processes, ensuring inclusive collaboration and equitable treatment across diverse teams. Leaders employing this skill effectively balance their responsibilities for impartial mediation with the pragmatic demands of project delivery, practicing explicit neutrality techniques to transparently disclose and manage personal biases or conflicts.

Grounded in ethical awareness, integrity, humility, and honest self-reflection, this competency extends to cultural awareness, intercultural cooperation, generational diversity, and neurodiversity sensitivity. Leaders remain unbiased, non-judgmental, and respectful toward diverse mindsets, consciously avoiding stereotyping or generalizations. They demonstrate political intelligence through balanced judgments, making explicit ethical decisions and practicing ethical stewardship of organizational resources.

Ultimately, impartial thinkers foster environments characterized by managed neutrality, inclusivity, and fairness, where diverse perspectives are valued and integrated into decisions that benefit both individuals and the broader organizational context.

The competency element “Impartial Thinking” is closely associated with theoretical frameworks such as Mindfulness—particularly the practices of cognitive debiasing and non-judging—as well as with aspects of executive functioning like changing perspectives. Additionally, it aligns with Balanced Leadership, a sub-component of the overarching concept of Servant Leadership, which emphasizes balancing opposing forces or interests.

Attentive and empathetic listening centers on authentically engaging with others to genuinely understand their concerns, feelings, and perspectives. This involves active and reflective listening, characterized by genuine curiosity, sincere interest, and the explicit ability to remain quiet and fully absorb what is communicated. Authentic empathy underpins this skill, helping leaders read emotional cues accurately, regulate emotional responses thoughtfully, and foster meaningful human connections.

Applying principles of Nonviolent Communication (NVC), empathetic listening emphasizes respectful understanding without immediate judgment or response. By adhering to Covey’s principle—seeking first to understand before being understood—leaders cultivate trust and open dialogue, creating an environment where individuals feel seen, heard, and genuinely valued.

The competency element “Attentive and Empathetic Listening” is enriched by its relation to theoretical models such as Schulz von Thun’s “Four Sides of a Message”, various types (loops) of feedback, and profiling tools like the PCM Profile or the Needs Profile.

Assertiveness involves clearly and respectfully expressing personal and team needs, ensuring these are transparently communicated and adequately addressed. It requires a combination of visibility and authenticity, enabling leaders to speak openly about expectations and boundaries while remaining genuine and approachable. This transparency is supported by accountability and self-activation, empowering individuals to take ownership of their positions and actively engage in productive dialogue.

At its core, assertiveness entails balanced risk-taking and courage, enabling leaders and team members alike to address sensitive topics confidently, directly, and constructively. Incorporating principles of Nonviolent Communication (NVC), assertiveness focuses on articulating needs respectfully and empathetically, fostering healthier, more collaborative relationships within the team and beyond.

The competency “Assertiveness” is closely linked to theoretical frameworks such as the Thomas-Kilmann Conflict Mode Instrument (TKI) and is integral to effectively applying Nonviolent Communication (NVC).

Facilitating open dialogue and feedback involves proactively establishing an environment of psychological safety, enabling transparent, two-way communication among team members. Leaders adopting this approach see themselves explicitly as mediators and facilitators, utilizing structured mediation and moderation techniques to support team learning and collaborative decision-making processes.

Clear, adaptive communication—including tonal awareness, non-verbal cues, and context-sensitive adjustments—is fundamental, complemented by a high degree of rhetorical intelligence. This openness and transparency encourage authentic engagement, shared humor, and enjoyment within the team, further strengthening internal networking and relationship-building skills.

Ultimately, by consistently fostering open channels for feedback and dialogue, leaders reinforce trust and psychological safety, empowering team members to contribute openly and constructively to both individual and collective goals.

The competency “Facilitating Open Dialogue and Feedback” builds upon the foundational concept of Psychological Safety and relates closely to theoretical perspectives on humor, which foster dialogue at eye level, reduce tension and stress, and positively enhance bonding within teams.

Service-Oriented leadership prioritizes the individual needs of team members, fostering a supportive, empowering, and collaborative environment that balances personal well-being with overarching project objectives. Leaders adopting this approach demonstrate role agility, flexibly adapting between horizontal leadership practices—such as servant or facilitative leadership—and result-oriented responsibilities.

Core practices include actively encouraging autonomy, meaningful contributions, and self-organized work styles, thus avoiding micromanagement. Leaders provide psychological and spatial safety, recognizing and accommodating individual professional and personal circumstances, and promoting emotional security and stability. By leveraging coaching, teaching, and positive psychology, they encourage personal growth, self-help through peer resolution, and professional development, while also rewarding achievement and recognizing individual strengths explicitly.

Ultimately, service-oriented leaders build trust relationships, team spirit, and high-performing teams by leading authentically by example, motivating with genuine enthusiasm, and ensuring active engagement, empowerment, and meaningful career progression for all team members.

The competency “Service-Oriented Leadership” closely aligns with the established leadership model known as “Servant Leadership,” emphasizing the importance of addressing individual values, motives, needs, knowledge, and skills, as well as respecting the reciprocity between leader and team.

Systems-oriented problem solving involves a holistic approach that emphasizes identifying connections and root causes within complex project environments. By aligning project goals with broader organizational strategy, leaders practice strategic decision-making that carefully balances immediate operational demands with deeper relational needs. Central to this capability are analytical skills and structured problem-solving frameworks that support comprehensive assessment of information, thoughtful input gathering, and informed decision-making processes.

This skillset relies on systemic thinking and situational awareness to understand potential challenges thoroughly and anticipate resistance, drawing on organizational change management expertise to navigate structural issues effectively. Coupled with curiosity, creativity, and a willingness to encourage innovation, leaders adopt an adaptive practical intelligence—often referred to as “horse sense”—which enables pragmatic flexibility and improvisation, grounded in mastery of foundational methodologies.

Moreover, being AI-savvy yet critically reflective of technological interventions underpins this skill. Leaders must continuously evaluate the appropriateness and human-centered relevance of digital tools, ensuring technology complements rather than complicates solutions. Ultimately, this integrative mindset of big-picture thinking ensures decisions are both strategically sound and practically effective.

The competency “Systems-Oriented Problem Solving” strongly relates to Senge’s Systems Thinking concepts, particularly through elements such as Mental Models and Team Learning. Additionally, it incorporates executive functions like Reflecting on Proceedings, Taking Decisions, Creative Thinking, and Logical and Strategic Thinking. Artificial Intelligence further enriches this competency, as it inherently employs a systems-oriented approach in problem-solving processes.

Visionary and growth-oriented leadership involves strategically aligning team efforts with clear, purpose-driven objectives, explicitly connecting day-to-day activities to meaningful, long-term organizational goals. Leaders adopting this mindset practice patience and strategic decision-making, ensuring their vision integrates seamlessly with organizational strategy and ethical values. They foster a culture of accountability and stewardship, actively encouraging innovation and creativity that directly contributes to sustainable growth.

Central to this approach is a strong commitment to continuous professional and personal development, supported by strategic networking that builds long-term partnerships and opportunities. By prioritizing future-oriented team and organizational development, visionary leaders cultivate an environment in which individuals and teams are inspired and empowered to achieve meaningful, ethically aligned outcomes. 

Trust serves as the foundational basis for the competency “Visionary and Growth-Oriented Leadership,” linking it closely to theoretical frameworks related to trust and psychological safety. Additionally, Senge’s concept of “Building a Shared Vision” adds a systems-oriented dimension, complemented by cultivating a Beginner’s Mind—as described by Kabat-Zinn—which emphasizes openness to learning, innovation, and collaborative vision-building.

Creating clear paths to action means systematically transforming team and stakeholder needs into well-defined, structured steps, ensuring clarity and alignment around shared objectives. By explicitly articulating intentions, expectations, and responsibilities, leaders provide strategic and structural support, which reduces misalignments related to goals, roles, processes, and expectations.

This approach emphasizes reliable accountability, ensuring that responsibilities are transparently assigned through effective delegation and outcome-oriented leadership. Clear action plans, combined with structured processes, help project managers prioritize tasks and balance immediate operational pressures against the necessity for sustainable solutions and conflict resolution.

At its core, this skillset promotes clarity, reliability, and strategic prioritization through structured time management practices. By maintaining a vision- and purpose-oriented perspective, leaders foster alignment within the team and organization, enabling consistent, effective action toward defined goals and outcomes.

The competency “Creating Clear Paths to Action” aligns closely with Senge’s systems-oriented concepts, particularly Personal Mastery and Continuous Improvement. Additionally, it incorporates executive functions such as Setting Targets and Acting on a Plan, Deploying Attention, and Retentivity.
 

What matters most?

9 out of the 10 competency elements were subject to the 4th hypothesis of this thesis, with people involved in projects (in different roles) asked to rank them. 

The weighting of the stakeholder perspectives in evaluating the skills and traits for needs-focused leadership is set to emphasize an operational focus. Therefore, the ratings provided by Project Managers are given the highest weighting (45%), as they hold primary responsibility for daily project execution, directly influencing team dynamics, motivation, and operational efficiency (Yang, et al., 2011). Project Sponsors receive the second-highest weighting (30%), reflecting their strategic oversight and critical role in aligning leadership effectiveness with organizational objectives and long-term goals (PMI, 2018). Finally, Project Team Members are weighted at 25%, acknowledging the importance of their direct experience with leadership behaviors, yet recognizing the more subjective and individually varied nature of their assessments (Conway & Huffcutt, 1997)

This distribution ensures a balanced evaluation, clearly emphasizing the operational impact of leadership while integrating strategic and experiential perspectives, resulting in the following ranking:

It is important to note that the competency elements, and even more their components, differ in terms of their developmental timelines. Some components, understood as “skills,” are relatively straightforward to cultivate and thus particularly suitable for targeted personal development and training. In contrast, other components require extensive time and effort to develop—often exceeding the duration of a project or even an individual’s tenure—making them crucial considerations in recruitment and selection processes.

Special attention should be given to technically oriented project managers who lack these essential traits. In such cases, deliberately evaluating whether to staff the project with a more suitable candidate is advisable. Furthermore, organizations might benefit from reconsidering the general roles and responsibilities assigned to individuals whose profiles strongly emphasize technical expertise at the expense of critical interpersonal or leadership competencies.

It is important to emphasize that ranking competencies by priority does not imply that an organization can or should sacrifice one skill or trait in favor of another. As repeatedly highlighted throughout the interviews and survey responses—and further underscored by the consistently small effect sizes observed in the ranking analyses—all competencies can be considered equally important in practice. The prioritization primarily serves to design and implement tailored development strategies, while distinguishing between traits and skills supports targeted efforts and effective resource allocation in individual and organizational development initiatives.

Using AI to Continuously Develop Interpersonal Skills

In the evolving landscape of leadership and project work, interpersonal skills are no longer a static asset but a dynamic capability requiring regular reflection, feedback, and growth. AI-based tools can serve as powerful partners in this developmental journey, offering structured, personalized, and responsive support.

By integrating regular self-assessment sessions—daily, weekly, or after key project milestones—AI tools can function like a digital reflective journal. These check-ins help leaders stay emotionally aware, recognize emerging patterns in their interactions, and track progress over time. They reinforce momentum in both motivation and mediation, supporting long-term development rather than one-off improvements.

AI can help leaders strengthen mindful decision-making by identifying potential cognitive biases—such as confirmation bias, anchoring, or overconfidence—in written communications, meeting summaries, or decision logs. These real-time nudges prompt deeper reflection and encourage leaders to re-evaluate assumptions, integrate alternative perspectives, and make more balanced decisions.

Targeted growth requires targeted input. AI tools can analyze a leader’s behavior patterns or learning history and curate relevant materials, including short articles, real-world case studies, or micro-courses. Whether the focus is on de-escalating conflicts, enhancing emotional intelligence, or embracing servant leadership, the AI ensures that learning remains context-relevant, timely, and aligned with specific interpersonal development goals.

Needs-focused Leaders = Servant Leaders?

Several interviewees and literature suggest overlaps between needs-focused leadership and Servant Leadership. However, setting aside Greenleaf’s original concept and related frameworks, the author associates the term “servant” primarily with a fixed role—implying a static rather than adaptive mindset—and the notion of exclusively serving others’ needs.

A key distinction highlighted in this thesis is the importance of self-empathy: genuine empathy and effective mediation begin by establishing one’s own emotional integrity. Much like the safety instruction in commercial aviation—to put on one’s own oxygen mask first before assisting others—leaders must first assess their emotional state, manage personal triggers, and ensure internal stability before effectively addressing others’ needs. This self-awareness helps avoid unintentionally becoming part of conflicts and aligns closely with assertiveness, balancing everyone’s needs, including the leader’s own.


Another crucial point is that not all needs must or can be fully satisfied. Often, explicit acknowledgment and emotional validation alone can calm tensions. While “servant” might imply a mandate to fully meet others’ needs, a “needs-focused” approach explicitly includes emotional validation, clearly distinguishing between empathy and immediate solutions.

Yet, a critical boundary remains: extensive focus on individual needs risks blurring the line between leadership and therapeutic roles (“red-couch scenarios”). Clearly identifying and respecting these boundaries is itself empathetic, protecting the leader’s emotional integrity and keeping the focus within the project’s scope.

The author deliberated between the terms “needs-oriented” and “needs-focused,” ultimately choosing “needs-focused.” While “orientation” suggests an attitude or mindset, “focus” implies deliberate practice, involving nuanced skills such as differentiating stimuli, identifying triggered judgments, interests, values, and underlying needs, and managing resulting emotions effectively.
 

1.1	MOVE Trust Model (trust tripod)

Trust develops gradually through demonstrated reliability, authenticity, transparency, and consistent alignment between words and actions. Trust, in turn, reinforces psychological safety, openness, and collaboration, directly influencing motivation and ensuring sustained project success. Given the inherent fragility of trust, any breaches of integrity can swiftly erode it, highlighting the importance of continuous, intentional actions to maintain and deepen relational trust among team members.

Grounded in the theoretical work of researchers like Amy Edmondson, trust and psychological safety are overarching themes throughout this thesis. They are embedded both explicitly and implicitly in procedural elements—such as emotional frontloading—and in the empathetic, secure execution of the process and its individual components. These concepts also underpin key competencies from the MOVE Competency Model, including Facilitating Open Dialogue and Feedback, Attentive and Empathetic Listening, Service-Oriented Leadership, and Visionary and Growth-Oriented Leadership. Rather than isolated states, trust and psychological safety serve as foundational principles.

The critical link between trust and psychological safety emerged consistently across the qualitative interviews and literature review. These insights informed the development of the MOVE Trust Model, which integrates academic research with empirical findings. Trust acts as the essential “glue” connecting needs-focused behaviors from the process model with the proactive leadership roles defined in the competency model.

The model was developed by analyzing trust-related statements from interview transcripts, clustering them thematically, and examining their underlying logic. These empirically derived insights were then validated and enriched through relevant academic research on trust (Mayer, et al., 1995; Kramer & Tyler, 1996, pp. 116-117; 265-269; Schoorman, et al., 2007). (Mayer, et al., 1995) (Kramer & Tyler, 1996, pp. 116-117; 265-269) (Schoorman, et al., 2007) 
The resulting comprehensive synthesis prioritized frequently emphasized trust-building elements from both empirical and theoretical sources. To ensure practical applicability, the model specifically focuses on active input factors—attitudes and behaviors that individuals can consciously adopt—rather than outcomes or static conditions. This final refinement underscores the model’s robustness, clarity, and real-world usability.

The trust model works like a tripod table, that needs three equally strong and stable legs: Openness, Reliability, and Good Faith.

OPENNESS:
Transparent communication
Open expression of one’s thoughts and feelings

Proactively addressing difficult topics
Active, unbiased listening

RELIABILITY:
Integrity (alignment of words and actions)

Predictable and consistent behavior
Creating reliable structures and frameworks
Constructive handling of mistakes

GOOD FAITH: 
Offering trust proactively (“trust first”)
Positive mindset towards others
Genuine concern for others’ well-being
Willingness to show vulnerability and accept potential disappointment
Initiating shared positive experiences


Side findings and reflections beyond the core scope

Perception vs. Practice in Needs-Focused Leadership

While some project managers consider themselves to be “empathetic” or “needs-oriented,” the question remains to what extent such self-perception translates into effective action. The author has previously outlined the importance of a practical, outcome-driven approach—emphasizing disciplined action over merely adopting an empathetic attitude. The quantitative data suggest a potential discrepancy in perception: for example, project managers tend to rate the importance of “attentive and empathetic listening” lower than team members do. This could indicate a gap between how project managers see themselves and how their behavior is perceived by others. Although this issue merits further investigation, it lies beyond the scope of this thesis.

Cultural Influences on Leadership Perception

After the survey had been launched, a follow-up conversation with one of the earlier interviewees sparked the idea of identifying specific cultural groups within the dataset, depending on the final distribution of respondents. Upon completion of the survey, two cultural clusters were defined for exploratory analysis: selected English-speaking countries (USA, Canada, UK) and selected German-speaking countries (Germany, Austria).

Preliminary results indicate notable differences in the perceived effectiveness of needs-focused leadership between these clusters. While this suggests that cultural factors—potentially shaped by language, norms, and self-perception—may influence leadership perception and practice, a more nuanced, differentiated analysis is required to validate these initial observations. Investigating these cultural dynamics further could offer valuable insights but lies beyond the scope of this thesis.


Executive Personality, Empathy, and Project Leadership

Recent empirical studies indicate that ‘dark personality’ traits—particularly Narcissism, Machiavellianism, and Psychopathy—are significantly more prevalent among senior executives and company heads than in the general population or lower management tiers. (Diller, 2021) Up to one in five business leaders may exhibit psychopathic tendencies (Chamorro-Premuzic, 2019). Such “corporate psychopathy” is not just an ethical issue because of the suffering it creates—it also potentially costs businesses billions of dollars annually. (Croom, 2021)

Project Sponsors often come from an executive level, as well as do influential stakeholders shaping the organizational project management culture. This leads to the question, to which extent such a type of personality may negatively influence the application and its effectiveness when it comes to applying a needs-focused leadership approach in projects. While this topic goes beyond the scope of this thesis, the question how these elements might be related deserves further research and analysis.

Systemic Perspectives on Needs-Focused Leadership

This thesis has repeatedly highlighted the interrelations and reciprocal effects between project leadership, corporate governance, and organizational culture. In other words, there are dynamic interactions between the individuals involved, the organizational context in which they act, and the broader societal and environmental frameworks that shape this context. These connections extend to larger concepts such as the Moral Economy, Economy for the Common Good, the UN Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), the UN Global Compact, ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance), CSR (Corporate Social Responsibility), and B Corporations.

Needs-focused leadership, in particular, aligns closely with ESG principles—especially the “Social” dimension—by promoting mental health, employee well-being, and engagement. From this perspective, leadership that focuses on both individual and organizational needs can also contribute to broader societal goals. The concept of “value” in leadership should therefore be understood not only in terms of performance and outcomes, but also in terms of its impact on people and society at large.

Exploring needs-focused leadership from such a large-scale systemic perspective could yield valuable insights; however, this level of analysis lies well beyond the scope of the present work.

Methodological and Conceptual Limiations

•	Gender Distribution: Nearly two-thirds of participants identified as male (64.5%), potentially limiting the generalizability of results and underrepresenting the perspectives of female and non-binary professionals.
•	Experience Level: Participants were predominantly experienced project professionals, with nearly half (46.3%) reporting over 20 years of project experience. This focus on highly experienced individuals may limit the applicability of findings to younger or less experienced professionals.
•	Role Distribution: The sample showed an uneven distribution across project roles, with project managers forming the majority (52.0%). The dominance of this role could bias results, as project team members (22.9%) and sponsors or higher-level decision-makers (25.2%) are comparatively underrepresented, despite possibly differing perspectives.
•	Age Distribution: The average age of respondents was approximately 47 years, indicating a relatively mature participant base. Thus, findings may not fully reflect the attitudes or perceptions of younger project professionals or emerging generations within the field.
•	Sampling Method: The use of convenience sampling via the author’s professional networks further limits generalizability. This non-probability sampling approach may result in participants sharing similar characteristics, experiences, or attitudes, which might not accurately represent the broader population of project management professionals.
•	Researcher Bias: There is a potential risk of researcher bias, including confirmation bias, due to the author’s prior experiences, personal perspectives, and expectations regarding needs-focused leadership. This bias may have inadvertently influenced survey design, question phrasing, or interpretation of results.
•	Language Bias: The survey was conducted in English, while many participants are not native English speakers. Language barriers may have influenced their understanding of survey items and potentially affected the accuracy and reliability of responses.
•	Complexity and Phrasing of Questions: Individual feedback highlighted potential issues regarding the complexity or phrasing of certain survey questions. This might have led to misunderstandings or misinterpretations of survey items, potentially affecting the accuracy of responses.
•	Ranking Question Limitations: Several participants indicated that ranking the leadership aspects was challenging, stating either that “all aspects were equally important and none should be ignored,” or that “the ranking depends heavily on context.” This suggests that a forced ranking may not fully capture participants’ nuanced views and may limit the interpretability of the ranking results.
•	Order Effects: The nine ranking items were presented in a fixed order rather than randomized. Non-randomization could have led to order effects, where participants’ responses are influenced by the positioning of items.
•	Lack of Psychometric Validation: The items used in the survey were developed based on the author’s expertise and literature knowledge but were not formally validated through established psychometric procedures (e.g., factor analysis, reliability and validity testing). Thus, the survey items may not meet formal psychometric standards, potentially affecting the robustness and interpretability of the results.
•	Small to Moderate Effect Sizes: The observed statistically significant effects, while meaningful, were generally small to moderate in magnitude. Thus, despite statistical significance, the practical impact or real-world relevance of these findings may be limited and should be interpreted cautiously.
•	Lack of Group Differentiation: The exploratory nature of the analysis did not support the identification of statistically significant participant subgroups. As a result, all item responses were interpreted as existing along a continuum without clear clustering or group-based distinctions. This limits the ability to draw conclusions about how different stakeholder groups may perceive or prioritize leadership elements differently.
•	Use of AI Tools: AI-supported transcription and analysis may have introduced minor inaccuracies. However, the resulting efficiency and depth of insight contributed positively to the overall quality of the research.
•	Geographical Distribution in Qualitative Sample: The interviewees were internationally distributed, covering a range of countries across Europe, North and South America, Africa, Asia, and Oceania. However, despite this global reach, the selection was not evenly balanced across regions, with a stronger representation from Europe and Western countries. As the sample was selected purposively from the author’s professional network, the perspectives may reflect culturally similar contexts and leadership understandings, potentially limiting the generalizability to other cultural or regional settings.
•	Geographical Distribution in Quantitative Sample: The survey included participants from 74 different countries, reflecting a broad international reach. However, the sample is heavily skewed toward certain regions, particularly Austria (41.7% of valid responses) and Germany (12.4%), which together account for over half of all valid responses. This regional concentration may limit the representativeness of global perspectives and cultural diversity, especially from underrepresented areas such as Africa, South America, and parts of Asia.

Directions for future research

Interdisciplinary Foundations and Future Research Directions

The concept of needs-focused leadership intersects with multiple academic disciplines, offering a rich field for further exploration. Among the most relevant areas are:

•	Leadership and Emotional Intelligence, bridging business administration and psychology, with a focus on self-awareness, empathy, and social competence in leadership roles.
•	Human-Centered Management, which connects business with philosophy by emphasizing leadership rooted in human values, dignity, and ethical reflection.
•	Intercultural Leadership, combining sociological and managerial perspectives, explores how cultural contexts influence leadership behavior, perception, and effectiveness.
•	Sustainable and Ethical Leadership, linking business administration with ethics, focuses on long-term societal responsibilities and alignment with ESG principles, CSR, and the common good.
•	Mental Health and Leadership, integrating psychology and management, highlights the growing importance of emotional integrity, psychological well-being, and mental health awareness in leadership practice. Future research could further examine how “dark” psychological traits (such as narcissism or psychopathy) among project sponsors or influential stakeholders impact the adoption and effectiveness of needs-focused leadership approaches within projects

An increasing awareness of so-called “non-typical” traits—such as neurodivergence or the presence of psychological disorders among team members—suggests that future leadership research should examine competencies specifically suited to these dynamics. This includes how leaders can recognize, respect, and adapt to such individual differences in a constructive way.

Additionally, future research could investigate which PCM (Process Communication Model) personality types significantly influence—either positively or negatively—the effectiveness of needs-focused leadership. Such studies could clarify whether certain personality profiles inherently facilitate or challenge leadership behaviors and outcomes.

Finally, the quantitative results of this research revealed notable differences in how various roles—such as project managers, team members, and sponsors/executives—prioritize leadership competencies in the context of needs-focused leadership. These differences offer a promising starting point for further inquiry. Future studies could investigate the potential gap between self-perception and external evaluation of leadership behavior, drawing on psychological theories or practical business management approaches, particularly within HR and leadership development. Such research may yield valuable insights into how perception, expectations, and leadership effectiveness interact in real-world project environments.

Beyond project contexts, future research could explore the applicability of the MOVE Model in the field of organizational change management (OCM). As change managers similarly navigate uncertainty, emotional dynamics, and stakeholder alignment, these models may provide valuable frameworks for fostering motivation, psychological safety, and trust during change processes. Investigating their effectiveness in OCM could expand their relevance and provide insights into their adaptability across different leadership domains.

Cultural and Role-Based Perspectives

Cultural factors also deserve deeper analysis. As mentioned in the previous chapter, it may be valuable to compare the perceptions of leadership effectiveness across different project roles—such as project managers, project sponsors, and team members—and across cultural groups. One area of interest is whether members of certain groups systematically over- or underestimate their effectiveness on specific leadership dimensions.

Further comparative studies might explore models such as “Scandinavian leadership” versus other regionally or culturally distinct leadership paradigms. Differences in self-perception, stakeholder perception, and measured outcomes could shed light on how leadership effectiveness is shaped by cultural and role-based expectations.

Future research could also explore dual or shared leadership models in project contexts, where leadership responsibilities are split—often between strategic and relational roles. These models may enhance team cohesion and decision quality but also pose challenges around role clarity and coordination. Investigating their impact on project outcomes and psychological safety could offer valuable insights for evolving leadership practices.

Beyond the project context, future research might also investigate whether similar needs-focused leadership principles apply in functional organizations. Roles such as senior executives, line managers, or functional experts may face analogous challenges and opportunities in fostering needs-oriented leadership approaches.

Operationalization and Validation of Models

While the PCM model (Process Communication Model) is scientifically grounded, the Spectrum of Needs model developed by the author is currently based on practical application. Future work could include: 
•	Psychometric evaluation (e.g., item analysis) of the model
•	Integration of the Spectrum of Needs with PCM or similar frameworks
•	Exploration of AI-supported tools for analyzing individual Needs Profiles and deriving leadership insights from them

The broader application of AI to such models could open new avenues for real-time analysis and support in leadership contexts, potentially enhancing personalization and responsiveness in leadership behavior.

The Business Case and Strategic Integration

One important future direction lies in making the business case for needs-focused leadership more explicit. This includes clearly separating:

•	Perception-based metrics (e.g., team satisfaction, subjective well-being)
•	Measurable project outcomes (e.g., on-time delivery, budget adherence, stakeholder satisfaction)

A differentiated analysis of these “soft logic” vs. “hard logic” indicators would strengthen the argument for the strategic relevance of needs-focused leadership, particularly when engaging sponsors and top executives.

To support such a strategic integration, the MOVE Competency Model could be mapped against the three dimensions of the PMI Talent Triangle:

•	Ways of Working
•	Power Skills
•	Business Acumen

This alignment would support a more balanced leadership development strategy that encompasses both technical and interpersonal capabilities, reinforcing the broader organizational value of such an approach.





Methodological Enhancements for Future Research

To improve the accuracy and generalizability of research on needs-focused leadership, several methodological adjustments are recommended:

•	Broaden role representation: Include more project sponsors and team members to balance the perspective distribution.
•	Randomize ranking items: This can minimize order effects and cognitive biases in preference data.
•	Pre-test survey instruments: Refine complexity and clarity of questions to reduce misunderstandings, especially in international or interdisciplinary samples.
•	Enhance methodological differentiation: Future research is recommended to adopt a methodological design that enables greater differentiation and the identification of significance groups—potentially through improved scale development and the application of repeated measures ANOVA.
•	Avoid data dredging (p-hacking): Given the richness of variables (e.g., age, gender, experience, industry, project size/duration, country, language, certification, organizational type), future studies should clearly define hypotheses and control for spurious correlations.

These methodological improvements are relatively straightforward to implement but can substantially enhance the interpretability, credibility, and applicability of future research findings.

Trust: Establishment and Maintenance

Trust, as repeatedly pointed out, is primarily built through reliability, honesty, authenticity, transparency, and integrity (i2, i4, i6, i8, i19). Interviewees stress the importance of consistently aligning one’s words with actions—frequently described as “walking the talk”—and regularly reaffirming team members’ competencies (i6, i29). This affirmation boosts motivation, positively impacts project dynamics (i6), and strengthens interpersonal credibility (i29). Moreover, trust emerges gradually from demonstrable competence, benevolence, predictability, and familiarity, though the latter may sometimes function irrationally (i5).

Notably, establishing deeper relational trust requires substantial time investment—typically at least six months for basic reliability and up to a year for deeper relationships (i4). The incremental nature of trust development was vividly captured by interviewees who described it as being “built in drops” yet easily “lost in buckets” (i17, i46). Consequently, preventing breaches of trust—such as avoiding behind-the-back discussions—is critical because trust, once damaged, is difficult and costly to rebuild (i2).

Trust is closely interlinked with psychological safety, a state in which team members feel understood, valued, and supported, irrespective of mistakes or personal complexities (i7). Project leaders hold a key responsibility in creating psychological safety to ensure that team members are engaged and understand the meaning of their work (i39). A practical example of fostering psychological safety is demonstrated by a manager who effectively filtered external pressures from senior leadership, thus safeguarding the team’s emotional well-being and performance (i39).

However, interviewees caution that creating psychological safety requires authenticity and natural emotional closeness, warning explicitly against forced or excessive intimacy. Overly personal closeness or sympathy among team members can obscure objective evaluation and lead to biased, or “blind,” trust (i4, i47).

Interviewees highlight specific leadership behaviors critical to cultivating trust and psychological safety. Leaders must practice consistency in emotional responses, demonstrating reliable impulse control to enhance trust within the team (i32). Furthermore, explicitly addressing stereotypes and prejudices early on, particularly in intercultural teams, has a positive impact on motivation, trust, and overall team dynamics (i1).

Additionally, a fundamental leadership practice identified is the use of a deliberate pause before responding or acting. This reflective practice enables effective management of emotional triggers, supports informed decision-making, and ultimately enhances trust-building (i27).

Interviewees underscore that trust is critical to facilitating successful conflict mediation and improving cooperation (i29). A trust-based environment helps team members confidently raise concerns, trusting they will be addressed respectfully and taken seriously, thereby proactively preventing conflicts (i7, i21). Personal interactions, such as genuinely checking in with team members about their well-being, further strengthen trust and support early detection of conflicts (i22).

Interestingly, interviewees identify humor as naturally flourishing in trust-rich environments, typically within smaller, more intimate groups of trusted colleagues (i34). Furthermore, trust proves essential for neurodivergent team members, enabling them to openly discuss unique needs and differences, thus supporting inclusive collaboration (i26).

Conversely, interviewees warn about potential ethical risks associated with combining emotional intelligence techniques and transactional analysis in leadership practices. While powerful, such emotional influence must be exercised cautiously to avoid manipulation, which severely undermines trust and ethical integrity (i19).

Effective delegation, when grounded in trust, forms the backbone of a needs-focused leadership approach. Interviewees emphasize that trustful delegation not only empowers team members but contributes to successful implementation of leadership practices centered around understanding and fulfilling team members’ needs (i49).

Concrete experiences illustrate the real-world implications of these principles. For example, an interviewee involved in youth social work shared a positive experience where trust-building facilitated open discussion on highly sensitive, personal issues, demonstrating the practical power of trust and psychological safety in achieving successful outcomes (i27).

Interviewees also recognize the strategic value of trust-building as an investment. Although fostering trust may temporarily slow short-term performance, its long-term benefits substantially enhance overall team effectiveness and sustained project success (i19).

Needs-Focused Leadership and Motivation

Project teams frequently encounter challenges due to high-pressure environments, uncertainty, rapid changes, and setbacks, such as cancelled product launches after extensive development (i17). Successfully navigating these complexities depends on leaders’ ability to effectively motivate teams and manage conflicts proactively, rather than reactively (i44).

Traditionally, motivational strategies relied heavily on fulfilling baseline hygiene factors like salary and comfort. However, contemporary approaches emphasize empathetic, needs-based leadership, recognizing team members as complex social beings driven by deeper psychological motivations and momentary context, rather than solely by static personality traits (i2, i5, i24).

A recurring theme across interviews is that explicitly addressing and satisfying team members’ personal and professional needs enhances motivation, collaboration, and overall project success (i2, i22). Consistently meeting fundamental psychological needs—such as recognition, personal growth, autonomy, mastery, and status—surpasses the motivational effects of basic incentives like monetary compensation (i5, i29).

Interviewees emphasize the critical importance of proactively identifying individual motivational triggers according to various motivational theories. Stephen Reiss’s theory highlights the value of recognizing specific individual motivators, particularly under high-pressure conditions (i12). Similarly, Maslow’s hierarchy underscores the motivational benefits derived from satisfying higher-level needs, such as self-actualization, which explains higher engagement levels in voluntary work (i19).

Leadership styles affect team motivation. Interviewees note the importance of charisma, as charismatic leaders more effectively inspire and motivate team members (i15). While humor itself does not directly motivate employees, fostering a psychologically safe and enjoyable work environment indirectly promotes positive attitudes and boosts team morale and engagement (i34).

Good organizational clarity also serves as a critical foundation, facilitating creativity and flexibility, thereby enabling successful improvisation during challenging project phases (i9).

Effective leaders practice genuine empathy, actively building emotional openness among team members to reduce communication barriers and enhance overall performance and conflict resolution (i3, i8). Superficial empathy, however, is quickly perceived by team members, damaging trust and undermining genuine motivation (i47).

The integration of Non-Violent Communication (NVC) offers practical tools for leaders to direct attention toward team members’ underlying personal needs, enhancing emotional management and conflict prevention (i8). Additionally, leaders must respect personal boundaries, ensuring team members have adequate personal and family time, thus supporting their emotional well-being (i8).

Effectively addressing personal needs early fosters stronger resilience against later conflicts, making conflict resolution simpler and more efficient (i25). Regular fulfillment of these needs proactively prevents conflicts, rather than relying solely on reactive conflict management strategies (i24).

Teams that cooperate—actively working together toward shared outcomes—exhibit deeper motivation and mutual support compared to teams that merely collaborate or coordinate tasks (i19). Project managers can enhance this cooperation by intentionally connecting individuals or subgroups with complementary or similar needs, fostering mutual support and collective motivation (i43).

Clearly and compellingly communicating project relevance and alignment with the organization’s strategic goals enhances motivation and prevents task-related frustration (i13, i17, i32). Successful project leaders establish unified motivational goals and individual narratives tailored slightly to stakeholders’ personal motivations and objectives, ensuring a cohesive yet personalized project vision (i32, i35).

An effective strategy highlighted involves senior leadership, including CEOs, directly communicating the strategic importance of projects to project teams, ensuring that the teams understand and personally identify with the broader organizational impact (i17).

Interviewees highlighted organizational constraints, particularly within matrix structures, where project managers lack direct authority over team members’ career progression or compensation (i13, i15, i49). Despite these constraints, leaders successfully motivate team members by providing recognition, visibility, training opportunities, and certifications, thereby meeting higher-level psychological needs such as acknowledgment and professional growth (i29, i49).

Moreover, systematically and proactively managing hygiene factors reduces demotivation and stress within teams, establishing a stable foundation from which further motivational efforts can effectively build (i9).

Inclusivity impacts team motivation and cohesion. Leaders must avoid premature judgment or stereotyping, especially concerning neurodivergent team members, whose daily capacities may fluctuate, requiring adaptive flexibility in work planning (i26). Equitable treatment within teams also strongly enhances team motivation and commitment, whereas perceived unfairness undermines engagement (i27).

Proactively addressing personal circumstances—such as religious practices, family needs, and individual sensitivities—builds trust, demonstrates genuine care, and boosts team motivation and cohesion (i47).

Effective motivational leadership recognizes that motivation is not static but depends greatly on the current satisfaction of needs at any given moment. This dynamic perspective highlights the importance of regular, genuine interaction to detect early signs of demotivation, especially challenging in virtual environments (i25).

Leaders often only acknowledge motivation’s importance reactively after encountering problems. Therefore, proactive motivational strategies—such as informal interactions, early intervention, and intentional team-building activities—should be integrated systematically into leadership practice to prevent motivational problems before they escalate (i25, i44).

Value-driven management approaches help translate intangible team perceptions and motivational states into measurable, tangible outcomes. This reduces misconceptions, improves transparency, and enhances overall stakeholder satisfaction (i43).

The transition from traditional project kick-offs to Agile lift-off meetings also notably improves project alignment and motivation by enabling deeper initial discussions and clearly establishing expectations and guardrails from the outset (i17, i20). Emphasizing autonomy and trust allows leaders to intervene decisively only when genuinely required, further enhancing motivation through empowerment (i20).

“Emotional Frontloading” and Early Relationship-Building

Emotional frontloading involves dedicating initial project time to social bonding, emotional connection, and developing a deep awareness of team members’ personal needs, values, and expectations. Establishing this emotional baseline early in a project influences subsequent team dynamics, communication, and conflict management (i14, i17).

According to multiple experts, emotional frontloading should precede detailed project planning (i6). The process involves fostering self-awareness, emotional integrity, self-esteem, and aligning personal and shared values within the team, thus creating a foundation of trust and openness essential for effective teamwork (i6, i8).

However, emotional frontloading must be managed carefully. While beneficial, it is crucial to avoid overwhelming team members with excessive emotional intensity at the very beginning (i7).

Building personal relationships with each team member substantially improves motivation, conflict resolution, and overall teamwork (i44, i13). Simple measures such as informal interactions—lunches, coffee breaks, and shared humor—promote openness, trust, and comfort within the team environment (i8). Being mindful of team members’ personal circumstances, such as family events or health concerns, further strengthens bonds and engagement (i13).

A comfortable, psychologically safe environment is crucial for teams to communicate openly without fear of negative consequences. This sense of safety fosters productivity and enables proactive conflict resolution (i8, i10).

Several interviewees recommend developing a formalized “Team Charter” early in the project lifecycle. Such charters explicitly define team communication rules, collaboration expectations, and shared values, providing clarity and direction for ongoing interactions (i4, i31). Additionally, guiding principles—distinct from abstract values—offer clear behavioral guidance, particularly during difficult or conflict-heavy phases (i38).

Initial kick-off meetings should explicitly address emotional expectations, individual communication preferences, and establish a baseline for managing stakeholder motivation and conflicts throughout the project lifecycle (i22, i17). Conducting these kick-offs in person rather than remotely enhances emotional alignment and personal connections critical to sustained project success (i17).

Brief pre-kickoff conversations clarify roles, availability, and individual needs, quickly aligning the team and enhancing long-term performance (i37).

Despite the immediate pressures of project deadlines, interviewees consistently highlight that allocating substantial initial time—up to 70% of initial project planning—to relationship-building and needs assessment enhances long-term project outcomes (i6). Investing time upfront to form cohesive, engaged teams saves substantial time and improves efficiency in later project stages (i35).

Early and deliberate emotional investment at the project start—through structured relationship-building activities—helps shorten the storming phase of team development, proactively preventing conflicts and enhancing later communication and understanding (i13, i8).

Effective emotional frontloading also includes strategic team member selection. Successful project managers actively select members based on both professional expertise and personal compatibility, ensuring the right mix of relevant disciplines and interpersonal cohesion (i6).

When facing value-based dilemmas or conflicts within teams, project leaders should initially focus on finding common ground through joint activities rather than directly confronting conflicting values. This indirect approach fosters mutual understanding and trust, easing the path toward conflict resolution (i4).

Experts recommend applying the emotional frontloading and needs-focused approach systematically throughout the team development phases:
•	Forming phase: Intensive emotional frontloading, needs identification, and clear expectations (i15, i16).
•	Storming phase: Active emotional alignment and proactive conflict management using the established emotional foundation (i15).
•	Norming phase: Defining explicit team rules, collaborative planning, and refining emotional understanding based on prior phases (i15).
•	Final project phase: Reassessing emotional baseline and alignment to ensure continued motivation and effective conflict resolution (i17).

Proactive and Preventive Conflict Management

Conflicts in project settings are commonplace but usually minor, typically arising from technical disagreements, differences in estimations, risk assessments, and stakeholder expectations (i16). Frequently occurring triggers include unclear agreements and inadequate expectation management, necessitating ongoing renegotiation (i43). Notably, conflicts often occur during initial or final project phases, closely tied to constraints such as budget, scope, and quality (i25).

The GRPI model underscores that conflicts primarily emerge from unclear Goals (80%), followed by unclear Roles, unclear Processes, and least by interpersonal relationships (i9). Poor organizational structures exacerbate this problem by forcing continual renegotiation of roles and expectations, leading to inefficiency and stress (i9). Additionally, ignoring diversity in gender, age, disabilities, language, or cultural backgrounds frequently escalates conflict intensity and hinders team cohesion (i14).

Early detection and direct communication about potential conflicts prevent escalation into crises (i2). Project leaders should swiftly address subtle emotional signals, unusual behaviors, or indirect communication that might indicate emerging conflicts (i2, i32, i13). Hybrid and remote working conditions can mask conflicts, making proactive detection and resolution even more critical (i2).

A proactive approach involves explicitly addressing sensitive topics early, particularly cultural and religious considerations such as Ramadan or other fasting periods, which may otherwise generate avoidable tensions in intercultural teams (i1). Providing intercultural competence training at the project outset further enhances collaboration and prevents misunderstandings (i1).

Conflict resolution is fundamentally a leadership responsibility; delaying or avoiding intervention often escalates issues unnecessarily (i21). Project managers must rapidly address conflicts at early stages to minimize negative impact and associated costs (i2). Effective leaders facilitate resolution through preventive tools, such as clear role and responsibility definitions, and intervene directly when necessary, by speaking to individuals separately before mediating joint discussions (i10).

Interviewees emphasize that conflict management should focus primarily on future-oriented solutions rather than dissecting past mistakes, thereby avoiding blame and personal animosity (i13). Leaders must transparently communicate the rationale behind decisions, particularly when addressing project constraints, to mitigate conflicts proactively (i25).

Formal mediation by project managers is relatively rare; however, informal mediation and moderation play essential roles, especially within international or consultancy projects where balancing stakeholder interests is critical (i22, i25, i7). Informal mediation is essential to avoid jeopardizing valuable international projects due to unresolved conflicts (i7).

Structured mediation processes, mirroring Lean Six Sigma methodologies, emphasize hypothesis-driven problem-solving and systematic follow-up. Mediation should remain finite, enabling teams to resolve conflicts independently without repetitive intervention. Successful mediation practices typically begin with an initial consultation clearly communicating the procedure, confidentiality, and structured phases involved (i28).

Integrating mediation transparently within regular project meetings maintains ongoing communication about conflicts, preventing isolation and minimizing escalation (i7). Project leaders benefit from reflecting on their communication styles, as overly direct communication may inadvertently trigger conflicts, highlighting the importance of adjusting approaches based on feedback (i29).
Allowing teams to generate their own conflict resolution strategies, such as creating a project-specific code of conduct or signaling mechanisms for private discussions, enhances the likelihood of sustainable conflict management (i28, i1). Healthy competition or rivalry within teams, if kept positive, can boost motivation and productivity without devolving into negativity (i16).

Effective team-based conflict resolution involves humor, informal approaches, and shared experiences, swiftly resolving tensions before formal mediation becomes necessary (i25, i34). Moreover, ensuring good organizational structure and clear definitions (goals, roles, processes) dramatically reduces interpersonal tensions (i9).

Employing knowledge management systems and historical data (lessons learned) can support project managers in resolving conflicts, providing relevant experiences and insights for proactive intervention (i33). Recruitment processes should also be carefully managed, as unresolved conflicts elsewhere in organizations may manifest indirectly in decision-making scenarios (“Garbage Can phenomenon”) (i4). This recognition helps proactively address indirect or hidden conflicts that could undermine team cohesion or effectiveness.

Misinterpretation of neurodivergent behaviors can escalate conflicts due to insufficient awareness or misunderstandings. Explicitly acknowledging neurodiversity within teams and providing appropriate education prevents incorrect judgments and enhances effective communication (i26).

A common gap identified in project management is the lack of understanding of organizational strategy among project managers, leading to misalignment within teams and subsequent conflicts. Clearly communicating strategic goals prevents such misalignments (i35).

High-performing teams benefit from conflicts resolved respectfully and openly, clarifying that task disagreements do not imply personal animosity (i44). Beneficial conflicts, particularly those focused on technical or innovative differences, foster creativity and productivity, contrasting with relational conflicts stemming from insufficient trust and poor team foundations (i38). Explicit investment in conflict resolution processes and guiding principles is essential to manage relational conflicts effectively (i38).

In rescue projects, the assumption should be that multiple conflicts (technical, managerial, interpersonal) already exist, necessitating immediate, structured interventions (i32).

Needs-Focused and Emotionally Intelligent Mediation

Conflicts within project teams are inevitable, yet their constructive management is essential for sustaining motivation, psychological safety, and overall project success. A mature team is characterized by its experience in constructively resolving conflicts, viewing them not as threats but as opportunities for growth (i2, i19). Effectively managed, conflicts can drive innovation, strengthen team cohesion, and enhance motivation (i10, i15, i43, i45).

Conflicts typically arise when team members’ personal needs, such as appreciation, purposefulness, personal growth, freedom, belonging, security, and clarity, remain unmet or unrecognized (i8, i28). Project team conflicts escalate when these underlying personal needs are neglected or inadequately addressed (i30). Thus, identifying these critical needs is central to effective conflict resolution (i28, i41).

However, individuals often lack awareness of their own underlying needs and emotions, complicating mediation by focusing solely on surface positions rather than deeper interests or values (i21). Exploring beneath these positions to uncover deeper interests, values, and unmet needs is a fundamental aspect of mediation (i21). Identifying emotions beyond immediate anger—such as frustration or insecurity—is critical for moderating emotional responses and creating openness for resolution (i21).

Mediation is a structured, non-advisory approach designed to empower participants to find solutions independently, promoting self-determination and responsibility among team members (i28). It involves clear identification of conflicts (“the fish must be on the table”), analysis of underlying needs, formulating sustainable solutions, and implementing regular follow-up reviews to ensure continued satisfaction and motivation or to address emerging issues promptly (i2, i21).

Effective mediation necessitates establishing a “safe space” for open, respectful, and empathetic communication (i6, i44). It requires mediators to model emotional stability, self-awareness of their own needs and biases, and a non-judgmental, curious attitude toward all participants (i21, i28). Essential principles include confidentiality, trust in the mediator, and a genuine willingness from all involved to engage in honest dialogue (i7).

Techniques such as reframing (shifting from problem-focused to solution-focused dialogue), empathetic guessing, and explicit acknowledgment of emotions or past experiences help unlock conversations, especially when participants initially struggle to articulate their needs clearly (i7, i21, i28). The doubling technique—empathetically speaking for participants—can also be valuable when employed sensitively and consensually (i28).

Effective mediation employs explicit techniques to facilitate clearer communication and deeper understanding. When participants struggle to articulate their emotions or needs, mediators may reframe “needs” as “interests,” easing conversation and reducing emotional resistance (i28). Attention to subtle cues, such as controlled facial expressions and attentive “lip movements,” ensures messages are clear yet emotionally sensitive—maintaining the principle of being “hard on issues, soft on people” (i12).

Moreover, structured reflection, including deliberate pauses or silence, can enable meaningful insights and deeper comprehension among conflicting parties (i38).

Being emotionally intelligent and self-aware impacts a mediator’s effectiveness (i12, i28). Self-awareness of personal stressors, motivators, resilience, and tolerance for ambiguity directly influences conflict management capability (i12). Project leaders thus bear responsibility for sensitively helping team members recognize their own values and emotional states during conflict situations (i4).

Understanding implicit fears, motivations, and needs behind behaviors promotes a constructive, understanding-oriented attitude rather than a confrontational or corrective stance (i27, i45). Ethical principles, often implicit, guide behaviors such as respect, appreciation, and self-awareness during conflicts (i4).

Effectively resolving conflicts through a needs-focused approach results in stronger interpersonal relationships, deeper emotional bonds, increased motivation, and enhanced psychological safety within teams (i8, i20, i25). Successfully resolved conflicts improve mutual understanding, clarify boundaries, reinforce cooperative dynamics, and noticeably boost team motivation and collaboration (i20, i25, i28). Conversely, unresolved conflicts demotivate and limit openness and transparency within teams (i14).
Real-life experiences emphasize these principles vividly. A conflict stemming from personal history between two team members was successfully resolved through mediated dialogue, explicit ground rules, and reframing techniques (i29). Another concrete example involved acknowledging individual insecurities, resolving a critical dispute quickly, and enhancing collaboration (i41).

Conversely, ignoring interpersonal conflicts can lead to severe outcomes. Technically oriented project managers who disregard emotional dynamics can cause severe conflicts and personal distress, illustrating the critical importance of emotional intelligence (“power skills”) in managing teams effectively (i35). Technological solutions also frequently fail when underlying interpersonal conflicts and communication issues are overlooked (i11).

While classic conflict-resolution models like Thomas-Kilmann are widely used, their limitations must be recognized. Specifically, the Thomas-Kilmann model’s categorization of conflict-handling styles as sometimes showing low interest in either relationships or outcomes is fundamentally flawed. Genuine conflict resolution demands high interest and engagement in both relationships and outcomes, promoting cooperation and collaboration (i19).

Effective mediation fosters innovation by creating an atmosphere in which previously unheard or undervalued ideas can surface and be collaboratively implemented (i7). Resolving conflicts transparently through a needs-oriented approach thus enhances overall team productivity, creativity, and proactive conflict management skills (i7, i28).

Leadership Challenges and Mediation Boundaries

One key issue repeatedly highlighted is the inherent difficulty managers face when acting as mediators. Since managers are part of the organizational system, maintaining neutrality is often problematic due to their vested interests, biases, and direct stake in the project outcomes (i7, i16, i21, i23). Managers may inadvertently become parties to the conflict themselves, complicating their role in the mediation process (i21). Explicitly recognizing and transparently documenting conflicting positions can help managers maintain objectivity and manage personal biases effectively (i7).

Managers frequently make the mistake of stepping prematurely into the role of a judge, attempting to quickly decide who is right or wrong. Such judgment often proves ineffective in resolving conflicts, as it can escalate tensions instead of addressing the underlying needs (i21). Genuine neutrality, therefore, requires careful, deliberate adherence to predefined ground rules and transparency regarding personal biases, helping leaders maintain trust and respect within their teams (i23, i29).

Yet, absolute neutrality remains practically impossible for managers who must balance impartiality with achieving successful project outcomes. Thus, clearly defining their mediator role and the boundaries of that role becomes critical to navigating such conflicts effectively (i7, i16).

A obstacle is the perception of mediation as a sign of weakness, particularly within directive leadership cultures where inclusive and consultative approaches are less common or appreciated (i7). Explicitly labeling an intervention as “mediation” may intimidate stakeholders; framing these activities as necessary and pragmatic “problem-solving” conversations is usually more acceptable and effective (i7).

Not all conflicts require immediate deep emotional resolution. Leaders should strategically assess when intense emotional interventions—so-called “red couch discussions”—are genuinely beneficial or if simpler resolutions suffice for immediate operational needs (i32). When conflicts escalate to a highly emotional level (referenced as Glasl level 5 or higher), addressing the underlying emotional needs becomes particularly challenging. Such intense conflicts often involve a high risk of emotional exposure, resulting in participants’ loss of face, thus making mediation complex (i24).

Moreover, direct exploration of root causes or deeper ethical and value-based issues in emotionally-charged (“hot”) conflicts often exacerbates tensions rather than resolves them. Hence, an indirect approach focusing first on immediate interests, rather than deeper values or ethics, typically yields better initial outcomes (i4, i10). Ethical reflections, while valuable for diagnosing underlying communication breakdowns, risk complicating matters further if introduced prematurely or inappropriately (i4).

Effective mediation requires clear communication, delivered “slowly, logically, and loudly,” to maintain authority and avoid misunderstandings amid heightened emotions (i12). Process-oriented thinking and careful planning are essential for managerial mediation, recognizing that perfect solutions may be elusive initially, yet immediate proactive intervention remains better than inaction (i21).

External support, such as professional coaches or consultants, can enhance conflict management outcomes, provided management is open to their involvement (i22). Where external help is refused or impractical, managers sometimes use indirect methods such as creating alignment against external challenges (“common enemy”) to indirectly strengthen internal cohesion and ease internal conflicts temporarily (i22, i25).

Clearly defining mediation boundaries and acknowledging their limits is essential. When needs or interests are fundamentally irreconcilable, openly discussing incompatibilities and being prepared to halt mediation efforts is vital (i7). Additionally, recognizing historical conflicts, which may still influence current interactions, helps leaders strategically address the core issues rather than being sidetracked by past grievances (i32, i47).

Some conflicts involve divergent objectives so severe that identifying common ground becomes more complex than in structured processes, such as planned organizational change initiatives (i5). Thus, a pragmatic focus on clearly defined project boundaries, constraints, and behaviors (aligning with principles such as the Harvard Negotiation Project) is crucial in mediating team-based conflicts effectively (i5).

When confronted by aggression or personal triggers, maintaining composure through conscious prioritization of professionalism and personal reputation is critical. Such emotional self-regulation preserves authority and effectiveness in tense situations (i29).

Genuine relationship deepening following conflict resolution requires thorough and effective conflict management processes, yet such careful attention is often compromised in dynamic and fast-paced business environments (i24). Additionally, mediation and emotional management are frequently neglected due to time constraints or insufficient recognition of their importance, further limiting the potential for genuinely positive and lasting conflict resolutions (i35).

Integrated Mindset and Skillset

Emotional Intelligence, Self-Awareness, and Inner Leadership

Central to effective leadership is emotional stability, also referred to as emotional integrity, which enables project leaders to mediate conflicts, motivate teams, and respond effectively to team members’ emotional and professional needs (i2, i6, i23, i25). Stability, however, is not a fixed state but fluctuates. Leaders should consistently reflect, recalibrate their emotional integrity, and prioritize emotional self-regulation as an ongoing practice rather than a one-time achievement (i40, i44).

Effective emotional regulation, as highlighted by interviewee I18 through Dan Siegel’s neuroscience-informed “brain in the palm of your hand” model, involves keeping the “thinking brain online.” This cognitive clarity reduces complexity, miscommunication, and relational friction, allowing leadership skills to naturally emerge from a regulated emotional state, thus enhancing confidence, competence, and effectiveness (i18).

Leaders must remain vigilant about their cognitive state, recognizing when they are open and empathetic (“above the line”) versus defensive or stubborn (“below the line”), actively self-correcting their approach (i44). Empathy, clearly distinguished from emotional entanglement (sympathy), ensures balanced, objective leadership, improving acceptance of difficult decisions and potentially preventing costly project failures (i3, i49).

Authentic leadership fundamentally relies on personal self-awareness and continuous self-development (i37). Despite its critical importance, project managers frequently neglect explicit cultivation of self-awareness (i3) Subjective self-perception often differs from how others perceive us—especially in areas like empathy. (i52) Regular structured self-reflection—ranging from daily quiet time (i13), weekly reflections (i44), structured post-event reviews (i3), and structured external communication analysis such as recording and reviewing one’s own speech (i12)—is crucial to maintain clarity, emotional stability, and ongoing personal growth.

Self-awareness helps leaders recognize their own motivators, strengths, weaknesses, and biases, often supported by individual assessments, psychological tests, and professional coaching. These personalized evaluations prevent a one-size-fits-all approach to leadership training, making personal development more effective and tailored (i13, i33). Leaders who acknowledge their limitations, maintain humility, and visibly demonstrate openness foster mutual respect, equality, and positively influence team interactions and project outcomes (i8, i10).

Increased self-awareness also mitigates negative influences such as perfectionism and imposter syndrome, preventing alienation, stress, and burnout within teams (i49). Understanding one’s personality profile—including individual needs and emotional triggers—enables early identification of stressors and personalized strategies for stress reduction. In this way, both the profile and the underlying model serve a stress-mediating function, similar to the broader role of needs-focused leadership. (i53) Moreover, explicit awareness of one’s personal values and consistently maintaining integrity visibly supports trust-building within teams and prevents compromising leadership credibility (i19).

Authentic leadership involves genuine care for team members, maintaining “high standards and high devotion”—technical excellence combined with empathy and emotional engagement (i40). Effective leaders actively listen without judgment, respect differences, and thoughtfully interpret observed behaviors (i26). Simultaneously, they establish clear professional boundaries, balancing emotional support with necessary limits to avoid excessive personal involvement in team members’ private issues (i39).

Effective mediation within teams requires empathy, personal integration, and a holistic perspective aligned with Otto Scharmer’s Theory U, emphasizing being “connected to oneself” and understanding the broader systemic context (i2). Leaders who have personally experienced setbacks or failures tend to exhibit higher empathy levels, better understanding team members’ struggles or perceived failures (i37). Project managers should occasionally embrace vulnerability themselves, enhancing team closeness and fostering environments where members openly share authentic needs (i47).

Developing empathy, emotional intelligence, and self-awareness demands consistent personal practice rather than one-time interventions. A continuous improvement mindset enhances the effectiveness of a needs-focused leadership model, facilitating ongoing adjustment and refinement of strategies for meeting evolving human needs (i41).

Mindset maturity or vertical development evolves gradually through persistent reflection and practice rather than chasing immediate transformation (i3). Vertical mindset development typically occurs in stages: Expert (individual contribution), Achiever (achieving results through others), and Catalyst (team-building and long-term impact or legacy). However, only a small fraction (around 10%) of professionals reach the Catalyst mindset, enhancing empathetic and transformative leadership capabilities (i3). Younger leaders, especially Millennials and Gen Z, often naturally embrace a “growth mindset,” actively seeking positive impacts on teams and society beyond immediate project success (i30).

Effective project leaders also need mindsets prioritizing optimism, positivity, and emotional well-being, balancing emotional support and professional clarity in communications (i6, i12). Explicitly managing uncertainty, maintaining genuine curiosity about oneself and others, and appropriately managing emotions and fears are vital skillsets enhancing a needs-focused leadership approach (i37).

Practical techniques for developing emotional intelligence and self-awareness include regular external supervision or feedback to complement self-reflection, ensuring balanced personal and role-related perspectives (i4). Short-term leadership training often lacks depth due to insufficient time to dismantle professionals’ protective façades; therefore, longer-term developmental processes enhance emotional intelligence, empathy, and overall personal growth (i37).

Leaders can benefit from mandatory personal development or self-experience, analogous to psychotherapeutic training, allowing them to confront and resolve personal emotional issues first. This preparation improves subsequent effectiveness as emotionally intelligent leaders and mediators (i37). Managers frequently underestimate the importance of emotional self-preparation before conflict mediation, often neglecting to verify their emotional stability before intervening. Emotional self-clarity (“self-clarification”) is especially crucial when leaders are personally involved in conflicts, as personal investment impairs neutrality (i21, i24, i49).

Structured integration of Positive Psychology and Positive Leadership concepts further enhances leaders’ capabilities, particularly in crisis management and employee retention scenarios, by focusing on success factors rather than deficiencies (i24).

Integrating neuroscience principles into leadership education, as evidenced in successful project implementations such as a mainline station project referenced by I18, boosts leaders’ confidence, competence, and courage (i18). Understanding neuroscience explicitly addresses core leadership competencies (or PMI’s “power skills”), bridging a knowledge gap in emotional intelligence within project management education (i18).

Despite clear benefits, resistance to emotional and human-oriented leadership approaches persists due to misconceptions about psychotherapy, coaching, and consulting roles, lack of relevant skillsets or motivation, and organizational cultures that do not incentivize these competencies (i37). Clarifying professional boundaries and roles can alleviate unnecessary fears and resistance. Additionally, explicit recognition and active management of self-worth issues can support project managers’ contentment with their own performance, further improving their effectiveness in managing team members’ needs (i6).

Leadership Mindset & Interpersonal Dynamics

Effective project leadership fundamentally relies on a leader’s supportive, moderating, empowering, and collaborative mindset, moving beyond directive control (i2, i9, i15). Core elements include openness, curiosity (“asking why?”), humility, and genuine willingness to listen and learn (i8, i27). Leaders embracing these qualities naturally create an environment where individuals feel respected and valued, free from hierarchical bias or judgment (i2, i14).

Adopting a clear “people-first” philosophy—prioritizing interpersonal relationships and team members’ needs even under pressure—is essential for lasting success (i13). This humanistic worldview underpins servant leadership principles, fostering autonomy, motivation, and team effectiveness by balancing personal and professional needs (i13, i17).

Explicit attention to social justice—recognizing and addressing intersections of gender, sexuality, and ability within teams—further cultivates trust, inclusivity, and openness (i27).

Importantly, leaders must differentiate professional interests from underlying personal needs, enhancing motivation, engagement, and effective conflict resolution (i6). Positive behavior, kindness, mercy, forgiveness, and constructive criticism contribute to compassionate, respectful, and productive team management (i8, i10).

Psychological safety is paramount, essential for team members to express authentic thoughts, emotions, and needs openly without fear of negative consequences (i18, i23, i42). Psychological safety fundamentally supports productive team communication, conflict resolution, proactive engagement, and innovation (i14, i35).

Psychological safety can even be more foundational than intellectual competencies or neurodiversity awareness, influencing relational complexity and team performance (i18, i24). Moreover, humor flourishes naturally as a clear indicator of psychological safety within teams (i34).

Project managers require specific interpersonal skills, notably emotional intelligence, cultural intelligence, resilience, mindfulness, adaptability, and deep self-awareness (i17, i22). Empathy—understanding others’ needs and their environmental context—and self-awareness are essential for effectively handling varied individual needs (i3).
Yet leaders must carefully distinguish empathy from sympathy. Empathy fosters responsive sensitivity without compromising professional clarity, whereas sympathy risks inappropriate involvement and blurred boundaries (i3, i12, i49).

Effective communication skills—including assertiveness, active and deep listening, moderation, clarity, strategic thinking, openness to diverse perspectives, impartiality, analytical yet holistic thinking, and body-language reading—are repeatedly emphasized as critical for effective mediation, motivation, and conflict management (i21, i28, i38, i43, i49).

Explicit neutrality during conflicts and motivational interventions is crucial, as biases or favoritism undermine trust and team cohesion (i47). Balancing empathy with clear direction is necessary; excessive empathy dilutes authority, while strictness without empathy fosters resistance (i12).

Notably, soft skills are most effectively taught by strong, clear-minded leaders (“hardliners”) since genuine empathy demands toughness and clarity—not softness (i12).

Effective leadership integrates coaching elements, focusing on meaningful engagement and empowering team members to autonomously identify solutions rather than immediately providing answers (i39, i44). Genuine curiosity, sincere interest, and effective questioning techniques enhance open communication of personal needs (i5, i21, i28).

Conflict mediation requires assertiveness, systemic thinking, self-awareness, impartiality, clarity, moderation skills, and accountability aligned with agreed-upon values, avoiding exacerbation of existing tensions (i7, i14, i21, i28).

Leaders must finely tune their “radar,” maintaining sensitivity to team needs without crossing into psychotherapeutic roles, clearly distinguishing professional openness from personal privacy (i40). Young or inexperienced leaders benefit by openly acknowledging their learning curve, actively seeking guidance, and engaging in supportive peer-group exchanges (i7).

Communication Skills and Techniques

Clear communication, active listening, openness, trust-building, psychological safety, and respect for diverse viewpoints form foundational skills every project manager must possess (i8, i22). Communication transcends technical expertise; thus, emphasizing interpersonal skills’ importance to technically-oriented managers and sponsors requires explicitly linking these competencies to concrete risks and potential financial losses (i36).

Effective leadership communication operates on three integrated levels: the individual person, their position within the hierarchy, and their functional responsibilities. A weakness at any of these levels can lead to communication breakdowns (i12).

Moreover, non-judgmental communication and explicitly avoiding discussions about absent team members contribute to trust-building and healthier interpersonal relationships (i13).

Communication in project management depends on tone of voice and body language. Tone effectively conveys emotional states and intentions, impacting perception and team response (i12). Leaders should consciously modulate their voice and gestures to manage meeting dynamics, emphasize seriousness, or reduce tension (i13).

Additionally, rhetorical intelligence—the awareness and intentional use of personal and others’ communication styles—supports motivation, relationship management, and conflict resolution (i12). Explicitly explaining one’s communication style to diverse teams helps prevent misunderstandings (i29).

Humor emerges as a valuable yet complex communication technique. It serves as a powerful tool for humanizing interactions, enhancing emotional connectivity, relieving stress, and reinforcing team cohesion (i8, i25). Authentic humor aids in conflict resolution, reduces tension, and facilitates open dialogue, provided it genuinely avoids ridicule or trivialization (i14, i22, i33). Effective humor usage demands considerable relational intelligence and an understanding of individual team members’ sensitivities and boundaries (i34).

Leaders should approach humor cautiously, avoiding forced humor directed at introverted or shy team members, as this could cause further discomfort or withdrawal (i34). Self-deprecating humor—where individuals joke about themselves—should be used sparingly, as excessive self-deprecation can undermine leadership credibility (i34). If uncertain about a joke’s appropriateness, it’s safer not to use it, considering potential exclusion or alienation of certain team members who may not share or appreciate the joke’s context (i34).

It’s essential to differentiate clearly between humor and fun: fun implies general pleasantness and lightness, while humor carries psychological complexity and potential risks of offense or hurt depending on context and intent (i34).

Moreover, humor at work involves psychological and philosophical complexities; therefore, there’s no straightforward method for its implementation as a leadership tool, and it should never be treated casually due to potential misunderstandings or offenses (i34). Psychological safety, while essential, does not imply humor without boundaries; societal and organizational limits always govern its usage at work (i34).

Coaching represents a vital dimension of leadership communication. It involves guiding individuals through structured questioning and reflective practices toward clearly defined goals, presupposing emotional stability (i37). Coaching techniques empower team members by encouraging self-responsibility and developing self-efficacy through open questioning rather than immediately providing solutions (i44). Genuine personal conversations, even simple ones like asking “How are you?”, can transform team relationships, breaking superficial interaction barriers (i37).

Effective communication requires sensitivity to intercultural dynamics. Direct calls, intentional pauses, and adapting communication styles to align with different cultural preferences build trust, especially with remote stakeholders (i29). Silence or minimal responses often signal underlying disengagement, resignation, or conflict; proactive and open questioning based on approaches like Carl Rogers’ client-centered therapy and Rosenberg’s nonviolent communication can uncover these hidden issues (i12, i27, i29).

Intentional pauses during conversations specifically help manage emotions and enhance clarity, particularly when communicating across cultural divides (i29).

Language use and grammatical accuracy impact perceived competence, as language reveals educational background, social environment, and professional competence. Grammatical errors might trigger Halo/Horn effects, influencing judgments about communicators’ credibility (i12).

The shift towards virtual communication, accelerated by COVID-19, has resulted in declining interpersonal communication skills among younger project members, requiring targeted training and enhanced support in social competencies (i14). Energy management is crucial in virtual settings, necessitating leaders to adapt their communication pace and style to avoid overwhelming team members (i29).

As advanced AI systems increasingly handle routine communication, human communicators must refine their analog interpersonal skills to maintain professional relevance (i12). Practical techniques such as self-recording with smartphones to analyze and improve clarity, effectiveness, and emotional intelligence in communication can enhance leadership effectiveness (i12).

Personality profiling tools (e.g., MBTI) can offer valuable insights into individual communication and working styles, but they must be used with caution. Over-reliance on such tools may lead individuals to consciously or subconsciously manipulate their results or present aspirational self-profiles rather than genuine reflections of their actual personality traits. Such inaccuracies diminish the practical utility of these tools and could mislead team interactions and leadership decisions (i49).

Needs-oriented project leaders should cultivate empathy, intercultural competence, robust communication skills, and sensitivity to different communication styles within teams (i16). Project leaders must also balance openness and directness carefully; overly informal approaches (such as using informal addressing like “duzen” in German language) may negatively affect perceptions among certain stakeholders or sponsors (i7).

Pragmatic Skills & Situational Awareness

Effective project management transcends mere execution of tasks, integrating adaptive intelligence and pragmatic flexibility—often referred to as "Horse Sense." This adaptive practical intelligence is essential for dynamically responding to evolving project contexts and interpersonal dynamics (i6). Core competencies include high self-reflection, excellent communication skills, genuine interest in people, and adaptive practical intelligence (i6). Additionally, external consultants provide valuable insights and pragmatic advice derived from their personal experiences, actively enriching project leadership perspectives (i37). Job descriptions should emphasize core human interaction skills and practical adaptability rather than overly detailed technical specifications (i6).

Key competencies for pragmatic, needs-focused leadership include clear communication, decisive conflict management, structured management capabilities, vigilant awareness of team dynamics, empathy, and clear role definitions (i2, i4, i7). Ideal needs-oriented leadership also requires impartiality, openness to multiple perspectives, and attentive listening to underlying concerns (i5).

Leaders must balance detailed attention to individual needs with a broader systemic perspective, understanding and anticipating ripple effects throughout the organization (i43). Situational awareness is foundational, enabling leaders to perceive, interpret, and effectively respond to complexities and interpersonal nuances (i5).

Effective improvisation, analogous to jazz musicianship, demands mastery of foundational project management methodologies combined with flexibility and creativity. Effective improvisation combines clear structure with flexibility, as jazz musicians master their instruments thoroughly before improvising (i9). Without mastery of these fundamentals, improvisation leads to poor outcomes (i9). Modern project environments, described metaphorically as "walking in fog," demand adaptive learning rather than rigid solutions (i18).

Today's project managers require a multidimensional skill set encompassing emotional intelligence, intercultural competence, resilience, introspection, analytical thinking, and AI literacy (i33). While technical skills are important and inherent to the practical competencies necessary for driving projects effectively—aligned with the "Move" component of the author’s model (i17)—successful project managers adopt holistic, systems-oriented perspectives beyond the triple constraints of cost, time, and scope (i20). However, an overly extensive competency list might create unrealistic expectations and stress, as noted by some leaders (i24).

Organizations should prioritize comprehensive social skills training, such as workshops on group dynamics and conflict management (i14). Many project managers currently neglect interpersonal dynamics due to inadequate social-skills training or an overwhelming focus on measurable outcomes like time, budget, and risks (i14). Generic personality tools like MBTI or e-colors can help leaders identify team members' needs and motivational drivers effectively, but should be complemented by deeper, nuanced tools and models (i41). However, approaches like Nonviolent Communication (NVC) face limited adoption in technical communities, partly due to its human-centered nature, individual maturity levels, personal attitudes, and limited awareness (i38). Understanding motivation and conflict-resolution frameworks (Maslow, SCARF, SDI) enhances leadership capabilities (i3, i42).

Horizontal leadership styles, including Holacracy and Agile, enhance stakeholder engagement and project success by flattening hierarchies and promoting autonomy (i17). Models for motivation and conflict resolution can be effectively applied in both training and coaching contexts, requiring only slight methodological adaptations (i44). However, project leaders must balance coaching with result-oriented responsibilities, acknowledging inherent conflicts of interest that prevent pure coaching roles (i44).

Leadership involves balancing individual team member needs against overarching project goals, recognizing situations when personal needs become subordinate to broader objectives (i20). Effective leaders maintain openness to diverse thinking approaches—linear, lateral, creative, and exponential—to manage complexities effectively (i10). Initial uncertainty in teams often affects openness, honesty, and transparency, necessitating careful consideration of both technical competencies and interpersonal dynamics (i14).

Understanding the distinction between therapeutic approaches and coaching is important: therapy addresses past issues for future well-being, while coaching pragmatically leverages past experiences to resolve immediate challenges or seize opportunities (i39).

Diversity, Inclusion, and Neurodiversity Awareness

Neurodiversity encompasses a broad range of conditions beyond autism spectrum disorders and ADHD, including dyspraxia, dyslexia, dyscalculia, Tourette’s syndrome, synesthesia, and high giftedness (i26). Approximately 16–25% of the general population is neurodivergent, highlighting the importance of addressing neurodiversity proactively in professional contexts (i26).

A critical aspect of neurodiversity involves atypical sensory processing, affecting perception, cognition, and sensitivity to environmental stimuli. For instance, up to 90% of autistic individuals experience hypersensitivity or hyposensitivity, directly impacting their interactions and comfort in everyday environments (i26).

Despite the challenges, neurodiversity can provide advantages. Neurodivergent individuals often exhibit unique strengths, innovation, and exceptional performance when their environments are supportive and aligned with their specific needs (i26). Gifted individuals, for example, perform exceptionally well when given autonomy and intellectual stimulation but might severely underperform under unsuitable conditions (i26). Similarly, individuals with autism or ADHD can experience intense periods of hyperfocus, making them highly productive under favorable circumstances (i26).

Currently, addressing neurodiversity in project management remains underdeveloped, despite increasing recognition of its relevance (i32). Integrating neurodivergent individuals, such as autistic team members, poses challenges, particularly because their preference for stability can conflict with the inherently temporary nature of project-based work (i32). Additionally, neurodivergent individuals often feel compelled to conceal their true needs due to negative past experiences or fear of misunderstanding, complicating effective team integration (i26).

A common misconception is that neurodiverse individuals occupy subordinate roles exclusively; however, they frequently hold leadership positions, bringing unique strengths and perspectives to these roles (i34).

Awareness and basic knowledge of neurodiversity are essential competencies for contemporary, needs-oriented project leaders (i32, i34). Project managers should incorporate neurodiversity awareness alongside other diversity considerations, such as LGBTQ+, into broader stakeholder management frameworks. Doing so supports effective communication, motivation management, and conflict resolution (i43).

Psychological safety is particularly critical for neurodivergent individuals, who may frequently feel misunderstood or pressured to conform, leading to emotional exhaustion and diminished performance (i26). Leaders should develop competencies in emotional intelligence, empathy, patience, trustworthiness, communication adaptability, and neurodiversity-awareness to effectively manage diverse teams (i26).

Neurodivergent individuals often have unique communication styles. For example, autistic individuals may communicate factually and directly rather than emotionally, potentially leading to misunderstandings with neurotypical colleagues (i26). Additionally, humor perceptions vary among neurodivergent individuals, risking misinterpretation or social discomfort when not carefully navigated by team leaders (i26, i34).

Leaders must be especially cautious with humor, given contemporary sensitivities to diversity, inclusion, and “wokeism,” which have made workplace humor a delicate matter (i34). Female project managers, specifically, face challenges in balancing friendliness (associated with higher pitch) with credibility (associated with lower pitch), emphasizing the nuanced demands of effective communication (i12).

Creating diverse teams is more beneficial than expecting individuals to personally embody all perspectives, highlighting the importance of respecting diverse viewpoints as foundational to effective project management (i10). It is vital not to single out neurodivergent team members; instead, addressing individual needs universally within projects avoids stigmatization (i37).

Social justice principles, including recognition, rights, and equitable development opportunities, are essential for effective teamwork. Teams and projects can serve as platforms to implement these principles, fostering inclusivity (i27). Additionally, cultivating an error-friendly culture encourages constructive acknowledgment of mistakes, supporting growth and learning within diverse teams, particularly regarding sensitive issues like gender identity or cultural diversity (i27).

Given the unique complexities neurodivergent individuals introduce into projects, careful management of their strengths, compensatory strategies, and communication preferences is required (i32). ADHD team members may require regular emotional check-ins and relational support for maintaining productivity (i26). Autistic individuals may face difficulties interpreting bodily sensations like pain or temperature, leading to behaviors easily misunderstood without proper awareness (i26).

Social cues or punishments typically do not effectively resonate with autistic individuals, emphasizing the need for tailored and sensitive management strategies (i26). Shame is often learned by neurodivergent individuals as a coping mechanism and can severely hinder open communication about their needs, making psychological safety paramount (i26).

Neurodivergent individuals often recognize and communicate effectively with other neurodivergent individuals, fostering positive dynamics when this mutual understanding is intentionally leveraged (i26). The ability of younger generations to articulate their needs and handle feedback constructively, thanks to early education in emotional literacy, contrasts with older generations, providing additional considerations for intergenerational team dynamics (i2, i12).

Spatial safety influences psychological safety, with both interrelated and critical for supporting vulnerable team members, such as those experiencing sensory sensitivities (i27). Addressing neurodiversity openly within teams is crucial for developing inclusive solutions, enhancing overall team effectiveness (i14).

While ideal leadership competencies for neurodiverse teams are increasingly identified, interviewee i24 emphasizes the importance of evidence-based validation for such competencies. Organizations should critically evaluate the scientific backing for specific leadership traits to ensure effectiveness (i24).

Process-oriented Approaches

Needs Identification and Communication

Consistently, practitioners emphasize communication as the central challenge in project management (i22). Yet, explicitly identifying personal and underlying team needs is not straightforward. Many individuals struggle to articulate their own needs and values (i4, i46), often confusing them with emotions, interests, or external stimuli (i46). Real-time interactions frequently lack analytical clarity, relying instead on intuition (i5).

A clear distinction between genuine needs, emotions, and triggers is critical for effective communication and project success (i6, i8, i49). Actively questioning, empathetic listening, and interpreting team members’ behaviors help distinguish these elements (i8). Needs should be specified as concretely as possible to avoid miscommunication and misunderstandings. Taking a reflective pause—such as letting a night pass—can sometimes help achieve the necessary clarity for this specification. (i52)

Practitioners underline the necessity of establishing safe, trust-based environments to encourage open discussions about needs (i44). Initial relationship-building through direct, personal interactions and trust is essential (i6, i8). Interviews highlight tools such as structured check-ins/check-outs (i2), informal interactions during coffee or lunch breaks (i29), and dedicated spaces for personal reflection and supervision (i28, i29).

Several practitioners advocate structured processes to systematically identify and address needs. Early workshops—like “workshop zero”—explicitly clarifying expectations, deliverables, and individual needs reduce future conflicts (i43, i5). Regularly scheduled check-ins (“pulse checks”) and weekly one-on-one conversations further ensure ongoing attention to evolving needs (i10, i40). Daily interactions and weekly feedback sessions reinforce team cohesion and continually address personal needs (i8).

Concrete methods for explicit needs identification include symbolic visualizations, “needs glasses,” focused duads or triads, collages, and “personal instruction manuals” highlighting individual preferences (“Do’s and Don’ts”) (i2). Structured personal conversations remain foundational, sometimes complemented by formal competency and personality analyses like Insights Discovery, AEC DISC Profile, and Assess by Schelen (now Outmatch) (i6). TMA Analysis (Talent Motivation Analysis) specifically aids understanding motivations and strengths to optimize team composition (i36).

Marshall Rosenberg’s Nonviolent Communication (NVC) is frequently cited as a valuable foundational framework (i2, i6). NVC not only offers techniques but emphasizes cultivating genuine intentions and attitudes (“being NVC”) rather than mere application of communication methods (“doing NVC”) (i46). Similarly, basic psychological models like Maslow’s hierarchy facilitate initial needs assessments (i32).

Practitioners strongly emphasize establishing a feedback and “feedforward” culture. Regular structured communication about past behaviors and future improvements enhances team effectiveness (i2). Agile retrospectives and structured post-mortem reviews are indispensable for aligning team perceptions with reality, addressing issues transparently, and continuously updating team expectations (i17, i22). Informal retrospectives—potentially off-site—further support genuine reflection and mutual understanding of team dynamics and interpersonal relationships (i13).

However, caution is noted that these sessions must maintain genuine, substantial value rather than becoming perfunctory routines (i7).

Despite the benefits of structured approaches, practitioners acknowledge inherent challenges. Written communication (e.g., emails) is less effective in addressing deeper human needs compared to direct personal interactions (i29). Explicitly stating values or personal needs may feel uncomfortable for many team members, suggesting indirect methods or experiential approaches could be more suitable (i4). Regular reassessment is critical, acknowledging that needs are dynamic rather than static (i40).

Practitioners highlight the importance of involving functional managers and, traditionally, HR to detect and address individual needs within the organizational context (i29). Proactive management of individual and organizational needs, clearly documented through structured roadmaps, enhances alignment and mitigates conflicts (i43). Transparent and detailed handover practices further improve stakeholder perceptions and future interaction quality (i43).

An interesting insight from practitioners is that validation and acknowledgment of team members’ needs through genuine communication often outweighs the direct fulfillment of these needs (approximately 80% vs. 20%) (i46). Proper practice involves recognizing, acknowledging, and communicating respect for expressed needs rather than solely meeting them practically.

Needs assessment is ideally reciprocal. Encouraging teams to analyze the project manager’s communication style and needs fosters mutual understanding, empathy, and improved collaboration (i47).

Effective Conflict Management, Mediation, and Motivation

A structured approach to understanding individual and team needs is essential for effectively dealing with perceived “difficult people,” thereby enhancing overall team functionality (i37). Experts stress the explicit recognition and understanding of team members’ underlying interests, values, biases, prejudices, and specific needs as foundational for meaningful conflict resolution and motivation (i25, i15). Quickly acknowledging these needs—clearly stating whether or not they can be fulfilled—helps manage expectations and maintains motivation, even if direct intervention is limited (i47, i15). Actively and sensitively addressing negative feelings and “pressed buttons” directly enhances openness and willingness to communicate (i5, i7). Investing adequate time upfront in understanding team needs reduces conflicts, improves smoothness throughout the project lifecycle, and prevents resource wastage through proactive planning rather than reactive problem-solving (i22, i43, i19, i27).

Emotional stability and integrity of the leader are essential prerequisites for authentic and empathic stakeholder engagement (i32, i15, i16, i22) Leaders should be aware that their decisions are often shaped by physical and emotional states, the causes of which may not always be consciously perceived. (i52)

Effective leaders manage their emotional energy by consciously scheduling critical conversations when they are emotionally stable and fully present, improving engagement quality (i32). Regular emotional integrity checks, including micro-retrospectives, mindfulness moments, hourly self-check-ins (“Am I being who I want to be right now?”), and structured pauses (“empathy breaks”), are practical methods to maintain emotional stability (i40). Project leaders must openly acknowledge when personal experiences influence their perspectives to mediate effectively (i7). Needs-focused stress reduction strategies—including the use of a personality profile—can have a replenishing effect on the leader’s emotional battery, helping sustain presence and resilience. (i53)

A Phased Conflict Management Process

A structured, phased approach to conflict management ensures clarity and systematic resolution (i21):

1.	Encourage self-resolution by parties involved.
2.	Manager attempts moderated mediation if unresolved.
3.	Seek external mediation assistance if internal mediation fails.
4.	Managerial decision-making as a final step.

Experts agree that conflicts, once effectively resolved within teams, increase trust and strengthen relationships (i23). Procedural models such as the author’s MOVE Model, Lean Mediation Project Management (i28), or Nonviolent Communication (NVC) provide valuable structured tools for confidently navigating emotional dynamics without becoming overwhelmed or delving into therapeutic areas (i37, i35, i38). Clear procedural guidance facilitates the systematic integration of soft skills into everyday project management, helping technically-oriented managers methodically handle emotional issues (i35, i29, i30).

While motivation often broadly addresses personality and long-term engagement, mediation focuses more immediately on situational conflicts (i41). However, motivation and mediation strongly overlap and benefit from integrated rather than separate handling (i15, i23). Strong interpersonal relationships and open communication simultaneously enhance motivation and reduce conflicts (i23). Clearly defining roles between project/program managers and dedicated change managers helps effectively manage these integrated aspects of motivation and mediation (i17).
Visual approaches, such as using Venn diagrams, effectively illustrate overlaps and unique elements among motivation and mediation components, making complex dynamics clearer to managers (i15, i44). Hands-on, easily applicable tools based on structured models (such as the MOVE model) facilitate rapid implementation, making them particularly appealing and accessible for real-world applications (i15, i38).

A needs-focused leadership approach, though often underappreciated, benefits project outcomes, reduces risks associated with interpersonal conflicts, and improves overall team satisfaction (i20, i15). Explicitly addressing underlying needs and fears in resistance management rather than superficial symptoms ensures lasting solutions (i2). Moreover, applying structured models proactively addresses common employee concerns, particularly in organizational change contexts, such as the widely-held question “What’s in it for me?” (WIIFM) (i41).

Recognizing situational contexts, such as urgency, affects the prioritization of needs and mediation approaches (i49). Impatience in projects often results less from actual time constraints than from unrealistic expectations or lack of open-mindedness (i3). Experts advise explicitly acknowledging feelings during emotional situations, even if immediate resolution or action isn’t practical, clearly communicating any deferred action (i29).

Emotional interactions and feedback must always occur privately (one-on-one), ensuring team members feel emotionally safe, respected, and validated (i8, i6). Shy or emotionally reserved team members often require gentle prompting and empathetic validation to express their thoughts and emotions openly (i8).

Regular retrospectives (lessons learned sessions) continuously evaluate and adapt mediation and motivational strategies (i15). Establishing a regular “change” or “motivation/conflict” agenda item normalizes discussions of sensitive topics, proactively maintaining team dynamics (i43). The structured model effectively serves as a basis for HR departments and leadership development programs, enhancing project leaders’ emotional intelligence and interpersonal capabilities (i31).

Experts caution against superficial attempts at handling interpersonal issues. Genuine employee motivation and conflict resolution require deep, authentic, and individualized engagement rather than surface-level interventions (i36). Differentiating clearly whether team members require concrete action or simply emotional validation (to be heard and understood) is critical to successful mediation (i22).

A challenging organizational situation described by an expert (I13) illustrates conflicts between longstanding employees and newer hires. Successfully navigating such conflicts involves empathetic listening, identifying dissatisfaction root causes, and supporting personalized developmental actions (e.g., training, education).

Intercultural and Adaptive Project Leadership

Intercultural teams benefit from inherent awareness of diversity, making explicit cultural differences easier to manage compared to homogeneous teams, where the false assumption of shared needs can cause misunderstandings (i2). Clear conduct guidelines, explicit management of stereotypes, and structured recognition of religious/ritual, social/communicative, and economic needs are foundational (i1). Generational differences further influence comfort levels in expressing personal needs: Generation Z tends to communicate directly, while Baby Boomers and Generation X often deprioritize personal needs in favor of professional boundaries (i5).

Leaders should proactively consider external factors such as civil unrest or socioeconomic conditions affecting team members, integrating these into their needs-oriented management approach (i29).

Clear definitions of the project manager’s role are critical. Project leaders primarily facilitate project outcomes and support but must avoid unrealistic expectations of being sole providers for fulfilling all team members’ needs (i20, i21). Explicit boundaries between “personal” (appropriately shared for effective connection) and “private” (not obligatory to share) are crucial to maintain professional integrity and prevent discomfort or overstepping (i40). Additionally, clearly defined “guardrails” should guide how deeply project leaders engage in personal and emotional issues, safeguarding against unintended intrusions into private matters (i16, i20).

Effective project leaders address behaviors and outcomes rather than exploring psychological root causes, maintaining clear managerial focus and appropriate professional boundaries (i10). Inexperienced project managers must exercise caution to avoid inadvertently eliciting deep personal revelations, which may surpass their capacity to manage professionally (i37).

Human factors frequently overshadow technical complexities in project management (i22). Structured yet flexible models (such as the author’s model) offer useful frameworks for emotional and interpersonal dynamics, helping technically oriented leaders address these softer issues without reducing interactions to superficial checklists (i36, i49). Avoiding overly linear or granular approaches in favor of integrated, continuous engagement ensures that team interactions remain authentic and responsive to dynamic needs (i24).

Empathy checkpoints incorporated into traditional project management phases can enhance emotional intelligence within teams. While formalizing these checkpoints may provoke resistance initially, genuine collaborative exploration of their meaning can mitigate discomfort (i39, i44). Effective emotional and motivational management requires sincerity, avoiding superficial validations that undermine genuine trust (i40).

Adopting a needs-oriented approach is beneficial, yet clear limits must be recognized—primarily when overly detailed attention to individual needs interferes with project execution and deadlines (i22). Special attention is needed for managing individuals who require direct rather than purely needs-based management, such as “drama queen” personalities (i22).

Needs-focused management in virtual settings demands additional patience and planning due to reduced informal interactions and increased cultural diversity (i15, i23). Regular but individually adapted check-ins help prevent perceptions of micromanagement (i29). Leaders should balance asking supportive questions without overwhelming team members or making them feel interrogated (i29).

Ultimately, responsibility for fulfilling personal needs remains with each individual. Leaders should focus on facilitating environments that reduce conflict triggers and encourage personal responsibility rather than attempting direct fulfillment of emotional needs (i21).

Error management impacts project trust and innovation. Amy Edmondson’s error framework helps differentiate acceptable innovation-driven errors (“right kind of wrong”) from repetitive, preventable mistakes that erode reliability and trust (i2). Balancing error tolerance and reliability supports a culture of experimentation and adaptability without compromising dependability (i2).

Project leaders must critically assess whether technological solutions, including AI, are genuinely appropriate for addressing underlying social or human-centered challenges in project contexts. This prevents technology from becoming a superficial fix rather than a meaningful solution (i11).

Structured training and certification—such as Nonviolent Communication (GfK)—are vital for maintaining professional integrity, depth, and effectiveness, preventing superficial, misapplied practices that undermine credibility (i46). Practical, structured tools are generally favored by technical managers for addressing leadership issues, provided they complement genuine emotional engagement (i38). It is useful to distinguish between competency elements that are more deeply rooted and take longer to develop—such as traits—and those that are more easily trainable—such as skills. This distinction enables more informed recruitment and staffing decisions, as well as the design of tailored personal development programs. (i53)

Formal competency frameworks (IPMA Eye of Competence, PMI Talent Triangle, Korn Ferry) can guide leadership development but must avoid overly granular, generalized approaches. Instead, frameworks should emphasize emotional regulation, integrity, relational dynamics, and contextual adaptability (i6, i17, i18).

Structured models for emotional and interpersonal skills enhance organizational and stakeholder relationships when thoughtfully applied. Integrating these approaches systematically through shorter educational modules (“learning bites”) makes continuous professional development feasible (i31). Organizational network analysis (ONA) further enriches understanding of systemic stakeholder relationships and dynamic interactions, complementing interpersonal strategies (i43).

Jim Collins’ Flywheel concept illustrates that consistently integrating people-focused approaches creates momentum over time, gradually embedding these practices into organizational culture (i42). Supervision, traditionally associated with therapeutic or educational fields, is strongly recommended to enhance leadership skills and effectiveness in managing interpersonal dynamics within projects (i28).

Organizational structures and supportive communication environments foster trust, but foundational elements of integrity and reliability at the individual level remain central for genuine trust-building within teams (i4).

Resistance to soft skill approaches remains common in project teams. Successful integration involves initially engaging supportive team members to demonstrate practical benefits, subsequently reducing resistance among skeptics (i23). Leaders should navigate resistance strategically, ensuring authenticity in needs-focused practices. Superficial or manipulative validations severely undermine trust and effectiveness (i40, i46).

Project management requires continuous adaptation, particularly when integrating new team members, necessitating fresh bonding and integration processes (i14). Awareness of group dynamics and adaptive leadership styles according to Tuckman’s stages (Forming, Storming, Norming, Performing) enhance team effectiveness and responsiveness to evolving needs (i27).

Recognizing the cyclical, dynamic nature of human needs—as illustrated by Ken Wilber’s figure-eight model—underscores adaptive flexibility, oscillating between autonomy and connection, stability and change (i2).

Beyond internal teams, the needs-focused approach holds potential for external stakeholder relationships. Structured, adaptive, and empathetic engagement improves overall stakeholder collaboration and project outcomes, validating broader applicability of these principles (i40).

Integrating Needs-Focused Leadership

Human Needs & Motivation

Bronislaw Malinowski laid foundational work in understanding human needs within social anthropology, influencing contemporary perspectives (i1). Human needs in socio-cultural contexts can be categorized into three interacting levels: religious/ritual needs, social/communicative needs, and everyday economic interests, all inherently influenced by physical, biological, and physiological factors (e.g., fasting rituals) (i1). Qualitative representativity in anthropological research demands balanced inclusion of these categories to avoid biases (i1).

The SCARF model by David Rock identifies five core social needs impacting motivation: Status, Certainty, Autonomy, Relatedness, and Fairness (i3). People prioritize these needs differently, greatly influencing their workplace choices and preferences; threats to these needs increase resistance, while their support enhances engagement (i3). Negative emotions generally result from harmed needs, while fulfilled needs evoke positive emotions (i3).

The human factor is increasingly seen as a “hidden champion” in project management—critical yet often underestimated (i25). Projects should not serve processes; rather, processes should serve people, as projects are fundamentally done for, with, and by people (i19). Delivering a project on time, budget, and scope alone is insufficient; success requires creating meaningful, positive differentiation for users (i19).

Employees typically have the capability to perform their tasks effectively. Problems primarily arise from human elements such as communication gaps, misalignment, unmet emotional needs, and unclear understanding of individual blockers (i39). Technical elements of project management—such as clearly defined objectives and boundaries—directly meet emotional needs for clarity and influence team culture and well-being (i37).

Effective project managers must carefully balance project objectives with the individual needs of team members, ensuring neither consistently overrides the other, to maintain productivity and motivation (i47). PMO Value Ring acknowledges the critical role of human needs by incorporating stakeholder management as a key service, explicitly addressing stakeholder motivations, maturity levels, and cultural aspects (i42).

Effective leadership involves understanding individual stakeholder needs within broader organizational contexts (i32). Leaders are advised to consciously perform a targeted “needs scan” of key stakeholders to prioritize attention and resources effectively (i32). Successful needs-focused leadership does not cater indiscriminately to every individual need, but rather transparently addresses and manages unmet needs, ensuring team members understand the rationale behind decisions (i35).

Emotional intelligence, encompassing self-awareness, social awareness, self-management, and genuine care for team members, is essential in project management (i45). Younger generations increasingly demand emotional intelligence at work and are likely to leave environments neglecting emotional well-being (i39).

A notable personal leadership success is when former employees return to the organization, highlighting the strong emotional bonds created and the effectiveness of leadership practices (i24). Conversely, neglecting team members’ emotional needs can lead to misaligned strategies, frustration, disengagement, reduced quality, conflicts, and increased employee turnover (i38). For instance, excessive need for validation due to unmet security and reassurance needs in a senior project manager can seriously impact performance (i38).

An illustrative case highlighting severe consequences of neglecting human needs involved leadership ignoring the needs of a disabled colleague, resulting in profound personal and professional harm. This example underscores the critical importance of need-oriented management practices (i27).

Three important abstract needs identified by experts are clarity, direction, and autonomy/freedom (i3). Clarity connects coherence (rational alignment of goals, methods, and motives) with resonance (emotional alignment, perceived meaningfulness, and appropriateness) (i9). Orientation emerges as a primary need of stakeholders, structured along three levels: “What” (Coherence), “How” (Resonance), and “Why” (Resonance) (i9).

Providing basic organizational structures such as orientation, coherence, resonance, clearly defined roles, goals, and processes should be considered hygiene factors—necessary for preventing dissatisfaction but insufficient alone for sustained motivation (i9).

Understanding and addressing both individual and collective needs are fundamental for achieving long-term alignment and success within teams and broader society (i30). Project team members each have unique personal needs linked to roles and aspirations, including career advancement, skill development, or overall job satisfaction (i30). Employees differ in their preferred level of emotional engagement at work, ranging from highly personal interactions to strictly task-focused attitudes (i39).

Proactively addressing individual needs can lead to substantial organizational benefits. For example, when two previously unknown employees’ individual needs were acknowledged, they achieved executive recognition and leadership roles (i10). Conversely, a traditional project manager who intervenes only upon visible performance issues fails to effectively address underlying motivational problems, exacerbating team issues (i48).

Conflicts and motivational issues escalate if underlying needs remain unaddressed. Organizations neglecting employee well-being and personal growth foster stressful and unproductive environments (i31). Effective needs-oriented leadership mitigates such conflicts and enhances overall organizational health. 

There is generally little difference in the nature of conflicts across organization sizes; fundamental human needs remain central regardless of organizational scale (i28).

ESG principles, specifically the “Social” element, inherently require prioritizing human needs, including mental health and flexible work arrangements, reflecting a broader shift toward recognizing individual and collective well-being as a business imperative (i41). Organizations imposing predetermined training paths without considering individual professional aspirations face strong criticism and risk disengaging employees (i49).

Technology projects demonstrate wide variability in expectations and needs even within seemingly homogeneous user groups, highlighting the importance of nuanced user understanding (i11). However, current research on assistive technologies often prioritizes basic functionality and immediate usability over genuine long-term improvements in well-being and quality of life (i11).

Implementing a needs-focused leadership approach was notably successful in a major COVID-19 project, emphasizing emotional stability, clear communication, and maintaining happiness despite intense challenges (i23).

Researching the influence of stakeholder interests on project outcomes regarding human needs is inherently complex, requiring continuous reality-checking and potential methodological adjustments throughout the research process (i1).

Needs-focused leadership & project success

Explicit attention to human needs in project management enhances measurable outcomes such as team and stakeholder satisfaction, reduced staff turnover, and overall project effectiveness (i2). A deeper understanding of stakeholder needs consistently correlates with improved project and business performance (i3). Empathetic leadership directly contributes to better business outcomes, organizational clarity, and effective prioritization, yet many organizations lack adequate frameworks to quantify these benefits clearly (i3).

Neglecting stakeholders’ true needs has led to notable project failures. Two software projects were canceled after substantial investments precisely due to such oversight (i3). Similarly, organizational change projects have failed because executives misunderstood or ignored team members’ needs, indicating that both inward (project team) and outward (beneficiary) stakeholder orientations must be considered (i3).

Effective leadership strikes a balance between clear direction and team autonomy, simplifying goals and expectations to enhance success (i3). Both over-delegation and excessive direction have contributed to project failures, suggesting a nuanced middle path is crucial (i3). Unrealistic expectations set by executives have consistently been detrimental, irrespective of project duration or resources (i3).

Team members’ personal needs often manifest as career advancement, recognition, visibility, or deeper personal concerns such as health and family situations. Recognizing these fosters team cohesion and contributes to project success (i6). Prioritizing individual emotional states directly aids in achieving project objectives, particularly through improved comfort, efficiency, and reduced stress (i8). However, balancing individual and team needs remains critical for optimal outcomes (i10).

Addressing interpersonal dynamics proactively throughout a project enhances team performance and effectiveness (i14). Effective mediation—addressing conflicts promptly and openly—positively impacts team trust, respect, and ultimately project outcomes (i6). Indicators of successful mediation include reduced conflict frequency and intensity, improved collaborative output quality, and greater team satisfaction. Nonetheless, these indicators should be internally monitored rather than publicly highlighted to maintain trust (i7).

Ignoring interpersonal leadership frequently results in severe negative consequences, such as project failures, budget overruns, delays, high team turnover, and loss of knowledge (i36).

Traditionally, project managers are evaluated primarily by the “iron triangle” (time, cost, quality), rather than by team motivation or satisfaction (i38). Yet, project success involves subjective perceptions, highlighting the importance of human experience beyond purely objective metrics (i30). Subjective measures, including team satisfaction, stakeholder alignment, and readiness for future challenges, are critical components of true project success (i14, i16, i30).

Alternative approaches to measuring success include frequent pulse surveys, qualitative feedback, Net Promoter Scores (NPS), and 360-degree feedback, despite their potential biases (i3). Perception-based metrics such as NPS effectively capture stakeholder alignment on project value and success, recognizing customer perceptions as more influential than those of the project team or executives (i30).

Metrics such as team retention rates, reduced conflict frequency, burnout rates, milestone achievements, decreased wasted meeting time, and increased productivity can also serve as practical indicators of the success of needs-oriented management (i15, i22). Yet, establishing direct causality between meeting individual needs and project outcomes remains challenging, with strategic organizational metrics indirectly reflecting these impacts (i10).

A binary view of project success (successful vs. unsuccessful) is increasingly inadequate. A nuanced scale (0–10), distinguishing clear failures (0–6), mixed results (7–8), and successes (9–10), better captures the complexity and variability inherent in project outcomes (i30). Measuring effectiveness can thus involve feedback loops, regular surveys, personal check-ins, and comparative analyses across similarly managed projects (i15, i25).

Needs-focused leadership improves project speed by reducing friction and wasted effort through better team dynamics (i5). Particularly in shorter projects, such leadership approaches become crucial for enhancing task accomplishment (i8). Maintaining team members’ inspiration, health, happiness, and providing professional growth opportunities are essential for sustained long-term success (i31).

Technical vs. Interpersonal Skills

Historically, project management emphasized technical skills such as scheduling, budgeting, planning, clear scope definition, and structured milestones as essential foundations (i15, i9). Clarifying what is explicitly “out-of-scope” and establishing clear “non-goals”, especially in consulting and creative contexts, has proven essential but remains underutilized despite aiding efficient resource management and reducing ambiguity (i37).

However, misconceptions persist that project management primarily involves simple task organization or list-making, undervaluing its true complexity and professionalism (i20). Additionally, coherence—the absence of contradictions or gaps in project structure, roles, and processes—is crucial yet frequently overlooked (i9). Purely technical management, without adequate attention to human factors, often results in reduced initiative, unclear responsibilities, and delayed project timelines (i37).

Standardized project management tools, such as project handbooks, continue to play a critical role by providing teams with a shared language, thus quickly creating clarity and common understanding (i37).

Effective project management increasingly relies on adeptly handling emotional and interpersonal dynamics, conflicts, motivation, and stakeholder relationships (i31). Empathy—deeply understanding individual team members’ perspectives, priorities, and concerns—is particularly vital for fostering shared purpose and enhancing team motivation (i30).

Despite their critical importance, emotional and interpersonal issues remain complex and difficult to systematically measure or address, often making them less attractive to project managers pressured to focus on quantifiable, technical metrics (i36, i39). Project managers historically originating from technical backgrounds often lack formal training or encouragement in emotional intelligence and human-centered leadership, exacerbating the neglect of these crucial interpersonal aspects (i40).

Additionally, project managers frequently resist engaging deeply in emotional dynamics due to fears of crossing into psychotherapeutic territory or becoming overwhelmed by individual emotional issues (i37, i39). Leaders and stakeholders also regularly struggle with managing personal and interpersonal risks effectively, particularly in high-stakes or crisis project environments (i18, i32).

Given inherent limitations, such as authentic interpersonal skills being difficult to teach individuals who naturally lack them, dual leadership models—pairing technically adept managers with emotionally intelligent counterparts—become increasingly valuable (i36, i39). Personal experiences reported from interviewees illustrate improvements in PMO reputation, project morale, and senior leadership relationships when employing dual leadership systems (i39).

Effective leadership ideally combines genuine emotional integrity (“missionary”) with pragmatic performance outcomes (“mercenary”), thereby aligning authentic interpersonal engagement with tangible results in team performance (i40). Moreover, effective interpersonal leadership involves balancing autonomy with maturity, empowering team members according to their capabilities rather than granting autonomy unconditionally (i35).

However, exclusively soft-skill-oriented approaches are cautioned against, as they may inadvertently create inefficiency through excessive consensus-seeking or people-pleasing behavior, highlighting the importance of integrating robust technical rigor alongside emotional intelligence (i37).

Agile methodologies, characterized by flexibility and self-organization, complement structured planning but should not replace clear goals, roles, and defined processes (i9). Misunderstandings of Agile approaches, due to insufficient training or preparation, can result in increased stress, conflicts, and reliance on tools such as Jira, often signaling underlying unresolved interpersonal dynamics (i14).

Successful teams using Agile principles regularly dedicate time—approximately 20%—for collective reflection on roles, processes, and interpersonal dynamics to maintain team coherence and mitigate accumulated issues negatively impacting performance (i9).

Situational leadership theory further reinforces that effective support from project managers to team members must adapt depending on specific project phases, situations, or individual team members’ needs and expectations (i33).

Exploring and explicitly embedding needs-oriented approaches within standard stakeholder management models enhances project effectiveness by systematically addressing personal goals and motivations beyond purely technical objectives (i25, i23). While valuable, addressing individual team members’ specific needs remains challenging due to resource constraints, particularly in smaller-scale projects. Consequently, selective application to key team members or leaders in larger or more complex projects is recommended (i30, i23).

The integration of structured emotional and conflict-management frameworks into established standards, such as PMI’s PMBOK and PMP certifications, is advocated by experts as it could legitimize these aspects and encourage their adoption among technically focused project managers (i35, i39). Indeed, PMI’s MORE model (Manage perceptions, Ownership, Reassessing project parameters, Expanding perspectives) explicitly incorporates stakeholder needs and emotional intelligence components, promoting wider awareness among emerging project managers (i30, i39).

Insights from interdisciplinary transitions—such as from technology to psychology, psychotherapy, and neuroscience—underscore the influence psychological understanding can have on project management approaches, leadership coaching, and organizational development (i18). This transition highlights the untapped potential of developing psychological and emotional competencies within project management, offering substantial opportunities for improvement at the PMO and individual manager levels (i42).

Regional insights, such as observations from Central Asia, illustrate how predominant technical approaches, neglecting human signals and emotional intelligence, ultimately reduce project effectiveness, emphasizing the global need for broader psychological and emotional integration in project management training (i41).

Tools like the Team Management System (TMS) provide valuable methods for assessing and addressing cultural and individual behavioral impacts within project teams, thus directly supporting interpersonal skills application (i32). Even highly technical roles, including robot programming, benefit from empathy, further reinforcing the universal relevance of emotional intelligence across diverse project contexts (i45).

Finally, effective project leadership explicitly avoids the goal of overly harmonious teams devoid of differences. Instead, a dynamic balance is sought, where productive disagreements and competitive spirits actively contribute positively to project success and innovation (i16).

Given these insights, experts strongly recommend continuous, personalized executive-level training in interpersonal leadership skills as essential to sustained organizational success, despite organizational tendencies toward cheaper, standardized technical training (i36).

A structured focus group discussion (FGD) involving multiple experts is also recommended as a practical next step to further validate and enrich current findings regarding effective integration of technical and interpersonal skills, particularly in relation to needs-focused leadership in project management (i33).

Cultural Awareness & Intercultural Management

Cultural diversity necessitates respect, sensitivity, and awareness from leaders (i10). Project managers must consciously recognize cultural differences to adapt their interaction styles, feedback methods, and communication strategies within diverse teams (i47). Furthermore, acknowledging one’s personal biases and developing cultural self-awareness is fundamental for effective intercultural engagement (i27).

Communication in multicultural environments can be impacted by cultural differences, especially regarding hierarchy, directness, and expressions of accountability (i32, i47). For example, hierarchical cultures may discourage direct eye contact and favor indirect communication, while other cultures expect straightforward interactions (i47). Recognizing distinctions between monochronic (linear, schedule-focused) and polychronic (flexible, relationship-focused) approaches to tasks and time management is essential for preventing miscommunication (i12, i13).

Cultural norms also influence emotional expression, such as shyness in Chinese cultures affecting honest feedback (i23) or humor patterns in hierarchical Eastern societies, where subordinates might reluctantly engage with humor initiated by superiors (i34). Western leaders, conversely, often hesitate using humor due to heightened sensitivity around diversity and inclusion topics (i34).

Needs-focused leadership, emphasizing empathy and intrinsic motivation, becomes increasingly important as project teams diversify globally (i13). This leadership style’s effectiveness depends heavily on cultural openness in communicating expectations and needs (i33). Intrinsic motivations, like self-realization, vary culturally and require adaptive motivational strategies (i32).

Moreover, learned social and cultural behaviors acquired in early childhood strongly affect individuals’ ability to identify and articulate personal needs (i46). Therefore, leaders must patiently facilitate safe spaces for open communication, overcoming cultural barriers such as hierarchical constraints or reticence (i21, i23).

Clear expectation-setting, frequent validation, and synchronization of communication norms are critical in multicultural teams (i8). Explicitly defining shared values, such as fairness and respect, prevents misunderstandings arising from diverse cultural interpretations (i4). Early face-to-face interactions and treating remote teams as equal partners rather than secondary units greatly enhance effective international collaboration (i16).

Effective management of intercultural teams also demands pragmatism alongside sensitivity. Leaders should acknowledge that complete harmonization of cultural needs might be challenging, yet efforts to understand and accommodate differences remain highly valuable (i6). Establishing clear team agreements from the outset creates a solid foundation for managing conflicts constructively (i5).

Explicitly acknowledging cultural diversity helps intercultural teams manage mediation and conflict effectively (i7). Cultural and personal boundaries influence openness in mediation scenarios, emphasizing the importance of culturally sensitive mediation strategies (i21). Mistakes due to cultural misunderstandings should be openly acknowledged by leaders, fostering accountability and reinforcing a respectful environment (i47).

Social and Cultural Anthropology offer valuable insights into intercultural dynamics within projects, often more comprehensively addressing intercultural and conflict-theoretical aspects than psychology alone (i1). Concepts such as the “Moral Economy” from anthropology can deepen understanding of stakeholders’ value-driven orientations, enhancing stakeholder management strategies (i1).

Regional cultural contexts shape leadership effectiveness. For instance, Asian cultures often emphasize role-model leadership, where leaders build trust, thereby encouraging team members to share genuine concerns openly (i23). Conversely, leadership in Central Asia struggles with empathy and emotional intelligence due to autocratic leadership legacies from the Soviet era, underscoring a strong need for empathetic leadership training (i41).

Understanding culture requires recognition of its inherent complexity, encompassing generational, organizational, societal, and individual experiences (i19). Leaders must maintain open-mindedness, curiosity, and understanding without resorting to broad cultural generalizations, thereby respecting individual complexities within diverse groups (i27).

Organizational Culture & Leadership

Organizational culture shapes the feasibility, effectiveness, and adoption of needs-focused leadership within projects. Culture affects how openly needs and conflicts are discussed, influencing stakeholder management, emotional openness, and psychological safety (i5, i6, i13, i20, i42, i46).

Positive team experiences—such as openness to learning, error tolerance, and employee commitment—can embed beneficial attitudes into organizational DNA (i5). Conversely, traditional Western business culture typically separates personal and work lives, neglecting emotional well-being at work (i39). Contextual factors such as corporate culture and time pressure often force leaders to remain superficial, limiting deeper interpersonal engagement despite available tools (i37, i44).

Humor shapes organizational culture, influencing workplace satisfaction and dynamics (i34). However, inappropriate humor involving sensitive topics (e.g., disability, ethnicity, religion, gender) poses substantial risks. Effective humor research must consider individual, group, organizational, and societal layers (i34).

Project sponsors greatly influence the successful implementation of needs-focused leadership, yet many sponsors lack formal training and exhibit insufficient involvement or support for project teams (i16, i36, i41). An estimated 70% of sponsors inadequately support projects, whereas only 30% actively engage with the human needs of project teams (i41). Sponsors often prioritize KPI-driven and technical management approaches, neglecting interpersonal aspects (i36). The commitment of top leadership to interpersonal management is crucial for successful needs-focused approaches, as such methods demand dedicated time and resources—often undervalued in high-pressure corporate environments (i17, i46). Furthermore, high-level executives often lack empathy, a characteristic perceived as beneficial for making tough strategic decisions but detrimental to human-centered leadership (i44).

Project Management Office (PMO) maturity affects the integration and execution of needs-focused leadership. Mature PMOs establish standards derived from retrospectives, promote balanced approaches (technical and human factors), and systematically support emotional intelligence and stakeholder management training (i20, i22, i33, i41). Ideally, a mature PMO includes responsibilities for training in emotional intelligence and needs-focused leadership, yet many PMOs primarily focus on technical competencies (i41). PMOs can also actively coach project sponsors, addressing their maturity and knowledge gaps (i42).

PMOs and senior management typically support needs-focused methods due to their benefits but must strategically time their introduction to avoid peak workloads (i23). Needs-oriented approaches should be explicitly integrated into PMO standards or stage-gate processes to enhance their systematic adoption (i17, i36).

Corporate governance and established organizational structures affect the successful adoption of needs-focused leadership by influencing the behaviors and approaches of project managers (i33). Organizational culture and performance management systems determine whether project managers are seen as strategic leaders or mere administrators (i15, i37).

Companies rarely integrate structured interpersonal leadership approaches into formal guidelines or stage-gate processes, despite evidence suggesting increased project success rates from doing so (i36). Mandating empathy-focused methods into formal frameworks risks procedural superficiality unless genuinely embraced by organizational values (i40).

Attitudes and skills required for effective project management leadership, especially interpersonal competencies, must influence hiring practices and competency development (i33, i38). Advanced organizations increasingly adopt holistic performance reviews (e.g., 360-degree evaluations) rather than focusing solely on technical skills (i49).

Organizations historically prioritize investment in technical training over emotional intelligence or conflict management training, reflecting organizational undervaluation of non-technical skills (i35, i38, i39). Yet, integrating soft skills and emotional leadership training into organizational principles influences project effectiveness and team satisfaction (i38, i39). 

Scandinavian-influenced companies tend to be more progressive in people-focused training practices, resulting in lower turnover and absenteeism, highlighting how regional or cultural backgrounds affect leadership training priorities (i36). Quality of interpersonal training, such as GfK (non-violent communication), may be superior in organizational contexts compared to educational settings, despite superficial applications common in both (i46).

Modern organizational efficiency demands often lead to severe resource constraints (“anorexic” organizations), increasing vulnerability to disruptions and stress (i9). Projects frequently suffer from unrealistic optimism (“Conspiracy of Optimism”), creating persistent stress and perceived underperformance. Norway exemplifies recalibrating project plans to more realistic standards, reducing such negative impacts (i9).

Directive leadership styles (e.g., “locomotive-like” approaches) negatively affect team empowerment, engagement, and effectiveness, highlighting the need for adaptive leadership aligned with the strategic significance of projects (i17). Compromise within teams, while desirable, is counterproductive if it results in misalignment with strategic objectives. Project managers bear the responsibility of aligning team output with organizational goals (i35).

Critical interpersonal decisions relegated to informal social settings create inequality and exclusion within teams, suggesting the need for transparent, inclusive decision-making processes (i37). Western organizational culture often tolerates project managers who lack interpersonal skills, reinforcing perceptions of project management as predominantly technical, a view that urgently requires correction (i35).

Legal and ethical guidelines, such as human rights and anti-discrimination laws, should guide project management to ensure fairness and justice within teams (i27).

Project leaders should adopt a “value-focused mindset,” emphasizing creating value for individuals, the organization, and broader societal impacts (i30). Needs-focused leadership is beneficial at the portfolio management level, relying heavily on interpersonal trust and soft skills (i23).

HR or functional leaders should support project managers in areas exceeding their expertise or appropriate boundaries, emphasizing broader organizational support systems for team welfare (i39).

Change Management & Resistance

Change management increasingly occupies a strategic position in organizations, especially in large, transformational initiatives. Despite its recognized importance, the profession remains relatively immature compared to traditional project management, often affecting its consistent funding and recognition within organizations (i43). Nonetheless, the critical role of effective change management is clear: ensuring the absorption and exploitation of project outcomes, fostering genuine behavioral changes rather than superficial compliance, and addressing both emotional and technical responses to change (i19, i42, i43).

Resistance to organizational change typically stems from perceived threats to personal values, identities, and established routines (i4). Teams with longstanding histories and unresolved past conflicts often display intensified resistance, as personal grievances compound underlying emotional tensions (i4). Therefore, effective transformation leadership demands a deeper understanding of behavioral psychology, going beyond simplistic explanations that “people naturally resist change” (i35).

Successful change management distinguishes between emotional and technical resistance. Emotional resistance requires listening, empathy, and creating opportunities for genuine expression, effectively “unfreezing” team members by openly addressing concerns (i4, i43). Technical resistance, however, benefits from clarification, structured negotiation, and clear, non-participatory communication when changes are non-negotiable, thereby setting realistic expectations without falsely implying shared decision-making (i10, i43).

A needs-based approach has emerged as highly beneficial in managing organizational change. It helps uncover the root causes of resistance by empathetically addressing the underlying needs and individual concerns of stakeholders (i13). Addressing these individual needs explicitly is crucial, as it enables team members to comprehend and accept personal sacrifices for broader organizational benefits (i8). Moreover, systematically identifying and addressing stakeholders’ needs can enhance structure and predictability, distinguishing change management from more fluid approaches such as conflict resolution (i5).

Effective change management incorporates clear communication, empathy, proactive engagement, and elements resembling conflict management techniques, emphasizing the importance of understanding individual needs (i6). It also involves openly addressing scenarios where stakeholders might experience losses or disadvantages, thereby fostering transparency and trust (i32).

Introducing empathy-building methodologies such as Design Thinking or Agile practices incrementally can help skeptical leaders recognize the relational and performance benefits of emotionally intelligent leadership (i37, i39). Such structured methodologies normalize emotional intelligence within organizational culture without overwhelming resistant employees, thereby creating but manageable cultural shifts (i39).

Successful implementation of emotional checkpoints—moments designated to assess emotional alignment and address emerging concerns—further emphasizes the desirability, rather than mere acceptability, of emotional intelligence within organizational culture (i39). This normalization helps reduce resistance by gradually embedding emotionally intelligent practices into project management and regular business operations (i39).

The increasing specialization and strategic significance of organizational change managers (OCMs) reflect a growing need to address complex interpersonal dimensions of large-scale transformations (i17). Effective transformation leadership thus requires dedicated OCMs who specialize in motivation and conflict mediation, tasks typically distinct from the deliverable-focused responsibilities of program managers (i17).

Change management ideally occupies a strategic interface between the business and projects, systematically addressing stakeholder needs (i43). This strategic positioning facilitates the selection of internal champions, addresses fears among resistant employees, and manages the cultural pace of organizational adaptation (i39).

For organizational coherence, needs-oriented project management should transcend project teams and permeate daily business operations, preventing motivational discrepancies between project-specific contexts and routine activities (i25). Ensuring that needs-focused practices within project teams flow back into regular team environments maintains motivational consistency and organizational alignment (i25).

A critical aspect of successful organizational change is measuring outcomes rather than merely outputs, ensuring genuine behavioral change rather than superficial compliance (i42). Clearly defined yet flexible mandates, openly acknowledging both known and unknown elements, contribute to successful change initiatives (i3). Conversely, failed cultural change efforts often resulted from lacking incentives for stakeholders or from inadequate relational influence among mid-level managers (i3).

Artificial Intelligence in Needs-Focused Leadership

Enhancing Emotional Intelligence Through AI

Project management increasingly leverages AI to strengthen leaders’ emotional intelligence (EQ) and responsiveness. AI dynamically predicts stakeholder engagement, adjusting communications in real-time to streamline stakeholder management processes (i48). Using Natural Language Processing (NLP), AI proactively detects emotional, motivational, or conflict-related issues within internal communications, providing early warnings that enable managers to intervene proactively (i48).

AI tools also serve as emotional co-pilots, interpreting subtle emotional cues or distress signs from team members—including neurodivergent individuals—and suggesting empathetic responses (i26, i33, i51). This capability is especially beneficial in digital interactions, where emotional nuances might be overlooked by humans alone (i50).

AI-driven hyper-personalization advances individualized motivational strategies. By analyzing personalized data, AI can provide tailored motivational support, similar to a skilled human coach who deeply understands individual motivational triggers (i11, i50). This personalization surpasses generic HR emotional intelligence training, particularly benefiting project managers with specialized or unconventional professional backgrounds (i48).

An innovative example highlighted by interviewees involves integrating personal needs profiles into AI systems, combining conversational AI with personalized analysis to enhance self-awareness and emotional intelligence (i50). Such customized coaching supports ongoing personal development, helping leaders identify strengths, weaknesses, behavioral patterns, and actionable growth strategies (i50).

AI offers potential as a neutral and unbiased mediator in conflicts, reducing biases typically associated with human mediators, provided transparency and explainability are maintained (i11, i51). NLP techniques uncover hidden conflicts by analyzing team communications digitally, enabling early detection and resolution before issues escalate (i48). However, interviewees caution that AI’s capabilities in handling deep emotional or biographical conflicts are limited, making AI supplementary rather than primary in mediation contexts (i11, i49).

AI reduces manual effort involved in routine project management activities, freeing managers to invest more deeply in quality human interactions and strategic considerations (i48). AI-driven simulations enhance emotional intelligence and skill development through realistic, tailored scenarios that help leaders gain deeper insights into emotional dynamics and conflict management (i48).

Leaders can utilize AI effectively as a “sounding board,” generating ideas, practicing role-playing scenarios, and identifying blind spots or biases in decision-making (i51). AI-driven scenario-based prompting further aids leaders in assessing team members’ suitability and agility mindset before project assignments, thus optimizing team formation decisions (i48).

AI also demonstrates educational value, effectively adapting social-oriented case studies into discipline-specific teaching materials, thus enhancing personalized learning and professional development (i45). Reflective AI prompts can help leaders better understand personal and team strengths, weaknesses, and patterns, demonstrating AI’s potential in synthesizing extensive human experiences into actionable insights (i50).

Despite these advantages, AI currently does not enhance meeting efficiency, as brainstorming and direct human interactions remain irreplaceable (i48). Additionally, despite potential biases in AI algorithms, the technology greatly accelerates tasks and provides valuable insights into cultural differences within teams, thereby improving productivity compared to traditional methods (i48).

Goal-driven AI, involving reinforcement learning and iterative problem-solving, was noted as less suited for empathy-based leadership compared to hyper-personalization approaches (i50). Furthermore, sentiment analysis and recognition AI technologies, although capable of detecting emotions through visual and biometric data, still require human contextual interpretation to derive accurate meanings in leadership scenarios (i50, i51).

Interviewees repeatedly emphasize that AI cannot replicate uniquely human aspects such as empathy, creativity, relational validation, and ethical judgment (i19, i30, i46). AI’s role remains supplementary, enhancing but never replacing human emotional judgment, creativity, and ethical decision-making (i19, i30). Consequently, experts suggest an optimal AI-human balance of approximately 60% human judgment and 40% AI support to maintain people-centric leadership (i48).

Effective AI integration into leadership practices requires a strategic vision—referred to as “AI-Vision”—that emphasizes the thoughtful, human-centric embedding of AI beyond mere technical proficiency (i25). Leaders need to embrace AI proactively to maintain credibility, as team members may increasingly expect its use (i51). Managers who fail to integrate AI risk becoming disadvantaged due to inefficiency and reduced responsiveness in comparison to their peers who use AI effectively (i48).

Future advancements in AI may also provide ways to better quantify and measure the effectiveness of needs-focused management and training, further substantiating its practical benefits (i10). Generative AI, especially large language models (LLMs), is becoming commonplace not just for productivity but to enhance self-reflection, self-awareness, and deeper human connections (i50). AI’s evolution thus promises more meaningful conversations, alignment, and societal improvements when strategically combined with human-centered leadership practices (i30).

Ethical, Psychological, and Societal Considerations of AI Use

The potential for AI to simulate empathy has garnered attention. AI models, such as GPT-4, reportedly demonstrate moderate levels of empathy (60-70%) contingent on training data quality (i48). Experts agree that empathy can indeed be operationalized into observable behaviors, allowing AI to convincingly simulate empathetic interactions (i11, i45). For instance, practical cases already exist in which chatbots engage in human-like emotional conversations, indicating notable progress in empathic simulation (i45).

Yet, fundamental philosophical debates persist. While simulated empathy can fulfill utilitarian therapeutic purposes, questions remain regarding authenticity and phenomenological validity. Critics argue that AI does not genuinely empathize but rather predicts empathetic responses based on human-generated data patterns found in online sources (i11, i50). Thus, even highly convincing empathy simulations lack genuine emotional experience or holistic phenomenological awareness (i11).

Despite the promise of empathy-augmenting AI, experts caution against psychological dependencies on AI, particularly in leadership contexts. The use of EQ-enhancing AI tools may inadvertently lead leaders to neglect the development of their own emotional intelligence (i50). Such dependency risks are exacerbated by AI’s current shortcomings in advanced reasoning, wisdom, and contextual decision-making, illustrated by the DIKUW (Data, Information, Knowledge, Understanding, Wisdom) pyramid framework (i50). AI currently struggles particularly with reasoning and wisdom, critical for advanced contextual understanding (i50).

Further risks associated with AI in leadership roles include potential inaccuracies—often termed “hallucinations”—and the inability of AI to perceive unsaid or unwritten contextual nuances, underscoring the necessity of careful human oversight (i50).

Moreover, some individuals increasingly rely on AI for personal psychological support, transitioning from traditional psychological therapy to AI-driven interactions for emotional self-reflection and psychoanalysis, highlighting both effectiveness and affordability (i41, i50). However, the long-term implications of such dependencies remain largely uncharted territory, underscoring the necessity of rigorous longitudinal research on psychological outcomes (i11).

Ethical dilemmas inherent to AI use, especially regarding bias, privacy, and consent, represent crucial challenges. AI training datasets frequently embed biases, particularly related to race, culture, and gender, affecting AI’s performance in culturally diverse environments (i48, i51). It is estimated that every second AI model displays gender bias, and every fourth demonstrates racial bias (i51). Biases, however, are also inherent in human evolutionary psychology, emphasizing the critical need for AI literacy and continuous awareness training to mitigate these embedded biases (i51).

When used in leadership or team management, AI’s inability to accurately interpret nuanced intercultural or interpersonal dynamics may perpetuate stereotypes and foster misunderstanding (i11, i47, i48). Project managers and leaders employing AI in culturally diverse teams should meticulously review AI-generated suggestions to mitigate bias risks (i48).

Additionally, privacy concerns arise when deploying AI to identify emotional tensions or internal team dynamics through methods such as speech analysis. Technological capability does not guarantee user acceptance, especially given sensitive ethical issues surrounding informed consent and personal privacy (i11).
The psychological impacts of AI also extend into the realm of human-machine relationships. Humans frequently anthropomorphize technology, developing emotional bonds with machines, such as landmine-detecting robots or even cars, illustrating a deepening emotional engagement that may alter human interaction dynamics (i51). Such emotional projection onto machines is expected to grow substantially.

Individual interactions within teams could also shift notably due to AI usage. If a team member actively uses AI, this can challenge or augment their interactions with leadership, reshaping team dynamics and potentially creating new forms of friction or opportunities for collaboration (i51).

Further, generational differences influence perceptions of AI use. Younger generations accustomed to technology-integrated environments might perceive leaders who abstain from using AI as outdated, creating friction within teams and organizations (i51). Additionally, democratization of knowledge facilitated by AI may inadvertently cause internal friction as teams become equally knowledgeable, challenging existing hierarchies (i51).

AI’s deployment can lead to divergent expectations depending on professional roles and contexts. For example, in a hospital scenario, nurses and physicians expressed notably different expectations from a predictive AI system designed for managing patient delirium. Nurses hoped the AI would empower them in hierarchical conflicts with physicians, whereas physicians primarily sought workload reduction (i11). Such differences highlight the complexity and necessity of context-sensitive AI integration strategies.

From a broader societal viewpoint, AI will substantially affect psychological well-being and societal communication (i51). Optimistically, technological advancement could cyclically enhance empathy, trust, and societal integration (i30). However, long-term societal impacts of pervasive AI use remain uncertain, demanding careful evaluation, especially considering negative psychological impacts previously seen in technology adoption cycles (e.g., smartphones, social media) (i11).

Experts warn against adopting “technodeterminism” or “technopositivism,” advocating comprehensive humanistic and ethical frameworks to guide AI development (“humanize technology before it dehumanizes us”) (i11). Adaptive governance and continuous regulation (“society in the loop”) are essential to proactively address unforeseen negative impacts, emphasizing responsible open innovation grounded in human rights and flourishing (i11).

Additionally, recognizing the distinct applications and data requirements of various AI technologies, e.g., the “seven patterns” of AI—Autonomous, Conversational (LLMs), Pattern and Anomaly Detection, Recognition, Predictive Analytics, Hyper-Personalization, and Goal-Driven systems—provides a structured understanding crucial for informed governance and effective implementation strategies (i50).

The integration of AI in sensitive domains like motivation, conflict resolution, and self-reflection requires careful, responsible approaches. AI should function primarily as a supportive tool (“co-pilot”) rather than as the primary mechanism for managing people, particularly considering the risk of misinterpreting subtle emotional or linguistic cues (i47).

Experts emphasize preserving core human relational interactions, cautioning against delegating critical relationship-building responsibilities to AI, due to the risk of AI becoming counterproductive distractions from genuine emotional development and self-regulation (i11, i18). Human judgment, intuition, and culturally sensitive communication remain irreplaceable, despite the benefits of AI-assisted information gathering on team dynamics (i47).

Rigorous empirical research, intentional training in emotional self-awareness, mindfulness, and introspection, and psychological resilience are recommended to ensure psychologically healthy individuals capable of effectively navigating an increasingly AI-driven environment (i11, i51). Psychological health is essential to adapt successfully to the rapidly accelerating pace of technological advancements (i51).
Socio-Technical Integration and the Future of Human-Robot Collaboration
Interviewees foresee a collaborative future, where humans and AI-enabled robots function as co-intelligent team members, extending even into managerial roles (i45). In this scenario, AI primarily augments managerial tasks by offering decision-making support. Through its capability to analyze extensive data sets and communicate insights via storytelling, AI becomes an indispensable aid for human decision-makers, who retain ultimate ethical responsibility for decisions (i45).

The integration of intelligent systems, likened metaphorically to “having a genius mathematician on every team,” will profoundly impact all projects. No initiative is expected to remain unaffected by such potent analytical capacities (i51). Moreover, as technological developments in AI and robotics advance exponentially and converge, their effects become rapid, cascading, and transformative across sectors (i51).

As AI technologies evolve, personalized AI assistants will become commonplace, increasingly tailored through advanced memory features and expanded contextual understanding. Such personalized assistance aims to provide individually optimized support, making interactions more intuitive and effective (i51).

However, this rapid technological evolution implies continuous learning and adaptation. Professionals may soon face a reality of reskilling every six months, driven by the accelerating pace of technological change (i51).

Effective socio-technical integration emphasizes cooperative alignment between human teams and robotic systems. Implementing robotics should follow participative, bottom-up approaches (“co-creation”), ensuring genuine resonance with team requirements, rather than adopting imposed top-down solutions (i11). This ensures technology serves authentic team needs and mitigates resistance due to mismatches in expectations and experiences.

I45 emphasizes that successful integration requires awareness and consideration of varied perspectives within teams, including engineers and frontline construction workers, each group having distinct experiences and expectations concerning robot-based solutions (i45). Consequently, guiding teams through the socio-technical integration process involves carefully balancing these human elements alongside technical capabilities, underlining the importance of cooperation between humans and robots (i45).

While robots traditionally handle repetitive tasks, enabling humans to focus more effectively on complex decision-making, future developments could expand their role in empathetic contexts. Robots might feasibly operate according to structured, process-oriented models to support empathetic tasks, including needs identification and analysis. Nonetheless, human involvement remains critical for defining nuanced strategies, reflecting the irreplaceable nature of human judgment in complex interpersonal domains (i45).

Moreover, robots can be programmed with specific incentives and disincentives to cultivate behaviors aligning with empathy and trustworthiness. Trust emerges as a foundational principle, essential for successful human-robot interactions, underscoring mutual trust between both intelligent robots and their human counterparts as a core goal in future collaboration (i45).

As capabilities advance, robots may eventually exercise decision-making autonomy in increasingly complex scenarios, potentially surpassing traditional ethical frameworks such as Asimov’s laws of robotics*. This evolution necessitates careful ethical and practical reconsideration of robot autonomy, balancing operational efficiency and safety with human oversight and moral responsibility (i45).
