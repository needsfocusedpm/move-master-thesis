# 12 Side Findings and Reflections

Source type: Master’s Thesis  
Author: Daniel Hendling

Perception vs. Practice in Needs-Focused Leadership

While some project managers consider themselves to be “empathetic” or “needs-oriented,” the question remains to what extent such self-perception translates into effective action. The author has previously outlined the importance of a practical, outcome-driven approach—emphasizing disciplined action over merely adopting an empathetic attitude. The quantitative data suggest a potential discrepancy in perception: for example, project managers tend to rate the importance of “attentive and empathetic listening” lower than team members do. This could indicate a gap between how project managers see themselves and how their behavior is perceived by others. Although this issue merits further investigation, it lies beyond the scope of this thesis.

Cultural Influences on Leadership Perception

After the survey had been launched, a follow-up conversation with one of the earlier interviewees sparked the idea of identifying specific cultural groups within the dataset, depending on the final distribution of respondents. Upon completion of the survey, two cultural clusters were defined for exploratory analysis: selected English-speaking countries (USA, Canada, UK) and selected German-speaking countries (Germany, Austria).

Preliminary results indicate notable differences in the perceived effectiveness of needs-focused leadership between these clusters. While this suggests that cultural factors—potentially shaped by language, norms, and self-perception—may influence leadership perception and practice, a more nuanced, differentiated analysis is required to validate these initial observations. Investigating these cultural dynamics further could offer valuable insights but lies beyond the scope of this thesis.


Executive Personality, Empathy, and Project Leadership

Recent empirical studies indicate that ‘dark personality’ traits—particularly Narcissism, Machiavellianism, and Psychopathy—are significantly more prevalent among senior executives and company heads than in the general population or lower management tiers. (Diller, 2021) Up to one in five business leaders may exhibit psychopathic tendencies (Chamorro-Premuzic, 2019). Such “corporate psychopathy” is not just an ethical issue because of the suffering it creates—it also potentially costs businesses billions of dollars annually. (Croom, 2021)

Project Sponsors often come from an executive level, as well as do influential stakeholders shaping the organizational project management culture. This leads to the question, to which extent such a type of personality may negatively influence the application and its effectiveness when it comes to applying a needs-focused leadership approach in projects. While this topic goes beyond the scope of this thesis, the question how these elements might be related deserves further research and analysis.

Systemic Perspectives on Needs-Focused Leadership

This thesis has repeatedly highlighted the interrelations and reciprocal effects between project leadership, corporate governance, and organizational culture. In other words, there are dynamic interactions between the individuals involved, the organizational context in which they act, and the broader societal and environmental frameworks that shape this context. These connections extend to larger concepts such as the Moral Economy, Economy for the Common Good, the UN Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), the UN Global Compact, ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance), CSR (Corporate Social Responsibility), and B Corporations.

Needs-focused leadership, in particular, aligns closely with ESG principles—especially the “Social” dimension—by promoting mental health, employee well-being, and engagement. From this perspective, leadership that focuses on both individual and organizational needs can also contribute to broader societal goals. The concept of “value” in leadership should therefore be understood not only in terms of performance and outcomes, but also in terms of its impact on people and society at large.

Exploring needs-focused leadership from such a large-scale systemic perspective could yield valuable insights; however, this level of analysis lies well beyond the scope of the present work.
